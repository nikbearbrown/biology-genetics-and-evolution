**TL;DR.** Chapter 10 ended with the five forces of evolution operating inside one interbreeding population. Run those same forces in two populations that have stopped exchanging genes and you get divergence; run them long enough and the divergence is large enough that the populations can no longer interbreed even if reunited; the populations have become separate species. This chapter walks the mechanisms. It pins down what reproductive isolation actually is at the level of mating, fertilization, development, and meiosis — the prezygotic and postzygotic barriers that cut gene flow. It distinguishes allopatric speciation (geographic isolation does the heavy lifting) from sympatric speciation (reproductive isolation evolves without geographic separation), works through the canonical sympatric cases in plants (polyploidy is instant speciation) and animals (host-shift in Rhagoletis fruit flies, sensory drive in African Great Lake cichlids), and shows why adaptive radiations — the Galápagos finches, Hawaiian silverswords, Australian marsupials, possibly the Cambrian explosion itself — are the same allopatric-or-sympatric machinery cranking through ecological opportunity in parallel across many niches at once. The worked example traces a single speciation event in Galápagos finches end to end: ancestral *Geospiza* colonizing, beak depth under disruptive selection, assortative mating via beak-shape-coupled song, *BMP4* and *ALX1* allele frequencies diverging, hybrid intermediates selected against. By the end you should be able to classify any described isolating barrier, predict whether two populations are heading toward speciation, fusion, or stable polymorphism from divergence and gene-flow data, and explain at the level of meiotic mechanics why a tetraploid plant cannot make viable offspring with its diploid parent.

## A lake that almost dried up

Lake Victoria, in East Africa, is the second-largest freshwater lake on Earth by surface area — about 68,800 km², spanning the borders of Uganda, Kenya, and Tanzania, sitting at an elevation of 1,134 m on the African plateau. The lake holds about 500 species of cichlid fish, all of them haplochromine cichlids of the *Haplochromis* group plus close relatives, all of them closely enough related that mitochondrial DNA sequence differences between species are typically less than 1%, all of them morphologically and behaviorally distinct enough that a field biologist with a snorkel and a fish guide can tell them apart by eye ([Seehausen 2006, *Proc R Soc B* 273:1987–1998](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.2006.3539); review of Lake Victoria cichlid radiation).

Here is the puzzle. Lake Victoria, as a lake, is young. Sediment cores and lake-level reconstructions ([Johnson et al. 1996, *Science* 273:1091–1093](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.273.5278.1091); the seminal core paper) show that the lake nearly desiccated about 14,600 years ago — sometime during the Late Pleistocene, the lake basin held essentially no water, just shallow swamps and isolated pools. The current lake refilled when the African monsoon strengthened after the last glacial maximum. *The entire body of water that now exists is younger than the oldest pyramid at Giza.*

So the 500 cichlid species in Lake Victoria have evolved in approximately 100,000 to 200,000 years if you take a generous reading of the basin history, or as little as 14,600 years if you take the desiccation as a hard reset. Either way: this is the fastest known vertebrate adaptive radiation. By comparison, Galápagos finches — themselves famous as a fast radiation — produced 18 species in about 2 to 3 million years, ten to twenty times slower per species. Hawaiian honeycreepers produced about 50 species in 5 million years. Lake Victoria did more diversification in less time than any other vertebrate group on the books.

The cichlids did not just diversify in numbers; they diversified in form and ecology. Some are algae scrapers with chisel-shaped teeth pressed against rocks. Some are scale-eaters — *Perissodus* and relatives — that sneak up on other fish and bite scales off their flanks (and develop asymmetric mouth shapes, left-handed and right-handed, to optimize attack angle). Some are paedophages — they eat the eggs and larvae of other cichlids, in some cases ramming the brooding parent's mouth to force regurgitation. Some are pelagic plankton-feeders, hovering in open water. Some are crab-eaters with molarized teeth. Some are pure piscivores. Lake Victoria contains essentially every cichlid trophic specialization that exists across the African Great Lakes, packed into the new lake in a hundred thousand years.

And here is the second puzzle, which is the one that actually gets us into the mechanism. Most of the species are not separated by geography. They live in the same lake, often in the same rocky reefs, often in the same few square meters of substrate. The classical population-genetics intuition from Chapter 10 — that speciation requires geographic isolation to cut gene flow — does not obviously work here. Two species of *Pundamilia* (a Lake Victoria genus) can be sympatric within meters, mating preferentially with their own kind, hybridizing only rarely, and yet maintaining genetic distinctness over generations. What is keeping them apart?

The answer, worked out in the 1990s and 2000s by Ole Seehausen and colleagues at the University of Bern and the Eawag aquatic research institute, is *sensory drive*. The water in Lake Victoria varies in clarity and color with depth: shallow water transmits the full visible spectrum, deeper water filters out blue and transmits redshifted wavelengths. Cichlids that live at different depths see the world through different spectral windows. Their visual pigments — the opsin proteins in the photoreceptors of their retinas — have evolved tunings matched to their local light. *Pundamilia pundamilia* lives in shallower water, sees blue light efficiently, has bluer body coloration in males, and mates with females that discriminate by preferring blue males. *Pundamilia nyererei* lives in deeper water, has redshifted opsin tunings, has redder males, and has females that prefer red ([Seehausen et al. 2008, *Nature* 455:620–626](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature07285); the sensory drive paper).

When the lake gets turbid — as it has from agricultural runoff and eutrophication since the 1980s — both species' females can no longer discriminate by color. The visual signal that was keeping them apart is no longer transmitted reliably. The species fuse. Hybrids appear, the genetic distinctness erodes, and what had been two species becomes one ([Seehausen et al. 1997, *Science* 277:1808–1811](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.277.5333.1808); the eutrophication-driven hybridization paper). Speciation in Lake Victoria turns out to be reversible, in the lifetime of a single environmental perturbation.

I want you to hold this case for the whole chapter, because it does three things at once. It shows speciation that is *fast* — a hundred thousand years, possibly less. It shows speciation that is *sympatric* — no geographic barrier required. And it shows speciation that is *fragile* — kept up by a behavioral discrimination mechanism that depends on a particular sensory environment. When we get to the synthesis I want you to be able to recognize each of these moves elsewhere — in Galápagos finch beak-coupled song, in *Rhagoletis* apple flies discriminating hosts by smell, in Hawaiian silversword pollinator differences. The same machinery, in different sensory currencies.

Now back up. Before we can ask how speciation happens, we need to ask what "species" even means, what reproductive isolation is mechanically, and how the various flavors of speciation cash out in terms of the five forces from Chapter 10. We will earn the cichlid radiation as the closing case.

## What is a species? (Specification before argument)

Chapter 09 named the biological species concept and listed its four major failures. I want to pin both down properly here because most of what follows depends on having a precise enough definition that we can ask "are these two populations separate species?" and get a meaningful answer.

The **biological species concept** (BSC), formulated by Ernst Mayr in 1942 in *Systematics and the Origin of Species* and refined many times since: *a species is a group of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations that is reproductively isolated from other such groups*. The definition does two things. It packs together a positive condition (members of the species can interbreed and produce fertile offspring) and a negative condition (they cannot, or do not, interbreed with members of other species in nature). And it grounds species identity in the *flow of genes*, not in morphology, behavior, ecology, or DNA per se. Two populations are the same species if their gene pools mix; they are different species if their gene pools are isolated.

This is doing real work, and it is worth seeing what work it is doing. Under the BSC, "species" is not primarily a name for a *kind* — a category that organisms belong to by satisfying some checklist of features. It is a name for a *relationship* — a state of gene flow between populations. Two populations are conspecifics if their genes are pooling; they are heterospecifics if they aren't. This is what makes the BSC mechanistically useful for evolution: speciation is the process of gene flow ceasing, and "species" is the label we apply once the cessation is complete enough.

The BSC fails — and fails predictably — at four classes of organism.

**One. Asexual organisms.** Bacteria reproduce by binary fission. There is no interbreeding to test. A single *E. coli* cell divides into two clonal daughters; those daughters can exchange genes via horizontal transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction), but the exchange is not reproduction in the BSC sense. You cannot ask whether two strains of *E. coli* "would interbreed if given the chance" because the question does not parse. Microbiologists use other species concepts — average nucleotide identity (ANI) cutoffs around 95% for species, ecological niche differences, phylogenetic clustering — that work for clonal organisms but lose the gene-flow grounding of the BSC.

**Two. Fossil species.** A fossil cannot be tested for interbreeding with anything. Paleontologists work from morphology — they call two specimens the same species if their skeletal features match within the range of variation seen in living species, and different species if they don't. This is a **morphological species concept**, and it is the only option for extinct lineages. It also fails informatively: when two paleontological species turn out, on DNA evidence from preserved samples, to have interbred — as Neanderthals and modern humans did, contributing about 2% of the genome to non-African modern humans ([Sankararaman et al. 2014, *Nature* 507:354–357](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature12961); admixture quantification — Chapter 14 deep-dive) — the morphological boundary turns out to have been a leakier gene-flow boundary than the bones suggested.

**Three. Hybridizing pairs.** Many pairs of recognized species in nature interbreed where their ranges overlap, sometimes producing fertile offspring. Coyotes and gray wolves hybridize. Coyotes and red wolves hybridize extensively enough that the red wolf as a distinct species is under serious challenge ([vonHoldt et al. 2016, *Sci Adv* 2:e1501714](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.1501714)). Polar bears and grizzly bears hybridize occasionally in the wild; the offspring ("grolar bears") are fertile and have been documented in F2 backcrosses. The BSC's clean dichotomy — interbreeding or not — collapses into a continuum: low rates of gene flow, moderate rates of gene flow, full pooling. The species boundary in such cases is *partial* rather than absolute, and the BSC has to be applied with explicit specification of *how much* gene flow disqualifies sameness.

**Four. Ring species.** The clearest case is the *Ensatina* salamanders of California ([Wake 1997, *PNAS* 94:7761–7767](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.94.15.7761); the canonical ring-species paper, with caveats about whether it remains a textbook ring species under newer phylogeographic data). A ring of populations encircles California's Central Valley — populations along the coastal mountains on the west and populations along the Sierra Nevada on the east, connected by populations in the north and (in some readings) the south. Adjacent populations interbreed. But where the two ends of the ring meet at the southern terminus, the populations have diverged enough that they do not interbreed. So is there one species (because every adjacent pair can mix) or two (because the end-of-ring pair cannot)? The BSC, applied naively, gives both answers. The answer the BSC actually requires you to take seriously is that "species" is a category for which boundaries are not always crisp, and a chain of populations can carry the speciation process *part way* — far enough that the ends are isolated, not far enough that any single pair along the way is.

I want to be direct about what these four failures show. They are not arguments against the BSC. They are arguments against treating "species" as a *Platonic* category — as if there were a real metaphysical kind out there that an organism either belongs to or doesn't. The BSC tells you a useful thing: when two populations are exchanging genes freely, they are evolving as one unit, and when they are not, they are evolving as two. That is the biology. The label "species" is a discrete tag we paste onto a continuous process. The label is useful most of the time and breaks at the edges. The biology — the gene-flow status — is what is real.

Throughout this chapter I will use BSC language because it is the most precise grounding for the speciation process. When I need to invoke morphological species (paleontology), phylogenetic species (smallest monophyletic group on a DNA-tree analysis), or ecological species (populations exploiting a distinct niche), I will name the switch explicitly. The default is BSC.

## Reproductive isolation — the mechanism, in two halves

If speciation is the process of gene flow ceasing, then *reproductive isolation* is the substrate. Two populations are reproductively isolated when individuals from population A and population B cannot produce viable, fertile offspring together — or, equivalently, when the per-generation gene flow rate *m* between A and B has dropped to approximately zero.

The barriers that cause this come in two flavors, distinguished by *when* they operate relative to the formation of a zygote (a fertilized egg).

**Prezygotic barriers** prevent fertilization from happening in the first place. No zygote is produced. The two parents may not meet, may not mate even if they meet, may attempt to mate but fail mechanically, or may copulate but produce gametes that cannot fertilize each other.

**Postzygotic barriers** operate after fertilization. The zygote is produced, but it either fails to develop, develops into an organism that survives but cannot reproduce, or develops into an organism that itself reproduces normally but whose F2 offspring suffer collapse.

This isn't just taxonomy. The two flavors of barrier accumulate at different rates over evolutionary time, respond to different selection pressures, and have different mechanistic consequences for how speciation proceeds. The classical result, from Coyne and Orr's careful comparative work on *Drosophila* speciation ([Coyne & Orr 1989, *Evolution* 43:362–381](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1558-5646.1989.tb04233.x); [Coyne & Orr 2004, *Speciation*, Sinauer Associates](https://www.amazon.com/Speciation-Jerry-Coyne/dp/0878930892); the textbook): in *Drosophila*, prezygotic barriers tend to evolve faster between sympatric species pairs than between allopatric pairs — a signature of reinforcement (selection favoring discrimination when hybridization is costly). Postzygotic barriers accumulate roughly linearly with time since divergence in both cases. We will come back to this when we get to Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities.

### Prezygotic barriers — five flavors

**Habitat isolation.** Two species live in the same general area but occupy different microhabitats and rarely encounter each other. Two species of garter snake in California — *Thamnophis sirtalis* (terrestrial) and *Thamnophis atratus* (aquatic) — can be found within meters of each other, but one hunts on land for amphibians and small mammals and the other hunts in streams for fish and tadpoles; their reproductive encounters are vanishingly rare ([de Queiroz & Lawson 1994, *Biol J Linn Soc* 53:209–229](https://academic.oup.com/biolinnean/article-abstract/53/3/209/2645824)). Habitat isolation is not absolute — the snakes can interbreed in captivity — but in the wild it suffices.

**Temporal isolation.** Two species occupy the same habitat but breed at different times. The 13-year and 17-year cicada broods (*Magicicada* species) emerge in different years and never overlap; even where their geographic ranges intersect, the temporal offset means a 13-year brood adult never meets a 17-year brood adult during the brief reproductive window ([Cooley et al. 2018, *PLoS One* 13:e0204385](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0204385)). Two species of orchids may flower a month apart; their pollen never meets the other's stigma. Some North Pacific salmon populations that share rivers are temporally isolated by spawning at different times of year — chinook spring run, coho fall run, etc. — even when they share habitat the rest of the time.

**Behavioral isolation.** Two species can mate only if courtship signals match, and the signals are species-specific. *Anolis* lizards in the Caribbean use throat-fans called *dewlaps* — bright skin flaps extended in stereotyped patterns — to identify conspecifics; closely related *Anolis* species on the same island have dewlaps of distinctly different colors and pulsing patterns ([Losos 2009, *Lizards in an Evolutionary Tree*, University of California Press](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520255913/lizards-in-an-evolutionary-tree); the Caribbean *Anolis* radiation book). Firefly males of different species flash species-specific light patterns; a female of one species ignores the flash of another. Bird song is the canonical example: closely related songbird species use distinct songs, learned in juveniles, and females discriminate.

**Mechanical isolation.** Genitalia do not fit. In many beetles — especially carabid ground beetles and chrysomelid leaf beetles — male genital structures are exquisitely species-specific in shape, including lock-and-key fits with female reproductive tracts ([Eberhard 1985, *Sexual Selection and Animal Genitalia*, Harvard University Press](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674802919); the foundational book). Two beetles of different species attempting to copulate fail mechanically; no sperm transfer occurs. In plants, mechanical isolation operates via pollinator specificity — flowers shaped to attract one pollinator species exclude others, so pollen from species A never lands on species B.

**Gametic isolation.** Sperm and egg meet but cannot fertilize. The cleanest example is in sea urchins, which broadcast-spawn into open water — males release sperm, females release eggs, fertilization happens externally in seawater. Different species' eggs carry surface proteins (the binding protein **bindin** in *Strongylocentrotus*) that recognize species-specific sperm proteins; bindin from species A binds sperm from species A but rejects sperm from species B ([Palumbi & Metz 1991, *Mol Biol Evol* 8:227–239](https://academic.oup.com/mbe/article/8/2/227/1037571); [Vacquier 1998, *Science* 281:1995–1998](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.281.5385.1995)). The molecular recognition is the barrier. Two species can be spawning into the same cubic meter of water and produce no hybrid offspring because the proteins won't bind.

The unifying principle across prezygotic mechanisms: *they prevent the formation of a zygote*. Either the parents don't meet, don't mate, can't copulate, or can't fertilize. No hybrid zygote ever exists. No energy is wasted on a doomed embryo. From a selective standpoint, prezygotic barriers are *cheap* — they save the reproductive cost of attempting an unproductive cross.

### Postzygotic barriers — three flavors

**Hybrid inviability.** The zygote forms but the embryo fails to develop, or develops into an organism that dies before reaching reproductive age. Crosses between certain frog species (*Rana* species pairs) produce zygotes that begin development normally but arrest at gastrulation, when maternal-effect transcripts hand off to zygotic transcription — the paternal genome's transcripts don't integrate properly into the maternal cellular context, and development collapses. Crosses between many *Drosophila* species pairs produce inviable eggs or first-instar larvae that fail to molt.

**Hybrid sterility.** The hybrid survives, even thrives, but cannot reproduce. The mule is the canonical example. A horse (*Equus ferus caballus*, 64 chromosomes) crossed with a donkey (*Equus africanus asinus*, 62 chromosomes) produces a mule (63 chromosomes). The mule is viable, longer-lived than either parent, and historically valued for sure-footedness and load-carrying capacity. But during meiosis, the 63 chromosomes cannot pair into 31 or 32 homologous pairs — there are 31 horse chromosomes from mom, 31 donkey chromosomes from dad, plus one unmatched chromosome that has no homolog. Meiosis aborts. Gametes either don't form or form with random unbalanced chromosome complements that produce inviable zygotes if they did fertilize. The mule is a sterile dead end ([Chandley et al. 1974, *Cytogenet Cell Genet* 13:330–344](https://karger.com/cgr/article/13/4/330/72447); the cytogenetics of mule meiotic failure). I will work through the chromosome arithmetic explicitly in §"Polyploidy" below, because the same logic governs why polyploid speciation works in plants.

**Hybrid breakdown.** The F1 hybrid is viable and fertile, but F2 offspring (offspring of two F1 hybrids, or of an F1 backcrossed) suffer reduced viability or fertility. This is the signature of Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities — pairs of mutations that work fine in their original genetic backgrounds but produce dysfunction when shuffled into novel combinations in F2 individuals where Mendelian segregation has randomized which alleles came from which parental species. *Drosophila* crosses provide the classical evidence ([Dobzhansky 1936, *Genetics* 21:113–135](https://www.genetics.org/content/21/2/113); the foundational paper; [Orr 1996, *Genetics* 144:1331–1335](https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/144/4/1331/6033287); the BDM model worked out mathematically).

The unifying principle across postzygotic mechanisms: *they prevent the hybrid genes from entering the next generation*. The hybrid may live a perfectly fine individual life — the mule certainly does — but its alleles cannot move from population A into population B because the bridge organism doesn't transmit. Postzygotic isolation is the gene-flow barrier even when the individual barrier is not absolute.

### How fast do these barriers evolve?

The classical comparison comes from Coyne and Orr's work on *Drosophila* ([Coyne & Orr 1989](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1558-5646.1989.tb04233.x); [Coyne & Orr 2004](https://www.amazon.com/Speciation-Jerry-Coyne/dp/0878930892); updated analyses). They scored the strength of prezygotic and postzygotic barriers between many *Drosophila* species pairs against the genetic distance between those pairs (using Nei's D as a proxy for time since divergence). Two patterns:

1. **Both barrier types accumulate with time.** A species pair separated for 100,000 generations shows weaker isolation in both flavors than a pair separated for 5 million generations. This is the expected pattern under any of the mechanistic theories.

2. **Prezygotic barriers are systematically stronger between sympatric species pairs than between allopatric pairs of the same age.** This is the signature of *reinforcement*: when two species overlap geographically, hybrids are wasted reproductive effort, so selection favors any heritable variation that strengthens mate discrimination. Sympatric species pairs evolve prezygotic isolation faster than allopatric pairs because selection is actively pushing on the prezygotic barrier in sympatry, while allopatric pairs have no such pressure (they never meet, so there is no opportunity for selection to act on discrimination).

This is a subtle but mechanically important result. It tells you that speciation is not a passive consequence of time and divergence; it can be *accelerated by selection* under conditions where hybrids are penalized. Reinforcement is the formal name for this acceleration. We will see it again in the Lake Victoria cichlid case, where sensory drive produces strong prezygotic isolation in young species pairs precisely because mating with the wrong morph wastes reproductive effort.

## Allopatric speciation — geography does the heavy lifting

The straightforward route to speciation is the one Darwin saw on the Galápagos. A population is split into two sub-populations by a geographic barrier. Gene flow between them drops to approximately zero. Each sub-population evolves independently under its own selection pressures, its own mutation accumulation, its own drift. After enough generations, reproductive isolation has accumulated as a *by-product* of the independent evolution — sometimes through direct selection on reproductive characters, sometimes through pleiotropic effects of selection on other traits. If the populations subsequently come back into contact, they may not interbreed; if they do interbreed, hybrids may be selected against and reinforcement strengthens the barrier.

This is **allopatric speciation** — speciation with geographic separation. Two flavors are worth distinguishing because they have different mechanisms, different signatures in the data, and different time courses.

### Vicariance

**Vicariance** (from Latin *vicarius*, "substitute," via Croizat 1958 and Wiley 1981 vicariance biogeography) is allopatric speciation where the geographic barrier *arises within an existing range*. The population is contiguous, then something splits it — a rising mountain range, a forming river, an emerging isthmus, a glacier advancing through a forest. The two halves of the population become isolated by an event external to the organisms.

The cleanest worked example is the closure of the **Isthmus of Panama** about 3 million years ago ([O'Dea et al. 2016, *Sci Adv* 2:e1600883](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.1600883); the modern dating of the closure, which is debated and may be older than 3 mya in some readings). Before the isthmus closed, the tropical Pacific and Caribbean were connected as one ocean, and many shallow-water marine species occupied both sides — snapping shrimp (*Alpheus*), sea urchins, fish, mollusks. As the isthmus closed, populations on the Pacific side and populations on the Caribbean side were progressively cut off. Today, comparative DNA work shows roughly 20 pairs of *Alpheus* snapping shrimp where one species lives on the Pacific side, its sister lives on the Caribbean side, the two are 1–3% divergent in mitochondrial DNA, and laboratory crosses produce inviable or sterile hybrids ([Knowlton et al. 1993, *Science* 260:1629–1632](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.260.5114.1629); the foundational *Alpheus* paper). These are *geminate species* — twins, born of a single vicariance event, evolving independently ever since.

A more recent and geographically closer example: the Grand Canyon, deepening over the last 5 million years, has divided populations of the antelope ground squirrel into north-rim (*Ammospermophilus harrisii*) and south-rim (*A. leucurus*) species, morphologically distinct, genetically diverged, and reproductively isolated when tested in laboratory crosses ([Hafner et al. 2007, *Mol Phylogenet Evol* 42:687–708](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1055790306003654); [verify — phylogeography of *Ammospermophilus*]). The canyon is the vicariant barrier; the speciation is the consequence.

### Dispersal

**Dispersal** (or *founder-event speciation*) is allopatric speciation where the barrier was already there and the organisms cross it. A few founders reach a new island, a new lake, a new mountain valley, a new patch of habitat. Once across, they are reproductively isolated from the source population by the same geographic barrier they crossed, but the founding event also brings in genetic peculiarities — small founder population, sampling bias relative to source genetics, possible founder-flush effects on the next several generations — that can accelerate divergence beyond what slow allopatric drift would produce.

The Hawaiian honeycreepers are a dispersal-speciation textbook case. A single founding finch — current best phylogenetic inference suggests a Eurasian rosefinch ancestor (*Carpodacus*) ([Lerner et al. 2011, *Curr Biol* 21:1838–1844](https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(11)01084-7)) — colonized the Hawaiian archipelago somewhere between 4 and 7 million years ago, when the oldest currently-existing islands were forming. The descendants diversified into about 50 species in that time, with beak morphologies ranging from long curved nectar-probing beaks (*Drepanis*, *Hemignathus*) to thick parrot-like seed-cracking beaks (*Psittirostra* now extinct) to small insectivorous beaks (*Loxops*, *Paroreomyza*). Each species evolved its specialized beak on a different island or in a different elevational zone, in geographic isolation from its siblings, under the local selection pressure of available food.

The Galápagos finches are the same machinery in a different archipelago. About 18 species, descended from a single founder species perhaps 2-3 million years ago, distributed across 13 main islands. Each island's finches evolved beak shapes adapted to local food, with secondary speciation between islands as different lineages developed different reproductive characters (we will work through the Daphne Major *Geospiza fortis/G. fuliginosa* case below as the chapter's deep-dive).

### Time to speciation under allopatry

How long does allopatric speciation take? The honest answer is "it varies enormously." Coyne and Orr's *Drosophila* dataset suggests that complete reproductive isolation in fruit flies takes on the order of 1.5 to 5 million years under typical allopatric conditions; the Hawaiian *Drosophila* radiations took 5 to 10 million years to produce roughly 600 species; the Lake Victoria cichlids did 500 species in less than 100,000 years (where the speed comes from sympatric or microparapatric mechanisms, not pure allopatry).

The variance has several sources. *Selection intensity* matters: populations under strong differential selection diverge faster than populations under weak selection in similar environments. *Effective population size* matters: smaller populations drift faster and may speciate by drift alone in tens of thousands of generations, while large populations require more time. *Genetic architecture* matters: traits controlled by few large-effect loci diverge faster than traits controlled by many small-effect loci, because the former can shift modally in a few selective sweeps while the latter shift gradually. *Pleiotropic linkage between reproductive characters and ecological characters* matters: if selection on a beak shape happens to also affect song frequency (as in *Geospiza*), then ecological divergence drags reproductive isolation along with it, and speciation is fast.

The rough rule for an introductory chapter: allopatric speciation in vertebrates usually takes hundreds of thousands to millions of years; in invertebrates, sometimes faster; in plants with polyploidy available, sometimes a single generation (and we will see why next).

## Sympatric speciation — same place, different fate

The harder case — and historically the contested one — is **sympatric speciation**: reproductive isolation evolving without geographic separation. The two incipient species inhabit overlapping ranges, with potential gene flow at every generation, and yet reproductive isolation accumulates faster than gene flow can erase it.

Sympatric speciation was for a long time treated as theoretically possible but empirically rare ([Mayr 1963](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674865419); Mayr's *Animal Species and Evolution* explicitly argued sympatric speciation was nearly impossible in animals). The reason for the skepticism: gene flow between sympatric populations is a strong homogenizing force. Even one migrant per generation between two populations (Nm = 1, in the notation of Chapter 10) is sufficient to prevent neutral divergence by drift; sympatric populations have Nm rates orders of magnitude higher than that, so divergence must be driven by selection strong enough to overcome the homogenizing migration.

The 1980s-2000s saw a quiet but firm revival of sympatric speciation as a documented phenomenon, driven by three main case classes: polyploidy in plants (which is so well-established it was always uncontroversial); host-shift speciation in phytophagous insects; and sensory-drive speciation in cichlids. We take these in turn.

### Polyploidy — instant reproductive isolation by chromosome count

**Polyploidy** is the condition of having more than two complete chromosome sets. Most familiar organisms are *diploid* (two sets, written 2n); polyploids can be *triploid* (3n), *tetraploid* (4n), *hexaploid* (6n), and so on. Polyploidy occurs when meiosis fails in a way that produces gametes with the unreduced full somatic chromosome set instead of the halved gametic set.

There are two flavors of polyploidy that matter for speciation: autopolyploidy and allopolyploidy.

**Autopolyploidy** is genome doubling within a single species. A diploid (2n) parent produces, by meiotic failure, an unreduced gamete with the full 2n chromosome complement. If two such unreduced gametes fuse — which happens at low rate and is more likely if the species is partially self-fertilizing — the offspring is a tetraploid (4n), carrying four copies of every chromosome from the same source species.

Here is the speciation event. Cross the tetraploid (4n) back to a diploid (2n) parent and the offspring is a *triploid* (3n) — three copies of each chromosome. During meiosis in the triploid, each chromosome triplet tries to pair, but with three homologs at a synapsis you can pair only two at a time. The third chromosome is left unpaired (called a *univalent*) and segregates randomly into one daughter cell or the other. With *n* univalents segregating independently, the probability of any given gamete receiving a full balanced complement (a complete set of either 1 of each chromosome, or 2 of each) is approximately (1/2)^n — vanishingly small for any organism with more than a handful of chromosomes. For a plant with 2n = 14 (so n = 7 univalents in the triploid), the probability is (1/2)^7 = 1/128. For 2n = 20, it's 1/1024. Most triploid gametes carry unbalanced chromosome complements; most fertilization events produce inviable zygotes; the triploid is functionally sterile.

The tetraploid, by contrast, can pair its chromosomes during meiosis as either two bivalents (chromosome 1A-1B pair, 1C-1D pair) or one quadrivalent (1A-1B-1C-1D all paired together). Either way, meiosis can complete; balanced 2n gametes form; the tetraploid breeds true with other tetraploids. The tetraploid is reproductively isolated from the diploid parent population in a single generation, by the meiotic-arithmetic mechanism — no time, no divergence, no behavioral barrier required.

This is sympatric speciation in its cleanest form. The tetraploid and the diploid live in the same patch of habitat. They can encounter each other. They can attempt cross-pollination. The cross produces triploids, the triploids are sterile, no gene flow occurs. Two species exist in the same square meter from the moment the tetraploid forms.

**Allopolyploidy** is more common in nature and more important for crop plants. The mechanism: a hybrid between two related species — say species A (2n_A chromosomes) and species B (2n_B chromosomes) — produces an F1 offspring with n_A + n_B chromosomes that cannot pair properly during meiosis (chromosomes from A have no homologs from B and vice versa). The F1 is sterile, like a mule. But occasionally meiotic failure in this hybrid produces an unreduced gamete that carries the full set of both parental genomes — n_A + n_B chromosomes in the gamete instead of (n_A + n_B)/2. If two such unreduced gametes fuse, the resulting offspring is an *allotetraploid* with 2n_A + 2n_B chromosomes: two complete A genomes and two complete B genomes. Now meiosis can succeed, because each A chromosome has a homolog (another A chromosome) and each B chromosome has a homolog (another B chromosome). The allotetraploid is fertile, and its offspring breed true.

Bread wheat (*Triticum aestivum*) is the textbook allopolyploid. It is hexaploid (6n = 42 chromosomes), with three distinct genomes labeled A, B, and D, each contributed by a different wild ancestor. The A genome comes from *Triticum urartu*, the B genome from a species closely related to *Aegilops speltoides*, and the D genome from *Aegilops tauschii* ([Marcussen et al. 2014, *Science* 345:1250092](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1250092); the wheat genome phylogeny). The hybridizations and chromosome-doubling events occurred during early agricultural domestication, possibly 6,000-10,000 years ago for the BAD combination, with the A-B hybridization much older ([Pont et al. 2019, *Nat Genet* 51:905–911](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-019-0393-z)). Cotton (*Gossypium hirsutum*) is an allotetraploid; strawberry (*Fragaria × ananassa*) is an allooctoploid (8n = 56). The plant biology textbook estimate that around 50% of flowering plant species are polyploid at some point in their evolutionary history (most ancient polyploidy is *paleopolyploidy*, where genome doubling occurred long ago and subsequent chromosome rearrangements have obscured the original ploidy) traces to careful comparative work on the genomic signatures of past doublings ([Soltis & Soltis 2009, *Annu Rev Plant Biol* 60:561–588](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev.arplant.043008.092039); the polyploidy review).

Polyploidy works in plants, fails in most animals. Why? The answer is sex determination. Plants are mostly hermaphroditic, with male and female reproductive organs in the same flower or the same individual; doubling the genome doesn't produce a sex-imbalance problem because there is no separate sex chromosome to imbalance. In animals with XY or ZW sex determination, polyploidy doubles the sex chromosomes too — an XX female becomes XXXX, an XY male becomes XXYY — and the dosage-balance machinery (X-inactivation in mammals, MSL complex in *Drosophila*) is exquisitely tuned to the diploid ratio and fails badly when the ratio changes. Polyploid animal embryos usually die during sex determination. A few groups — some fish, some amphibians (especially in the genera *Xenopus* and *Ambystoma*), some invertebrates with sex determination by genome ratio — tolerate polyploidy. Most animals, including all mammals and all birds, do not.

The student takeaway: polyploidy is the canonical mechanism by which sympatric speciation occurs frequently and in real time in plants. About half of all flowering plant species owe their existence to polyploidy at some point. The mechanism is mechanical, not behavioral or ecological — chromosome arithmetic during meiosis produces instant reproductive isolation between the tetraploid and the diploid. We have observed polyploid speciation in real time in agricultural and ornamental plants in the last century; it is not in any sense a theoretical mechanism.

### Host shift — Rhagoletis pomonella in 150 years

The cleanest animal case of sympatric speciation in real time is the **apple maggot fly**, *Rhagoletis pomonella*, in eastern North America.

The story: *Rhagoletis pomonella* is a small tephritid fly whose ancestral host in North America was the native hawthorn (*Crataegus*). Females lay eggs in hawthorn fruits; larvae develop in the fruit and emerge as adults timed to the hawthorn fruiting season. The flies mate on or near their host fruit, so mate choice is coupled to host choice. This is the natural history that existed before European colonization.

Around 1850, *Rhagoletis pomonella* was first reported attacking introduced apple trees (*Malus domestica*) in the Hudson Valley of New York State — apples having been brought from Europe over the prior two centuries and planted widely in northeastern orchards ([Walsh 1867, *Trans Am Entomol Soc* 3:300–304](https://www.jstor.org/stable/25076252); the earliest report). A small subset of the hawthorn-using population had shifted to apples as a host. The shift was costly initially — apples have a different chemical profile, and many of the host-shifted larvae died — but a tiny fraction succeeded.

What happened next is the speciation. *Apples fruit about three to four weeks earlier than hawthorns* in northeastern North America. Selection on host-shifted flies favored earlier eclosion (adults emerging from pupation in time to find apple fruits at the right developmental stage). Within a few decades, the apple-using population had become temporally offset from the hawthorn-using population — apple flies eclosed in late July, hawthorn flies in late August, the temporal overlap shrank to a few days per year. Combined with the natural-history fact that *Rhagoletis* flies mate on or near their host fruit, the host shift produced *both* habitat isolation (apple flies on apples, hawthorn flies on hawthorns) *and* temporal isolation (different eclosion windows) in roughly 150 years — about 150 generations.

The genetic divergence between apple-using and hawthorn-using populations is now documented at multiple loci, particularly loci affecting diapause length (which controls eclosion timing) and host-preference behavior ([Feder et al. 1988, *Evolution* 42:929–934](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1558-5646.1988.tb02513.x); [Filchak et al. 2000, *Nature* 407:739–742](https://www.nature.com/articles/35037578); [Powell et al. 2013, *Heredity* 110:511–520](https://www.nature.com/articles/hdy20131)). The two populations have FST values around 0.1–0.2 at these loci, with much lower FST at neutral loci elsewhere in the genome — the signature of speciation-with-gene-flow where selection maintains divergence at host-preference loci while migration homogenizes the rest of the genome ([Egan et al. 2015, *Ecol Lett* 18:817–825](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ele.12460); the islands-of-divergence picture).

The flies are not yet fully reproductively isolated; gene flow between them is non-zero. But they are well along the path. Whether they will ever become "complete" species in the BSC sense — gene flow exactly zero — depends on whether the selection pressure on host preference and timing continues to outpace migration. They are a worked example of speciation *in progress*, observable in living memory.

Why does this matter beyond *Rhagoletis*? Because the same mechanism — host-shift coupled to mate-choice-on-host — is a general route to sympatric speciation in phytophagous insects. The pea aphid (*Acyrthosiphon pisum*) has host races on alfalfa and red clover. The leaf beetle *Neochlamisus bebbianae* has races on willow and maple. Sympatric speciation in insects, when it happens, often goes through this host-coupling channel ([Berlocher & Feder 2002, *Annu Rev Entomol* 47:773–815](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev.ento.47.091201.145312); the sympatric-speciation-in-phytophagous-insects review).

### Sensory drive — cichlids and the spectral window

The third sympatric mechanism is the one we previewed in the chapter opener: **sensory drive**, formalized in Endler's 1992 paper ([Endler 1992, *Am Nat* 139:S125–S153](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/285308); the canonical sensory drive paper). The argument: in any sensory channel an organism uses to discriminate mates, *the sensory environment* (light, water clarity, background noise, chemical milieu) constrains which signals work and which don't. Selection on the signal will produce signals matched to the local sensory environment. If the sensory environment varies across the range of a species — different light at different depths in a lake, different ambient sound in different forest habitats — then different sub-populations will evolve different mate-recognition signals, and assortative mating by signal will follow.

The Lake Victoria cichlid case is the textbook application. *Pundamilia pundamilia* and *P. nyererei* live at slightly different depths in the same rocky reefs; they differ in male body color (blue versus red); they differ in the wavelength tuning of their long-wavelength-sensitive opsin gene (*LWS*); females of each species prefer males whose color matches the spectral window their visual system is best at detecting ([Carleton et al. 2005, *Curr Biol* 15:2236–2243](https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(05)01403-3); the opsin tuning paper). The two species are in sympatry — within meters of each other — but the assortative mating produced by the depth-coupled visual signal maintains genetic separation.

This is sympatric speciation through sensory drive: the geographic distribution does not separate the populations, but the *sensory environment* gradient does, and the signal divergence that follows is enough to maintain reproductive isolation. When water turbidity from agricultural runoff erodes the visual signal — as it has in Lake Victoria since the 1980s — the species fuse. Sensory drive's contribution to reproductive isolation is *contingent on the sensory environment that produced it*, and the mechanism is reversible.

A close parallel exists in *Drosophila* species pairs that discriminate via pheromone signals (chemical sensory drive) and in birds that discriminate via song frequency (acoustic sensory drive in forest versus open-habitat birds). The general claim — that mate-recognition signal divergence driven by environmental sensory gradients can drive sympatric speciation — is now well-supported across multiple taxa, though the precise conditions under which it produces complete versus partial speciation remain an active area ([Boughman 2002, *Trends Ecol Evol* 17:571–577](https://www.cell.com/trends/ecology-evolution/fulltext/S0169-5347(02)02595-8); the sensory drive review).

## Worked example — speciation on Daphne Major, end to end

Now I want to walk through one speciation event from start to finish, tracing what each of the five forces from Chapter 10 is doing as the two species pull apart. The case is the Galápagos *Geospiza* radiation, focused on the small ground finch (*Geospiza fuliginosa*) and the medium ground finch (*Geospiza fortis*) on Daphne Major Island — two sister species that share the island, hybridize occasionally, and provide the best-documented contemporary speciation system in vertebrates ([Grant & Grant 2014, *40 Years of Evolution: Darwin's Finches on Daphne Major Island*, Princeton University Press](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691160467/40-years-of-evolution); the book-length summary; [Lamichhaney et al. 2015, *Nature* 518:371–375](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14181); the genomic analysis; [Lamichhaney et al. 2018, *Science* 359:224–228](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aao4593); the Big Bird lineage paper).

### Step 1 — colonization, ~2-3 mya

The Galápagos Islands are volcanic, emerging from the East Pacific Rise over the last few million years. The oldest currently-existing islands are about 4-5 million years old; the youngest (Fernandina, Isabela) are less than 1 million. The ancestral finch population colonized the archipelago from South America some 2-3 million years ago — current best phylogenetic inference points to a tanager-like ancestor (*Tiaris* or close relative) rather than a "true finch," with the founding flock probably comprising a few dozen birds blown off-course from the South American mainland ([Sato et al. 2001, *Mol Biol Evol* 18:299–311](https://academic.oup.com/mbe/article/18/3/299/1080990)).

This founding event is dispersal-speciation in its purest form: a small population, on a new archipelago, with empty ecological niches, and no immediate possibility of gene flow back to the South American source. The founders carried a subset of the parental population's genetic variation — a founder effect, as in Chapter 10. They began diversifying immediately.

### Step 2 — inter-island divergence, ~2 my

For most of the 2-3 million years since colonization, Galápagos finches have been diversifying *between islands*. Each island has slightly different food availability — some have abundant cactus (*Opuntia* — provides cactus finches' soft fruits and arils), some have abundant *Tribulus* (the spiked mericarps that select for thick beaks), some have abundant insects (small insectivorous finches), some have abundant fruit (vegetarian finches, large ground finches). Each island's finch population evolved beak morphology adapted to local food.

During this period, gene flow between islands is low but non-zero. Birds occasionally cross between islands — a strong wind, a storm, juvenile dispersal. The rate is enough that without selection, populations would homogenize; with selection on beak morphology different on each island, divergence accumulates faster than gene flow erases it. Classic ecological-selection-driven allopatric speciation.

Over 2 million years, the ancestral founder lineage produced 18 currently-recognized species of Darwin's finch ([Lamichhaney et al. 2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14181); the genome-based phylogeny), partitioned among the major islands and ecological types. Three ground finches (*Geospiza fortis*, *G. fuliginosa*, *G. magnirostris*) share many islands, including Daphne Major, distinguished primarily by body size and beak depth.

### Step 3 — secondary sympatry on Daphne Major

Daphne Major is small, about 0.34 km², a single volcanic cone. Today it hosts *Geospiza fortis* (medium ground finch) and *G. scandens* (cactus finch) as resident breeding species, with *G. fuliginosa* (small ground finch) and *G. magnirostris* (large ground finch) as occasional immigrants from neighboring islands. The Grants began their work on Daphne Major in 1973, and have been tracking the same population continuously since.

In 1982, an El Niño event flooded Daphne Major with rain, producing an abundance of small soft seeds. Small-beaked *G. fortis* did well, average beak depth on the island dropped. In 1977, drought (Chapter 09's worked example) had pushed beak depth up. In 2003-2005, a second drought further intensified, and an immigrant lineage of large-beaked finches (subsequently named the "Big Bird" lineage) interacted with the existing *G. fortis* in a particularly striking way that I want to trace in detail because it captures multiple speciation moves at once.

### Step 4 — the Big Bird lineage, in real time

In 1981, a single male *Geospiza* immigrated to Daphne Major from a neighboring island, almost certainly Santa Cruz. Genetic analysis later identified him as either a hybrid *G. fortis* × *G. conirostris* or a *G. conirostris* with non-standard morphology — a large-beaked bird, larger than the local *G. fortis* mean. He bred with local *G. fortis* females (his hybrid offspring formed an F1 generation, themselves substantially larger-beaked than typical *G. fortis*).

Over the next several generations, the F1 hybrids and their descendants bred preferentially with each other rather than with the local *G. fortis* mainstream. The Grants documented this in detail ([Grant & Grant 2009, *PNAS* 106:20141–20148](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0911761106)). The mechanism of assortative mating turns out to be coupled to beak shape: beak shape affects the resonant frequency of finch song (a bird sings through its bill, and the bill's shape filters the frequency), so birds with similar beak shapes sing similar songs, and finches learn to recognize potential mates by song templates established early in life. Large-beaked birds sang large-beaked songs; small-beaked birds sang small-beaked songs; the F1 hybrids and their descendants sang an in-between song that *G. fortis* mainstream individuals largely ignored.

By 2009-2010 — about seven generations after the original immigrant arrived — the Big Bird lineage on Daphne Major was a self-reproducing population, maintaining its distinct beak morphology and distinct song, breeding only with other Big Birds, and showing reproductive isolation from the surrounding *G. fortis*. The Grants and Lamichhaney's group reported it as a contemporary observation of speciation in progress ([Lamichhaney et al. 2018](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aao4593); the genomic analysis showing fixed differences at the *ALX1* and *HMGA2* loci that govern beak morphology).

Whether the Big Bird lineage is "really a species" depends on how strictly you apply the BSC. It is reproductively isolated from *G. fortis* through behavioral discrimination (song-mediated assortative mating). Its persistent gene flow with mainland and *G. fortis* sources is essentially zero. It maintains genetic distinctness over generations. At minimum, it is at species-pair boundary. The Grants estimate that if the current trajectory continues, complete reproductive isolation in the BSC sense will be reached within a few decades. We are watching speciation happen.

### Step 5 — what the alleles are doing

The molecular work on Darwin's finches has identified several major loci affecting beak morphology ([Abzhanov et al. 2004, *Science* 305:1462–1465](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1098095); the *BMP4* paper; [Lamichhaney et al. 2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14181); the *ALX1* identification; [Lamichhaney et al. 2016, *Science* 352:470–474](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aad8786); the *HMGA2* paper). *BMP4* affects beak depth: higher expression during embryonic development produces deeper beaks. *ALX1* affects beak shape (pointed versus blunt). *HMGA2* affects beak size scaling.

In the small ground finch (*G. fuliginosa*) versus medium ground finch (*G. fortis*) comparison, allele frequencies at these loci differ measurably between species. *G. fuliginosa* is fixed or nearly fixed for low-expression *BMP4* alleles (small beaks); *G. fortis* shows a wider distribution but with higher mean expression. The Big Bird lineage shows alleles at *ALX1* that are characteristic of *G. conirostris*-type morphology, distinguishing it from local *G. fortis*. The allele-frequency divergence at beak-shape loci tracks the morphological and behavioral divergence — exactly as you would predict from the population-genetics framework of Chapter 10 if you imagined selection on beak depth differing between two species, with the two species exchanging genes only rarely.

This is speciation made molecular. The beaks are the macroscopic phenotype. The alleles at *BMP4*, *ALX1*, and *HMGA2* are the underlying variants. The selection — differential survival under drought (small versus medium versus large seeds) — is the force from Chapter 10. The assortative mating — song templates coupled to beak shape — is the reproductive barrier. The five forces, running in two slightly-overlapping populations on a 0.34 km² island, producing speciation in observable time.

### Step 6 — the limit of the case

I want to be honest about where the worked example breaks down before we synthesize.

The Daphne Major case is not "clean" in the sense that pure allopatric or pure sympatric speciation would be. It's a *secondary-contact* case — finches that diverged in allopatry on different islands now come together on Daphne Major, where the divergence either holds (separate species) or erodes (fusion via hybridization). Hybridization happens. *G. fortis × G. scandens* hybrids occur at rates around 1-5% per generation depending on the year ([Grant & Grant 1997, *Ann Rev Ecol Syst* 28:391–414](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev.ecolsys.28.1.391)). Some of those hybrids are themselves fertile. Some leave descendants that backcross. The lineages are leaky.

So when the BSC asks "is *G. fortis* a separate species from *G. scandens*?" the honest answer is "yes, mostly, with a few percent gene flow per generation." That isn't a failure of the BSC; it's a feature of how the world actually is. Speciation is a continuous process — divergence, partial isolation, fusion-or-completion — and species are useful abstractions over the messy real thing. The Big Bird lineage is a species in progress. The *G. fortis × G. scandens* pair is a near-completion species pair with leaky reproductive isolation. Neither is a Platonic ideal-type. Both are working examples of the speciation machinery running in real ecological and genetic conditions.

That is the chapter's deep-dive. Hold it. We are going to use it as the reference case when we get to the misconceptions and exercises.

## Adaptive radiation — the speciation machinery, parallelized

When one ancestral lineage diversifies rapidly into many descendant species exploiting many different ecological niches, the result is called an **adaptive radiation**. The Galápagos finches are one. The Hawaiian honeycreepers are another. African Great Lake cichlids are a third. Australian marsupials are a fourth. Hawaiian silverswords (*Argyroxiphium* and related genera, all descended from a single tarweed ancestor) are a fifth ([Baldwin & Sanderson 1998, *PNAS* 95:9402–9406](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.95.16.9402); the silversword phylogeny). The Cambrian explosion may be the largest such radiation in history, with most major animal phyla appearing within a 10-30 million year window 540 million years ago ([Erwin et al. 2011, *Science* 334:1091–1097](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1206375); the Cambrian explosion review).

Adaptive radiation is not a new mechanism. It is the same allopatric (or sympatric) speciation machinery, running in parallel across many ecological niches that became simultaneously available. The conditions that produce adaptive radiation:

1. **Ecological opportunity.** Many niches are open. This happens when (a) a lineage colonizes a new geographic area with empty niches (Hawaiian honeycreepers, Galápagos finches), (b) a mass extinction clears existing niches (mammals after the K-Pg extinction 66 mya, ammonites in the Mesozoic), (c) a new habitat type forms that no existing lineage occupies (Lake Victoria after refilling), or (d) a key innovation opens new niches (Hox-gene duplications allowing body-plan diversification in the early Cambrian, or the evolution of pharyngeal jaws in cichlids).

2. **Founder lineage with evolutionary flexibility.** The lineage must have the genetic architecture to evolve different forms quickly. The Galápagos finches had beak-developmental modularity — *BMP4*, *ALX1*, *HMGA2* — that allowed beak shape to evolve rapidly under different selection pressures. The cichlids have two sets of jaws (oral and pharyngeal) that decouple food-processing from food-capture, allowing one to evolve while the other handles function — a key innovation often cited as enabling the cichlid radiation ([Liem 1973, *Syst Zool* 22:425–441](https://academic.oup.com/sysbio/article-abstract/22/4/425/1632437); the pharyngeal-jaw paper).

3. **Geographic or sensory structure that permits parallel divergence.** The Hawaiian archipelago provides multiple islands. The Galápagos provides multiple islands plus within-island elevational gradients. Lake Victoria provides spatially-varying spectral environments. The structure means that selection can push different sub-populations in different directions simultaneously.

When all three conditions are met, adaptive radiation can produce many species rapidly. Hawaiian silverswords: about 30 species from one tarweed ancestor in 5-6 million years; morphology from compact silver-leaved alpine rosettes (*Argyroxiphium sandwicense*, restricted to Haleakalā summit) to tall green-leaved shrubs (*Dubautia*) to climbing vines (*Wilkesia*). The morphological diversity within Hawaiian silverswords exceeds the morphological diversity within the entire sunflower family (Asteraceae) on the North American mainland that the silverswords descend from ([Carlquist 1980, *Hawaii: A Natural History*, Pacific Tropical Botanical Garden](https://www.amazon.com/Hawaii-Natural-History-Geology-Climate/dp/0915809079); the foundational natural-history book).

Australian marsupials are the macroevolutionary version. Australia separated from Gondwana around 35-40 million years ago, carrying a population of early marsupial mammals into geographic isolation from the placental mammals that subsequently radiated across Eurasia, the Americas, and Africa. In isolation, the Australian marsupials radiated into ecological roles occupied elsewhere by placentals — Tasmanian wolf (*Thylacinus cynocephalus*, recently extinct) convergently evolving the body plan of *Canis lupus*; the sugar glider (*Petaurus breviceps*) convergently evolving the gliding form of the placental flying squirrel (*Glaucomys*); the marsupial mole (*Notoryctes typhlops*) convergently evolving the body plan of the placental mole (*Talpa europaea*); kangaroos and wallabies filling ecological roles that on other continents were occupied by ungulates. The convergence is mechanistically informative: similar ecological opportunities under similar physical constraints tend to produce similar phenotypes, even when the underlying lineages are unrelated ([Springer et al. 1997, *Nature* 388:61–64](https://www.nature.com/articles/40386); marsupial-placental molecular phylogeny).

The Cambrian explosion — the most dramatic radiation in life's history — is harder to pin down causally. The fossil record shows that most modern animal phyla (about 30 of them) appeared within a roughly 10-30 million year window starting around 540 million years ago. Before that window, the fossil record contains mostly microbial mats, possibly some Ediacaran multicellular soft-bodied organisms, and very few clearly bilaterian animals. After that window, the major bilaterian body plans (mollusks, arthropods, echinoderms, chordates) are all in place and recognizable. What caused the explosion? Candidate explanations include (a) the duplication and elaboration of *Hox* genes enabling new body-plan diversification ([Carroll 2008, *Cell* 134:25–36](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(08)00819-6); evo-devo review — Chapter 13 will deep-dive on this), (b) atmospheric oxygen reaching a threshold permitting large active animals with high metabolic rates, (c) the evolution of predation triggering an arms race in defensive morphology (shells, exoskeletons), or (d) some combination. The honest position: the Cambrian explosion is the most striking adaptive radiation we know of, the causal mechanism is partially understood, and the precise contribution of each candidate explanation remains an active research question.

The takeaway for this chapter: adaptive radiations are not a separate mechanism of speciation. They are the same allopatric and sympatric machinery, run in parallel, across many ecological opportunities at once, in a lineage with the genetic flexibility to diversify. When you understand single speciation events, you understand adaptive radiations. The product looks dramatic — 50 honeycreepers, 500 cichlids, 30 phyla — but the machinery is the same.

## Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities — the genetics of hybrid breakdown

The most elegant piece of theory in this chapter is the **Dobzhansky-Muller model** for the genetic basis of postzygotic isolation. I want to walk through it because it explains why postzygotic barriers accumulate gradually with time and why hybrid breakdown can appear in F2 even when F1 hybrids look fine.

The puzzle the model solves: how can two populations evolve genetic incompatibility without either population passing through an intermediate "low-fitness" state? Any mutation that produced incompatibility with the existing population would be selected against immediately. So how do you build up the kind of mutations that, when combined with mutations from a different population, produce hybrid sterility or inviability?

Dobzhansky's 1936 answer and Muller's 1940s elaboration ([Dobzhansky 1936, *Genetics* 21:113–135](https://www.genetics.org/content/21/2/113); [Muller 1942, *Biol Symp* 6:71–125](https://books.google.com/books?id=zaY_AAAAYAAJ); [Orr 1996](https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/144/4/1331/6033287)): incompatibility evolves through *complementary substitutions at different loci*. Consider two genes A and B that interact (their proteins bind, or one regulates the other). Start with the ancestral genotype AABB. The ancestral population is fixed for genotype AABB at both loci.

Now split the population into two isolates, X and Y.

In population X, a mutation arises at locus A, producing allele a. The mutation is initially deleterious because Aa heterozygotes are slightly destabilized at the AB interaction, but it confers a small fitness benefit on its own, or it is neutral and drifts to fixation. After enough generations, population X is fixed for genotype aaBB.

In population Y, a mutation arises at locus B, producing allele b. By a similar process, the mutation is fine in the AABB background and drifts to fixation. Population Y is fixed for genotype AAbb.

Crucially: neither aaBB nor AAbb is itself low-fitness. The aa change is compatible with BB (population X has been doing it for thousands of generations). The bb change is compatible with AA (population Y likewise). Each population, on its own, is at a perfectly viable optimum.

Now bring the populations back together and cross them. F1 hybrids have genotype AaBb (one chromosome from each parent at each locus). The F1 has the *a* allele at the A locus and the *b* allele at the B locus *for the first time in evolutionary history*. The *ab* combination has never been tested by selection in either population. If *a* and *b* are mutually incompatible — if the products of *a* and *b* interact badly with each other — the AaBb hybrid suffers fitness loss.

In F2, where Mendelian segregation produces all 16 combinations of A/a and B/b, the genotype aabb (homozygous for both new alleles, never tested in either parental lineage) appears at frequency 1/16. If aabb is severely incompatible — say, lethal — then 1/16 of F2 individuals die. This is hybrid breakdown: F1 looks okay, F2 has elevated mortality, the underlying mechanism is segregation of complementary substitutions.

The arithmetic for accumulation: if each population fixes substitutions at rate *r* per locus per generation, then after *t* generations, each population has fixed approximately *rt* mutations. The number of *pairs* of substitutions (one from each population) that could produce incompatibilities scales as the *square* of *rt* — that is, the number of cross-population complementary pairs grows as t². The number of Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities should grow faster than linearly with divergence time, a prediction known as the **snowball effect** ([Orr 1995, *Genetics* 139:1805–1813](https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/139/4/1805/6033017)). Empirically, this has been confirmed in *Drosophila* and *Mimulus* (monkeyflowers) species pairs, where the number of identified hybrid-sterility loci roughly squares with divergence time ([Matute et al. 2010, *Science* 329:1518–1521](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1193440)).

The reflexive moment: the Dobzhansky-Muller model is a *theoretical mechanism* that solves a conceptual problem about how incompatibility can evolve. It does not say that *every* hybrid sterility is Dobzhansky-Muller. Some hybrid sterility is due to chromosomal rearrangements (the mule's odd chromosome number is a different mechanism), some is due to genome-wide regulatory mismatches, some is due to specific maternal-effect or zygotic-effect failures. Dobzhansky-Muller is the model that handles the case of *epistatic incompatibility between specific gene pairs*, and it has been mechanistically confirmed for several specific loci ([Presgraves 2010, *Nat Rev Genet* 11:175–180](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrg2718)). For other cases, other models apply. Use the right tool for the right question — that is the workshop's reflexive commitment.

## When two populations meet — speciation, fusion, or stable polymorphism?

We have the machinery. We can ask the prediction question: given two populations with measured divergence and measured gene-flow rate, what happens?

The framework, from Chapter 10's five-forces backbone applied to two populations:

Let *m* be the per-generation migration rate (the fraction of one population that comes from the other each generation). Let *s* be the strength of selection differentially favoring different alleles in the two populations (the typical selection coefficient at a locus under divergent selection). Let *Ne* be the effective population size of each population. The key dimensionless quantities are:

- **Nm** (the number of migrants per generation, *Ne × m*): determines how strong the homogenizing migration force is relative to drift.
- **Ns** (the strength of selection scaled by population size): determines how effective selection is at maintaining differences.
- **m/s** (migration relative to selection): determines whether selection at a locus can maintain divergence against migration.

The classic result ([Wright 1931, *Genetics* 16:97–159](https://www.genetics.org/content/16/2/97); [Slatkin 1985, *Annu Rev Ecol Syst* 16:393–430](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev.es.16.110185.002141); the gene flow review):

**If Nm > 1 and m/s > 1 (strong migration, weak selection), populations fuse.** Migration overwhelms selection; gene flow homogenizes the populations; whatever incipient divergence existed erodes. Lake Victoria cichlids under eutrophication-induced turbidity are an empirical example: when the visual signal that drove assortative mating breaks down, *m* effectively rises, *s* on color falls, and the populations fuse.

**If Nm < 1 and m/s < 1 (low migration, strong selection), populations diverge.** Selection at locally-adapted loci maintains divergence faster than migration erases it. Speciation proceeds. *Rhagoletis* apple flies versus hawthorn flies are an empirical example: selection on diapause timing (*s* moderate to strong at the relevant loci) outpaces gene flow (low *m* because flies mate on host fruit).

**If Nm and m/s are intermediate, populations may settle into a stable polymorphism.** Some loci diverge (those under strong divergent selection), other loci homogenize (those under weak or no selection). The result is a population pair with *islands of divergence* — small genomic regions where selection has maintained allele-frequency differences — embedded in a sea of genome-wide gene flow. This is exactly the pattern seen in *Rhagoletis*, in the European corn borer (*Ostrinia nubilalis*) E-race and Z-race, and in many other host-race or partially-isolated species pairs ([Wu 2001, *J Evol Biol* 14:851–865](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1046/j.1420-9101.2001.00335.x); the genic view of speciation).

This is what FST (the genetic-divergence statistic from Chapter 10) is reading when we apply it to two populations. FST measured genome-wide tells you the average divergence; FST measured locus by locus tells you which regions are under divergent selection (high FST) and which are homogenized by migration (low FST). Modern speciation genomics is, in significant part, the science of reading these locus-by-locus FST patterns to identify the genomic islands where speciation is happening.

The student-facing summary: speciation, fusion, and stable polymorphism are three outcomes of the same machinery under different parameter regimes. They are not categorically different processes. Given Nm, Ns, and the genetic architecture of differentially-selected traits, you can predict which outcome a population pair is heading toward.

## Hybridization can produce species too

One last twist that we have not yet handled: sometimes hybridization between species *produces* a new species, rather than eroding existing species boundaries.

**Hybrid speciation** is a documented mechanism in plants and, more rarely, in animals. The cleanest plant case is the sunflower genus *Helianthus*. *Helianthus annuus* (the common sunflower) and *Helianthus petiolaris* (the prairie sunflower) hybridize naturally where their ranges overlap in the central United States. Most hybrids are sterile or low-fitness. But occasionally, hybridization plus chromosomal rearrangements produces stable hybrid lineages with distinct karyotypes and ecological niches. *Helianthus anomalus*, *Helianthus paradoxus*, and *Helianthus deserticola* are three hybrid-derived species that occupy desert habitats neither parental species can tolerate ([Rieseberg et al. 2003, *Science* 301:1211–1216](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1086949); the *Helianthus* hybrid speciation paper).

The mechanism: an F1 hybrid between *annuus* and *petiolaris* is mostly sterile due to chromosomal differences, but a few hybrids carry chromosome combinations that pair successfully in meiosis. Those hybrids backcross within their own kind, and over a few hundred generations, they sort themselves into a stable lineage with a chromosomal architecture distinct from either parent. The new lineage is reproductively isolated from both parents (because of chromosomal incompatibilities) and ecologically distinct (it occupies habitats neither parent does). It is, by the BSC, a separate species.

Allopolyploid speciation in plants (described above) is the canonical formal case of hybrid speciation: hybridization plus genome doubling produces an instantly-isolated new lineage. Wheat is the most economically important example. There are also documented hybrid-speciation events in animals — most strikingly in some fish species (Italian sparrows *Passer italiae*, possibly some butterflies in genera *Heliconius* and *Lycaeides*), though animal hybrid speciation is rare compared to plants ([Mavárez et al. 2006, *Nature* 441:868–871](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature04738); the *Heliconius* hybrid origin paper).

Modern humans and Neanderthals, finally — to preview Chapter 14 — provide a documented but partial case. About 2% of the genome of non-African modern humans is Neanderthal-derived ([Sankararaman et al. 2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature12961); [Prüfer et al. 2017, *Science* 358:655–658](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aao1887)). The introgression is the result of hybridization events around 50,000-60,000 years ago when modern humans expanding out of Africa encountered Neanderthal populations in Eurasia. The hybridization did not produce a separate "modern human × Neanderthal" species; instead, the small fraction of Neanderthal genome that introgressed into modern humans persists in our genomes today, while the Neanderthals as a distinct lineage went extinct. Chapter 14 will work through the genomics of this in detail.

The general lesson: hybridization is not just an eraser of species boundaries. It can also be a generator of new lineages, particularly through polyploidy in plants and through chromosomal rearrangements in flowering plants. Speciation and hybridization are coupled processes, not opposites.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Speciation requires geographic isolation."** This was the dominant view in the mid-20th century (largely from Mayr), and it is still common in student intuition. It is wrong as a universal claim. Polyploidy produces sympatric speciation in plants in a single generation. Host-shift speciation in *Rhagoletis* and other phytophagous insects happens in sympatry. Sensory drive in Lake Victoria cichlids produces sympatric speciation through assortative mating coupled to depth-varying light environments. Geographic isolation is the most common route to speciation in vertebrates, but it is not the only route, and in plants it is not even the most common — polyploidy plus selection are the typical plant speciation mechanisms.

**Misconception 2: "All speciation is slow."** The geological-timescale intuition makes students assume speciation takes millions of years. Some does; *Drosophila* speciation typically takes 1.5-5 million years; mammalian speciation typically takes a few million years. But the variance is enormous. Cichlid radiations in African Great Lakes produced 500+ species in less than 100,000 years (Lake Victoria) to 5 million years (Lake Malawi). Polyploid plant speciation happens in one generation. *Rhagoletis* apple flies are speciating in 150 years (~150 generations). The Big Bird lineage on Daphne Major began as a single 1981 immigrant and was reproductively isolated within 7 generations. "Slow" is the default but not the rule. When selection is strong, populations small enough for drift, and reproductive characters tightly coupled to differentially-selected traits, speciation can be fast.

**Misconception 3: "Species are clearly defined categories."** Students arriving at this chapter usually carry an intuition that "species" names a discrete kind in nature. The BSC, applied carefully, shows that "species" is a useful working concept for the modal case (clearly-isolated, reproductively-distinct populations) but breaks down at the edges (asexual organisms, fossil species, hybridizing pairs, ring species, recently-diverged populations still partially exchanging genes). Speciation is a continuous process. "Species" is a discrete label we paste onto continuous biology, useful most of the time, blurry at the edges. This isn't a problem with the BSC; it's a feature of what speciation actually is.

**Misconception 4: "Hybridization is always bad and always erases species."** Hybridization can erase species (Lake Victoria cichlids under turbidity; red wolf-coyote fusion). It can also produce species (allopolyploid wheat; *Helianthus* hybrid lineages; possibly some *Heliconius* butterflies). It can also introduce adaptive alleles between species without erasing either (Neanderthal *HLA* introgression into modern humans, the *EPAS1* high-altitude adaptation in Tibetans introgressed from Denisovans — Chapter 14 will detail). Hybridization is a process with multiple possible outcomes depending on hybrid fitness, parental population sizes, and ecological context.

**Misconception 5: "Adaptive radiations are 'just' a lot of speciation events."** True at the level of basic mechanism — they are the same speciation machinery applied in parallel. But adaptive radiations also have *emergent* features that single speciation events don't have: ecological character displacement (sister species push each other to opposite ends of phenotype space when they overlap), key innovations (one trait — pharyngeal jaws, beak modularity, Hox-gene duplication — that unlocks a whole subsequent radiation), and macroevolutionary patterns of disparity (overall morphological diversity within the radiation). Treating an adaptive radiation as merely a sum of pairwise speciation events misses what makes a radiation a *radiation*.

**Misconception 6: "Sympatric speciation is impossible (or implausible enough we can ignore it)."** The 20th-century-Mayr view. Modern empirical work shows sympatric speciation is real, common in plants (polyploidy), documented in some insects (host-shift), and contested but plausibly significant in cichlid radiations and other sensory-drive cases. The student who learned "sympatric speciation is theoretical only" is learning a 50-year-stale version of the field.

## Synthesis — what the chapter showed

Step back. The chapter did six things.

**First**, it pinned down the biological species concept with its four named failures (asexual, fossil, hybridizing, ring species). The BSC grounds species identity in the flow of genes between populations, not in morphology or any other intrinsic property. "Species" is the label we paste on a continuous gene-flow process; the label is crisp in the modal case and blurry at the edges.

**Second**, it cataloged the two flavors of reproductive isolating barriers: prezygotic (habitat, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, gametic) and postzygotic (hybrid inviability, sterility, breakdown). Each flavor has different mechanism, different time-course of accumulation, and different sensitivity to selection (prezygotic can evolve faster in sympatry via reinforcement; postzygotic accumulates roughly with divergence time).

**Third**, it distinguished allopatric speciation (geographic isolation does the heavy lifting; two flavors — vicariance and dispersal) from sympatric speciation (no geographic barrier required). Allopatric is the common route in vertebrates; sympatric is common in plants (polyploidy) and in phytophagous insects (host-shift) and in cichlids (sensory drive).

**Fourth**, it worked through one speciation event in detail — the Galápagos *Geospiza* radiation on Daphne Major, with the Big Bird lineage as a contemporary observation. The five forces from Chapter 10 (mutation, selection, drift, gene flow, non-random mating) operating in two slightly-overlapping populations, with assortative mating coupled to beak shape via song, with allele frequency divergence at *BMP4*, *ALX1*, and *HMGA2*, produces reproductive isolation in observable generations.

**Fifth**, it framed adaptive radiations (Hawaiian honeycreepers, Hawaiian silverswords, cichlids, Australian marsupials, Cambrian explosion) as the same speciation machinery applied in parallel across many simultaneously-available ecological niches. Adaptive radiations are not a separate mechanism; they are pairwise speciation events run in parallel under the right conditions (ecological opportunity, evolvable lineage, geographic or sensory structure).

**Sixth**, it walked the Dobzhansky-Muller mechanism for the genetic basis of postzygotic isolation — how compensating substitutions at different loci, each fine in their own background, produce incompatibility when combined in hybrids. The snowball effect predicts incompatibilities should grow with the square of divergence time, a prediction empirically confirmed in *Drosophila* and *Mimulus*.

Lake Victoria's 500 cichlids in less than 100,000 years. *Rhagoletis* apple flies speciating in 150 years. Galápagos finches with a 7-generation Big Bird lineage. Polyploid wheat. *Helianthus* hybrid lineages in deserts neither parent can occupy. The mechanism is the five forces from Chapter 10, running in two populations, with gene flow restricted by some combination of geographic, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, gametic, or developmental barriers. Speciation is what evolution does when the populations are not allowed to mix.

The bridge to Chapter 12. Speciation produces new lineages. Chapter 12 asks how those lineages are related to each other — the science of *phylogenetics*. Given a collection of species, can we reconstruct the tree of their evolutionary relationships from DNA, morphology, or fossil evidence? Phylogenetics is the historical reading of the speciation process: each branch point on the tree is a speciation event, each tip is a current species, the lengths of branches encode how much divergence accumulated between events. Chapter 12 will work through how phylogenetic trees are built, what assumptions they require, and what they can and cannot tell you about evolutionary history.

Population genetics gave us the engine. Speciation is what the engine produces when it runs in parallel in disconnected places. Phylogenetics is how we read what the engine has done.

When an unreduced gamete (n) fuses with a normal haploid (n/2) gamete, the offspring is triploid (3n/2) — visually shown as having three of each chromosome. When an unreduced gamete fuses with another unreduced gamete (rare event), the offspring is tetraploid (2n) — instantly reproductively isolated. Display: chromosome-pairing animation for meiosis in diploid, triploid, and tetraploid genotypes — illustrating why pairing succeeds in diploid (2n bivalent pairs) and tetraploid (multiple bivalents or quadrivalents) but fails in triploid (univalent left unpaired). Gamete-viability calculator: for a triploid with 7 univalents, fraction of balanced gametes = (1/2)^7 ≈ 0.008. Speciation timer: tracks generations until a stable tetraploid lineage establishes (typically requires multiple unreduced-gamete events; with even rare rates, establishment occurs within tens to hundreds of generations in self-fertilizing plants).

The clearest pedagogical demonstration is running all three panels at once with parameters matched to empirical cases: Panel 1 set to vicariance + 1,000 generations + Ne = 1,000 + m = 0.001 (typical vertebrate allopatric speciation, slow); Panel 2 set to host-shift in 150 generations with intermediate selection; Panel 3 set to a 50% self-fertilizing plant with unreduced-gamete rate 0.01 (typical for many flowering plants). Students should see that the three modes operate at radically different timescales (millions of years, hundreds of years, one generation), use different mechanisms (genetic divergence, selection on host-coupled traits, meiotic arithmetic), but share the underlying logic of reduced gene flow plus genetic / chromosomal divergence.

A stretch goal: a "sensory drive" sub-mode within Panel 2, where two color morphs in the same lake population are subject to female mate choice based on a visual signal whose strength varies with water clarity. Students can set the clarity parameter and watch the populations diverge (high clarity) or fuse (low clarity), recovering the Lake Victoria cichlid dynamics.

**What would change my mind.** A demonstration that the canonical sympatric speciation cases — *Rhagoletis* host races, Lake Victoria cichlids, polyploid plant lineages — are systematically misclassified. Specifically: if careful population-genetic work showed that *Rhagoletis* "host races" are actually allopatrically derived populations from different historical refugia that secondarily came into contact (rather than the standard sympatric divergence narrative); or if the Lake Victoria cichlid radiation turned out to have been seeded by multiple highly-divergent ancestral lineages rather than one ancestor (which would make most of the "radiation" a sorting of pre-existing variation rather than in-lake speciation); or if polyploid plant species turned out to consistently fuse with diploid parents over evolutionary time (which would make polyploid speciation a transient phenomenon rather than a permanent diversification mechanism). Recent careful work has tightened the case for sympatric speciation in each of these systems without overturning the qualitative claim ([Bird et al. 2012, *Trends Ecol Evol* 27:484–493](https://www.cell.com/trends/ecology-evolution/fulltext/S0169-5347(12)00159-2); the sympatric speciation review). If new work substantially undermined the empirical base, the chapter would need significant revision.

**Still puzzling.** The relative contribution of selection versus drift in driving early speciation remains hard to pin down empirically. For a population pair that has just begun diverging — say, the Big Bird lineage on Daphne Major after 7 generations — it is genuinely unclear whether the divergence is being driven primarily by selection on beak morphology, by founder effects on the small initial lineage, by chance variation in song templates picked up by the small number of immigrants, or by some combination. The signatures of selection and drift can look very similar at early stages of divergence, especially with small effective population sizes. Distinguishing them requires longer time series, larger sample sizes, and more loci than most current speciation studies have. The honest answer is "both forces are operating; we cannot yet partition them cleanly in many cases."
