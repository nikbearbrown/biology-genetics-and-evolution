**TL;DR.** Three case studies, three timescales, one mechanism stack. **Case 1 — antibiotic resistance** runs evolution in days to weeks. A β-lactamase point mutation arises in one *E. coli* cell among the 10^11 in a human gut, antibiotic exposure assigns it a selection coefficient near 1, and the resistant lineage sweeps to fixation in roughly 40 bacterial generations — under a day. R plasmids carry resistance cassettes across genus boundaries by conjugation, a kind of gene flow Mendel never imagined. Fitness costs make resistance reversible in principle, but compensatory mutations lock it in. Antibiotic stewardship is evolutionary management with a clinical face. Murray et al. 2022 in *The Lancet* estimate ~1.27 million deaths in 2019 directly attributable to bacterial antimicrobial resistance. **Case 2 — human population genetics** runs evolution at thousands to tens of thousands of years. Modern humans left Africa about 60,000–70,000 years ago through a bottleneck small enough that today, non-African genetic diversity is a strict subset of African diversity. Founder events produced Tay-Sachs at carrier frequency 1 in 30 in Ashkenazi Jews, Ellis-van Creveld syndrome in the Lancaster Amish, Finnish disease heritage. Recent selection produced lactase persistence in pastoralist populations (with at least four independent regulatory variants, the cleanest case of convergent evolution at a regulatory element in our species); skin pigmentation tuned by the UV-folate-vitamin-D balance; sickle-cell, G6PD, Duffy, and thalassemia variants all converging on malaria from different molecular angles; Tibetan EPAS1 introgressed from Denisovans. The race-and-population-structure framing requires holding two things at once: population structure is real and medically relevant, *and* race as a social category does not map cleanly onto biological clusters. Roughly 85% of human genetic variation is within any single population; 15% is between. Both facts are true. **Case 3 — molecular human evolution** runs at millions of years. Humans and chimpanzees share ~98.8% of protein-coding DNA; most differences that matter for morphology are regulatory. Human Accelerated Regions like HAR1 evolve fast in non-coding sequence while staying deeply conserved everywhere else in the vertebrate tree. FOXP2 carries two human-derived amino acid changes, but Neanderthals shared them, and Atkinson 2018 weakened the original "recent selective sweep" claim. NOTCH2NL paralogs from a hominin-lineage duplication ~3–4 million years ago expand neocortical neurogenesis and produce both intelligence-relevant biology and 1q21.1 disease syndromes from the same chromosomal instability. Svante Pääbo's lab, 2010, published the complete Neanderthal genome from three bones in Vindija Cave; the Denisovan genome came from a single finger-bone fragment; non-African humans carry roughly 2% Neanderthal ancestry, Melanesians an additional ~5% Denisovan; Pääbo won the 2022 Nobel for this. The capstone closes by reading the whole book backward: every chapter contributed a piece of machinery that the three cases require. Genetics and evolution are not two subjects but one — the same mutation-drift-selection-gene-flow stack operating on different clocks. The worked example is sickle cell, end to end. ## A finger bone, a Klebsiella, a cup of milk

In May 2010 Svante Pääbo's group at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology in Leipzig published in *Science* a 3-billion-base-pair draft assembly of the Neanderthal genome, built from three bone fragments dredged from Vindija Cave in Croatia (Green RE, Krause J, Briggs AW, et al. 2010. "A draft sequence of the Neanderthal genome." *Science* 328:710–722; [DOI 10.1126/science.1188021](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1188021); PMID 20448178). The paper's central technical claim was that ancient DNA, recovered from bones tens of thousands of years old, could be reconstructed at draft-quality completeness despite contamination by modern human DNA, despite chemical degradation, despite the bones having been excavated and stored for decades before sequencing was even possible. The paper's central biological claim was that *non-African modern humans carry roughly 1 to 4 percent Neanderthal ancestry*. We are partial hybrids of an extinct hominin lineage, and the evidence is in our genomes.

This is the moment everything in this book converges. To read the Pääbo paper you have to bring meiosis and recombination from Chapter 1, because the Neanderthal ancestry is broken into chromosomal blocks whose lengths give the date of admixture. You have to bring Mendelian inheritance from Chapter 2, because the blocks segregate predictably across generations. You have to bring DNA structure and damage from Chapter 4, because ancient DNA is post-mortem deaminated cytosine to uracil at characteristic positions, and the lab has to correct for that. You have to bring the gene-to-protein chain from Chapter 5 to read the protein-coding changes. You have to bring mutation rates from Chapter 8 to date the human-Neanderthal split (~500–700 thousand years ago, give or take). You have to bring population genetics from Chapter 10 to estimate the admixture proportion from the frequency of Neanderthal-derived alleles in non-African populations and zero in Africans. You have to bring speciation from Chapter 11, because Neanderthals and modern humans were *not* fully reproductively isolated — the species concept stretches at the edges. You have to bring the phylogenetic methods of Chapter 12 to place the Neanderthal lineage on the tree. You have to bring the molecular-evolution machinery of Chapter 13 to compute substitution rates along the lineages.

The Pääbo paper requires all thirteen prior chapters of this book.

Now consider two other moments that happened at roughly the same time as Pääbo was assembling that genome. In a Chennai hospital in 2008, a Swedish patient who had been hospitalized in India was found to be infected with a *Klebsiella pneumoniae* strain carrying a previously undescribed metallo-β-lactamase, named NDM-1 after the city. NDM-1 hydrolyzes carbapenems, the last-resort class of β-lactam antibiotics, and it sits on a plasmid that has spread through Enterobacteriaceae across the world in under five years (Yong D, Toleman MA, Giske CG, et al. 2009. "Characterization of a new metallo-β-lactamase gene, *bla*NDM-1, and a novel erythromycin esterase gene carried on a unique genetic structure in *Klebsiella pneumoniae* sequence type 14 from India." *Antimicrob Agents Chemother* 53:5046–5054). Twenty years before that paper, NDM-1 didn't exist. Now it is on every continent. To understand how this happened you need mutation (Ch 8), selection (Chs 9 and 10), gene flow (Ch 10), and a non-Mendelian form of gene flow — bacterial conjugation — that the book has only previewed.

And consider a Maasai pastoralist boma in the Rift Valley around 1968, where Frank Allchin and others were beginning to ask why some adult humans digest milk and others get sick from it. The genetic answer, worked out over the next four decades, is that a regulatory variant in an intron of *MCM6* upstream of the *LCT* gene keeps lactase expression on through adulthood, against the mammalian default of weaning-time shutoff. The variant arose independently *at least four times* in pastoralist populations on two continents (Tishkoff SA, Reed FA, Ranciaro A, et al. 2007. "Convergent adaptation of human lactase persistence in Africa and Europe." *Nat Genet* 39:31–40; [DOI 10.1038/ng1946](https://www.nature.com/articles/ng1946); PMID 17159977). To read this story you need regulatory architecture (Ch 6), population genetics (Ch 10), the selective-sweep machinery (Ch 13), and the willingness to talk about ongoing human evolution.

Three cases. Three timescales. The Neanderthal genome runs at millions of years. The lactase variant runs at thousands. The carbapenem-resistant Klebsiella runs at weeks. The same mechanism stack operates in all three. *That is the synthesis this book has been building toward.* The chapter walks each case in turn, then returns at the end to read every prior chapter back into the cases.

## Three timescales, one stack

Before we open the cases, it's worth putting the organizing principle on the page. Evolution is not one phenomenon. It is the same machinery — mutation, drift, selection, gene flow — running at very different rates in very different contexts. The contexts differ in three ways that the rest of the chapter will keep returning to.

**Population size.** Bacteria live in populations of 10^9 to 10^12 cells per gram of stool, per liter of pond water, per square centimeter of skin. Modern humans live in a global population of about 8 billion, but the *effective population size* relevant to drift — the size of the idealized panmictic population that would have produced the patterns we see — is closer to 10^4. The difference between 10^9 and 10^4 is five orders of magnitude. Drift is feeble in bacteria and powerful in humans, even though census humans outnumber census bacteria of any one strain.

**Generation time.** Bacteria divide every 20 minutes to a few hours under good conditions. Humans reproduce roughly every 25 years. The ratio is roughly 10^6. A bacterial population can pass through 50 generations in a day. Human populations pass through 50 generations in 1,250 years.

**Mutation rate per generation.** Per base per generation, bacterial mutation rates are around 10^−10 (after the proofreading and mismatch repair you saw in Chapter 4). Per base per generation, human mutation rates are around 1.2 × 10^−8 (Kong A, Frigge ML, Masson G, et al. 2012. "Rate of de novo mutations and the importance of father's age to disease risk." *Nature* 488:471–475; [DOI 10.1038/nature11396](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature11396)). Bacteria are more accurate per base, but they have so many more chances per unit time that absolute mutation production rates in a bacterial infection vastly exceed those in a human pedigree.

When you multiply through, **bacteria explore sequence space about a million times faster than humans do per unit clock time, and the absolute number of new mutations entering a bacterial population per day is comparable to or larger than the number entering the entire human species per generation.** Evolution looks fast in bacteria because bacterial evolution *is* fast in any units you choose. The mechanism is identical. The clock runs differently.

This is the framing to hold. Now Case 1.

## Case 1 — Antibiotic resistance as rapid evolution

### The clinical face

A patient is admitted to a hospital intensive care unit with a urinary catheter that has been in place for ten days. She develops fever, hypotension, and a urine culture that grows *Klebsiella pneumoniae*. The antibiogram comes back the next morning: the organism is resistant to ampicillin, ceftriaxone, cefepime, piperacillin-tazobactam, ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, and — the one that decides the ICU team's night — *meropenem*. Meropenem is a carbapenem. Carbapenems are the antibiotics the team usually reaches for when other β-lactams fail. The lab also runs a molecular test and reports the strain carries *bla*NDM-1, the New Delhi metallo-β-lactamase. The therapeutic options remaining are colistin (toxic to kidneys), tigecycline (poor blood levels), and a few new agents like cefiderocol that may or may not be available on her hospital's formulary. Her risk of dying from this infection is, in published cohort studies, somewhere between 30 and 50 percent (Murray CJL, Ikuta KS, Sharara F, et al. 2022. "Global burden of bacterial antimicrobial resistance in 2019: a systematic analysis." *Lancet* 399:629–655; [DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(21)02724-0](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(21)02724-0/fulltext); PMID 35065702). The Murray paper estimates that bacterial antimicrobial resistance caused **1.27 million deaths attributable in 2019**, with 4.95 million associated deaths globally. That single year, drug-resistant bacterial infection killed more people than HIV/AIDS (~860,000) or malaria (~640,000).

The number does not lead this section. The mechanism leads. We open the bacterial side of the story by asking *what mutation* makes this Klebsiella resistant.

### A β-lactam, a transpeptidase, a hydrolyzing enzyme

A β-lactam antibiotic — penicillin, amoxicillin, ceftriaxone, meropenem — shares a four-membered ring fused to a five- or six-membered ring. The four-membered ring is the β-lactam. Its structure mimics the D-alanyl-D-alanine terminus of the peptide that bacterial **transpeptidases** (the penicillin-binding proteins, PBPs) recognize as they cross-link bacterial cell wall peptidoglycan. The PBP grabs the β-lactam thinking it is substrate, the ring opens, the active-site serine of the PBP gets acylated, and the enzyme is locked dead. Without functional PBPs, the bacterium cannot maintain its cell wall, and under osmotic stress it lyses. This is the textbook description of how penicillin kills *Streptococcus pneumoniae*. It works because the geometric mimicry is precise and because human cells do not build peptidoglycan.

Resistance happens, mechanistically, in a few canonical ways. Each is a worked example of a mutation type from Chapter 8.

**Point mutation in the PBP active site.** *Streptococcus pneumoniae* under penicillin pressure accumulates amino acid substitutions in PBP2x and PBP2b that lower the affinity of the active-site pocket for the antibiotic without breaking the enzyme's transpeptidase function. The pocket is just different enough that penicillin no longer fits well. Each substitution is a point mutation from Chapter 8; selection from Chapter 9 and 10 picks the ones that work.

**Acquisition of an altered PBP.** *Staphylococcus aureus* in the 1960s acquired *mecA*, a gene encoding PBP2a, an alternative transpeptidase with vastly reduced β-lactam affinity. PBP2a does the cell-wall cross-linking job that the methicillin-targeted native PBPs can no longer do under drug pressure. The strain becomes **MRSA** — methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*. The *mecA* gene sits on a mobile genetic element called SCC*mec* (staphylococcal cassette chromosome) that integrates into the chromosome at a defined site. *mecA* did not arise by point mutation of a *Staphylococcus aureus* PBP; it was *acquired* from somewhere else and inserted. Whoever donated it to *S. aureus* is still under investigation.

**Production of a β-lactamase.** A different strategy. Don't change the target — destroy the antibiotic before it reaches the target. The β-lactamases are enzymes that hydrolyze the β-lactam ring, opening it and inactivating the drug. Four families dominate clinically.

- **TEM** β-lactamases (named for the patient *Temoniera* in Athens from whom TEM-1 was first isolated in 1965) hydrolyze penicillins efficiently. Single amino acid substitutions — TEM-3 with E104K and G238S, for example — expand the substrate range to include extended-spectrum cephalosporins like cefotaxime and ceftazidime. Over 200 TEM variants are now catalogued. The TEM family is the clean textbook example of iterative adaptive evolution under repeated waves of selection. Each new generation of β-lactam antibiotic creates the pressure for the next active-site reshaping. Each TEM variant differs from the last by a handful of substitutions.
- **SHV** (sulfhydryl variable) β-lactamases follow a similar trajectory, originally chromosomal in *Klebsiella* and now disseminated on plasmids.
- **CTX-M** (cefotaximase, Munich) β-lactamases are the dominant extended-spectrum β-lactamases globally as of the 2020s. CTX-M genes originated as chromosomal *bla* genes in *Kluyvera* species — environmental bacteria — were mobilized by IS*Ecp1* insertion sequences onto plasmids, and have disseminated worldwide in what infectious-disease epidemiologists call the **CTX-M pandemic**. CTX-M-15 and CTX-M-14 are now found in *E. coli* causing community-acquired urinary tract infection on every continent. Each transfer event moved a chromosomal gene from one bacterium's genome into another bacterium's mobile DNA, where it could then jump again.
- **NDM-1** — New Delhi metallo-β-lactamase, first reported in 2009 — hydrolyzes carbapenems. Unlike TEM, SHV, and CTX-M, NDM-1 uses a *zinc* cofactor instead of an active-site serine, so the inhibitors that work on serine β-lactamases (clavulanate, tazobactam, avibactam) do not touch it. NDM-1 sits on plasmids, jumps between Enterobacteriaceae, and is now found globally. The patient in our opening case study is dying because of a gene whose name was invented within her lifetime.

You can read each of these mechanisms back to a chapter of this book. The point mutations in PBPs are Chapter 8. The single-base substitutions in TEM that expand substrate range are Chapter 8 plus the protein-folding logic from Chapter 5. The chromosomal-to-plasmid mobilization of *bla* genes is Chapter 7 (the engineering side of genome biology) writ at evolutionary scale. The acquisition of *mecA* on SCC*mec* is horizontal gene transfer, which we will come to in a moment.

### The selective sweep — math on the page

When a bacterial cell carrying a resistance allele finds itself in an antibiotic concentration that kills its susceptible neighbors, its **selection coefficient** is enormous. In Chapter 9 we defined *s* as the relative fitness advantage of one genotype over another. The susceptible cell, exposed to a bactericidal concentration of the drug, has fitness near zero. The resistant cell has fitness near one. So *s* — the advantage of resistant over susceptible — is close to 1. There are very few real-world selection coefficients this large outside the test tube; antibiotic exposure is among the strongest known.

In a population of effective size *N_e*, a beneficial allele that escapes initial drift loss reaches fixation in approximately:

```
T_fix ≈ (2/s) × ln(2 N_e)
```

For a bacterial gut population with *N_e* ≈ 10^9 and *s* = 1:

```
T_fix ≈ (2/1) × ln(2 × 10^9)
      = 2 × ln(2 × 10^9)
      = 2 × (ln 2 + 9 × ln 10)
      ≈ 2 × (0.69 + 9 × 2.30)
      ≈ 2 × 21.4
      ≈ 43 generations
```

At 30 minutes per bacterial generation under good conditions, **fixation in roughly 22 hours**. About one day. The susceptible strain that filled the patient's gut yesterday is replaced by a resistant strain today, *before her next dose of antibiotic has even worn off*. This is one of the few places in biology where evolution can be watched in real time without specialized equipment. A clinician with a Petri dish and a course of antibiotics is running a selective sweep on the floor of her own ward.

Real sweeps are messier than this calculation suggests. Selection coefficient is not constant — it depends on local drug concentration, which varies in space (urine has different antibiotic levels than blood or tissue) and time (peak versus trough levels). Resistance alleles often arise on plasmids whose copy number affects fitness in complex ways. Bacterial populations are structured (biofilms, microniches, host compartments) rather than panmictic. But the order of magnitude — hours to days for clinically observable resistance emergence — is correct, and it is the right number to anchor a student's intuition.

For comparison: the human worked example we will run in Case 2 has *s* ≈ 0.05 and *N_e* ≈ 10^4. Then `T_fix ≈ (2/0.05) × ln(2 × 10^4) ≈ 40 × 9.9 ≈ 400 generations`. At 25 years per human generation, that is **10,000 years**. The lactase persistence sweep ran for roughly that long. *The same equation, the same machinery, six orders of magnitude apart on the time axis.* Bacteria and humans are running the same algorithm; only the parameters differ.

### Horizontal gene transfer — gene flow without reproduction

Chapter 10 introduced gene flow as movement of alleles between populations through migration and reproduction. The Mendelian framework, going all the way back to Chapter 1, assumes alleles move only by way of meiosis and fertilization — through gametes, between individuals of compatible species. **Bacteria do not respect this framework.**

Bacterial cells exchange DNA by three mechanisms, and one of them is the dominant route by which antibiotic resistance spreads.

**Transformation.** A bacterium takes up naked DNA from its environment. Many species are naturally **competent** under specific conditions (*Streptococcus pneumoniae* during specific growth phases; *Neisseria* species; *Bacillus subtilis* under starvation). When a bacterium releases DNA by lysis, a nearby competent cell may pick it up and incorporate it into its chromosome by homologous recombination. The 1928 Griffith experiment that first hinted DNA was the molecule of inheritance — virulent pneumococcus killing mice while heat-killed virulent + live avirulent strains *together* killed mice — was a transformation experiment. Avery and colleagues identified the "transforming principle" as DNA in 1944. Transformation is the slowest and least efficient of the three transfer mechanisms in most bacteria but has been clinically important for *Streptococcus pneumoniae* penicillin resistance, in which competent pneumococci pick up PBP gene fragments from other *Streptococcus* species in the throat.

**Transduction.** A bacteriophage — a virus that infects bacteria — packages a fragment of its host's DNA by mistake during replication and carries it to the next bacterium it infects. The new bacterium incorporates the DNA into its chromosome. Bacteriophages are everywhere; in some marine ecosystems there are roughly 10^7 phages per milliliter of seawater. Phage-mediated transduction is the major route by which *Staphylococcus aureus* exchanges DNA, including some resistance genes.

**Conjugation.** Direct cell-to-cell transfer through a protein tube called a **pilus**, mediated by a plasmid that encodes the transfer machinery. One bacterium (the donor, carrying an **F plasmid** or a related conjugative element) extends a pilus, contacts a recipient cell, and threads a copy of the plasmid through into the recipient. The recipient becomes a donor for the next round. Plasmids can carry one resistance gene or many; an IncF plasmid family can carry *bla*CTX-M-15, aminoglycoside resistance, quinolone resistance determinants, and a handful of stress-response genes all on one mobile element. *One transfer event can move resistance to five antibiotic classes simultaneously.* Conjugation is the dominant clinical route for Gram-negative resistance dissemination.

The framing point I want to make stick: **conjugation is gene flow without reproduction**. A new bacterium does not have to be born for the resistance gene to spread; it spreads through the population by direct DNA transfer between living cells. This breaks the Mendelian framework you spent Chapters 1 through 3 building, because that framework assumes alleles move only with gametes. In bacterial population genetics, alleles can move *between species* — *E. coli* and *Klebsiella pneumoniae* can swap plasmids in your colon while you read this — without anyone reproducing. The Mendelian framework is a special case of a more general framework in which information moves by multiple routes. We did not name this until Chapter 14 because we needed the Mendelian framework first.

In computational terms, horizontal gene transfer turns the population's genealogy from a tree into a network. The standard phylogenetic methods from Chapter 12 assume a tree; when HGT is rampant, parts of the genome have different histories from other parts. Bacterial phylogenetics solves this by reconstructing trees from the **core genome** — the genes present in all strains, which are typically vertically inherited — and treating the **accessory genome** (plasmid-borne genes, integrated phages, transposable elements) as separately mobile.

### Fitness cost and compensatory mutation — a toy model

Why doesn't resistance "go away" once antibiotic use stops? Many resistance mutations carry a fitness cost in the *absence* of the antibiotic. PBP2a in MRSA cross-links less efficiently than the native PBPs do; methicillin-resistant *S. aureus* grows slower in antibiotic-free medium than methicillin-susceptible *S. aureus*. Rifampicin-resistant *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* with the canonical *rpoB* S531L substitution has a slower-acting RNA polymerase. The intuition the student should have is that the antibiotic-target enzymes evolved over hundreds of millions of years to be efficient at their jobs; almost any change that protects them from the drug also makes them worse at the job.

Here is the toy model that lets you reason about this. Suppose the resistance allele R has fitness 0.8 in the absence of antibiotic — a 20 percent cost. A second mutation, the **compensatory mutation** C, arises somewhere else in the genome. C has fitness 1.0 on the R background (it fully restores the cost of R) but fitness 0.9 on the wild-type background (it is mildly deleterious by itself). This is **epistasis** — the same compensatory mutation has different fitness consequences depending on the background it sits on.

In an antibiotic-free environment, the joint genotype R+C has fitness 1.0. The compensated resistant strain has lost its cost. It is now indistinguishable from wild-type in terms of growth rate. The wild-type itself (no R, no C) still has fitness 1.0. But the wild-type-on-compensated-background — that is, C without R — has fitness 0.9, because we said so. **And there is no force returning the population to wild-type R**, because R+C is not at a disadvantage anywhere relative to wild-type-without-C, and the C allele has already drifted to some frequency. The compensated resistant strain is stable indefinitely (Andersson DI, Hughes D. 2010. "Antibiotic resistance and its cost: is it possible to reverse resistance?" *Nat Rev Microbiol* 8:260–271; [DOI 10.1038/nrmicro2319](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrmicro2319)).

Empirically, this is what experiments find. Björkman, Andersson, Hughes, and others have evolved compensated resistant strains in the laboratory and shown them to be stable across hundreds of generations of antibiotic-free growth. Clinically, this is why withdrawing antibiotic use rarely leads to disappearance of resistant strains — the resistance is locked in by compensation, and selection no longer cares.

### Stewardship as evolutionary management

The closing move of Case 1 is conceptual. Antibiotic stewardship — the clinical practice of restricting antibiotic use to cases that actually need it, using the narrowest spectrum that works, treating for the shortest effective duration, rotating drug classes to avoid sustained selection on any one mechanism — is **evolutionary management**. It is the same logic that fishery managers use to avoid overfishing, that integrated pest management uses against pesticide resistance in agriculture, that conservation biologists use against rapid adaptation in introduced species. The metaphor is *antibiotics as a non-renewable resource*. Each time we use one, we accelerate the evolution of resistance to it. The total useful lifetime of penicillin, methicillin, ceftriaxone, or meropenem in clinical medicine is set by how aggressively we use it.

This is not a moral claim. It is a *mechanistic* claim, traceable through every concept this case has used — point mutation produces variants (Ch 8), selection acts on variants under drug pressure (Chs 9, 10), variants sweep to fixation in short time (Ch 10's selective-sweep math), horizontal gene transfer disseminates them (Ch 10's gene flow, generalized), compensatory mutations stabilize them (Ch 10's epistasis), and the global resistance burden is the sum of these processes operating in every patient's gut, every farm animal's gut, every wastewater stream, and every cell of every infection ever treated. The mechanism is the chapter. The chapter is the policy.

### A worked example embedded — sickle cell, end to end

I want to embed the chapter's worked example here, because sickle cell threads together everything Case 2 will need before we get there. Hold onto the antibiotic resistance framing for a moment — we will return to it in the closing synthesis — and walk a different rapid-evolution case that runs at human rather than bacterial scale.

The HbS allele is a single-nucleotide substitution at codon 6 of the β-globin gene HBB. The mRNA codon changes from GAG (glutamic acid) to GTG (valine). One A becomes one T in the DNA template (Chapter 4); the messenger RNA changes one letter (Chapter 5); a different tRNA is loaded onto the codon during translation (Chapter 5); the resulting β-globin protein has valine instead of glutamic acid at position 6. The β-globin chain in your textbook from Chapter 5 has glutamic acid there — a negatively charged hydrophilic residue on the surface of the folded hemoglobin tetramer. The HbS chain has valine — a hydrophobic residue. *This is a one-residue change, in a 146-residue protein, that turns a surface charge into a sticky hydrophobic patch.*

In the deoxygenated T-state hemoglobin, the valine of one tetramer fits into a complementary hydrophobic pocket on a neighboring tetramer. The tetramers polymerize into long fibers. The fibers distort the red blood cell from a flexible biconcave disc into a rigid sickle shape. Sickled cells clog small vessels, cause pain crises, damage organs, and shorten lifespan in the homozygous HbS/HbS state. The molecular logic is, in retrospect, unusually clean — one substitution, one protein, one fiber formation, one cellular phenotype. The first protein-level diagnosis of any human disease was Linus Pauling's 1949 demonstration that HbS migrates differently from HbA on electrophoresis (Pauling L, Itano HA, Singer SJ, Wells IC. 1949. "Sickle cell anemia, a molecular disease." *Science* 110:543–548; [DOI 10.1126/science.110.2865.543](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.110.2865.543)). This is where the phrase **molecular disease** comes from.

The genetics. HbA/HbS heterozygotes are clinically nearly normal — they have some HbS in their red cells but not enough to polymerize under typical oxygen tensions. They also have a substantial protection against severe *Plasmodium falciparum* malaria. The Allison 1954 paper from East Africa established the heterozygote advantage (Allison AC. 1954. "Protection afforded by sickle-cell trait against subtertian malarial infection." *BMJ* 1:290–294). The mechanism is not fully nailed down even now, but the leading account is that parasitized HbAS red cells are more readily removed by the spleen, parasite growth is impaired in the partially-deoxygenated environment of HbAS cells, and possibly HbS hemoglobin produces species of oxidized hemoglobin that the parasite cannot handle.

The population genetics. From Chapter 10's Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium under selection:

- Heterozygote fitness `w_AS = 1` (maximum)
- Homozygous-normal fitness `w_AA = 1 − t`, where *t* is the malaria cost
- Homozygous-sickle fitness `w_SS = 1 − s`, where *s* is the disease cost

At equilibrium, the HbS allele frequency is:

```
p_S* = t / (s + t)
```

This is the standard balanced-polymorphism formula from Chapter 10 (and from Wright 1932 and from a hundred textbooks since). In historical West African malaria-endemic populations, with `t ≈ 0.1` (a 10% malaria mortality penalty for non-carriers) and `s ≈ 0.5` (sickle-cell-disease severely reduces fitness in homozygotes without modern medical care):

```
p_S* = 0.1 / (0.5 + 0.1) = 0.167
```

Roughly one in six β-globin alleles is HbS. In the Piel et al. 2010 *Nature Communications* global map of HbS frequency, much of West and Central Africa shows allele frequencies in the 0.08–0.16 range — close to the theoretical equilibrium under historical malaria selection (Piel FB, Patil AP, Howes RE, et al. 2010. "Global distribution of the sickle cell gene and geographical confluence with malaria." *Nat Commun* 1:104; [DOI 10.1038/ncomms1104](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms1104)). In African Americans, after roughly 15 generations of relaxed *t* (no malaria in North America since the 19th century), the allele frequency has dropped to about 0.04 — predictable from selection-relaxation arithmetic over 350 years.

The phylogenetic and molecular-evolution part. The HbS allele has arisen *at least five times independently* across geographically separated malaria-endemic populations. The five named haplotype backgrounds — Bantu (Central Africa), Benin (West Africa), Senegal (West Africa), Cameroon, and Arab-Indian (Arabian Peninsula and Indian subcontinent) — share the same single-nucleotide A→T substitution at HBB codon 6 but sit on different surrounding chromosomal contexts. The same point mutation, the same phenotype, the same selection pressure, arrived independently in different populations. This is convergent evolution at the level of *identical mutations* in *separate populations* — about as clean a case as exists.

The translational medicine. Victoria Gray, who opened Chapter 7 of this book, received the first FDA-approved CRISPR therapy for sickle cell disease in 2023 (exa-cel, brand-named Casgevy). Cas9 ribonucleoprotein was electroporated into her bone marrow stem cells *ex vivo*; the guide RNA directed Cas9 to disrupt the BCL11A erythroid-enhancer; her edited stem cells were reinfused; her erythroid lineage now expresses fetal hemoglobin (HbF) at levels sufficient to outcompete HbS polymerization. The mutation she carried is a one-base substitution in a Chapter 5 gene; the genetics of her disease is Chapter 10; the population genetics that made her allele common is Chapter 10 and the convergent-evolution story is Chapter 13; the engineering that cured her is Chapter 7. Every chapter of this book was needed to take Pauling's 1949 paper to Gray's 2023 treatment.

This is the worked example I want you to carry. The student who can walk sickle cell from codon 6 through allele frequency through global geography through ancestral selection pressure to a CRISPR therapy is doing the integration this book has been training. The same exercise is what each Final Project option will ask you to do for one trait of your choice.

## Case 2 — Human population genetics

### Out of Africa, with a bottleneck

Every present-day non-African human population descends from a small group that left Africa roughly 60,000 to 70,000 years ago. This is the consensus picture as of 2025. The evidence is genomic and is unambiguous in its main outlines, though continuously refined in detail.

The genomic signal is striking and easy to state. At any given polymorphic site in the human genome, **the alleles present in non-African populations are a subset of the alleles present in African populations**. Africans carry essentially every variant non-Africans carry, plus many more that non-Africans never had. Genome-wide heterozygosity — the probability that two randomly drawn chromosomes at a random site carry different alleles — is highest in African populations and decreases approximately monotonically with geographic distance from East Africa, reaching minima in Native American and remote Pacific Island populations (Li JZ, Absher DM, Tang H, et al. 2008. "Worldwide human relationships inferred from genome-wide patterns of variation." *Science* 319:1100–1104; [DOI 10.1126/science.1153717](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1153717); PMID 18292342).

This is the **serial-founder pattern**. As the ancestral non-African population dispersed across Eurasia, each new founding subpopulation carried only a subset of the genetic diversity of its parent population. Each sub-founding lost a bit more variation. The cumulative effect is that distance-from-Africa correlates negatively with within-population genetic diversity. This is one of the cleanest demographic signals in any species' population genetics, and the only reasonable explanation for it is what Chapter 11 introduced as **founder effects** operating serially over the course of human dispersal.

The bottleneck number that gets quoted — "1,000 effective individuals" — is an *effective* population size, not a census. The Schiffels and Durbin 2014 *Nature Genetics* analysis of 34 whole genomes from nine populations using the multiple sequentially Markovian coalescent (MSMC) method inferred non-African effective population sizes in the few thousands during the relevant period (Schiffels S, Durbin R. 2014. "Inferring human population size and separation history from multiple genome sequences." *Nat Genet* 46:919–925; [DOI 10.1038/ng.3015](https://www.nature.com/articles/ng.3015)). *N_e* in the thousands tells you about how drift behaved; the actual census number of humans who left Africa was probably substantially larger but with population structure that lowered the effective number.

A clean way to put the same point: **drift was the dominant evolutionary force on the lineage that became non-Africans for the few generations after the dispersal**. Mutation, selection, and gene flow continued to operate, but the small effective population size meant drift was loud. Many alleles whose frequencies in non-Africans differ from their frequencies in Africans differ because of drift, not because of selection. Confounding drift with selection is one of the most common interpretive errors in human population genetics, and Chapter 10 spent considerable space on Hardy-Weinberg under drift specifically to give you the tools to avoid it.

### Founder effects in named populations

Founder effects are not a hypothetical category. They are observed at high resolution in specific populations.

**Ashkenazi Jews.** Carrier frequencies for several lysosomal storage disorders are dramatically elevated. **Tay-Sachs disease** (autosomal recessive, caused by mutations in *HEXA*) has a carrier frequency of about 1 in 30 in Ashkenazi populations compared to roughly 1 in 300 in non-Jewish populations of European descent. **Gaucher disease** (autosomal recessive, *GBA1*) has a carrier frequency of about 1 in 10–15 in Ashkenazim. **Bloom syndrome**, **Canavan disease**, **familial dysautonomia**, and **Niemann-Pick disease type A** all show similarly elevated carrier rates. Slatkin's analysis of the demographic pattern dates the relevant bottleneck to roughly the period 1100–1400 CE, with a small number of founders (perhaps a few hundred to a few thousand) followed by endogamy (Slatkin M. 2004. "A population-genetic test of founder effects and implications for Ashkenazi Jewish diseases." *Am J Hum Genet* 75:282–293; [DOI 10.1086/423146](https://www.cell.com/ajhg/fulltext/S0002-9297(07)63905-X)). Whether some of these alleles have been further amplified by heterozygote advantage remains debated; the dominant explanation remains genetic drift after a bottleneck.

**Old Order Amish of Lancaster County, Pennsylvania.** **Ellis-van Creveld syndrome** (autosomal recessive, *EVC* gene mutations) produces a chondrodysplasia with short-limbed dwarfism, polydactyly, dental anomalies, and congenital heart defects. The disease occurs at a frequency of about 1 in 200 in this Amish community versus 1 in 60,000 in general populations — roughly 300-fold elevation. The whole Old Order Amish community of Pennsylvania descends from about 200 founders in the 18th century, and a single ancestral couple (Samuel King and his wife) carried the EVC mutation that disseminated. This is the canonical clean case of founder effect by genealogy — the disease frequency can be traced to a specific historical marriage.

**Finnish disease heritage.** Finland has roughly 40 specific rare recessive diseases that occur at frequencies elevated by 10× to 100× over their global rates. *Aspartylglucosaminuria*, *congenital nephrotic syndrome of the Finnish type* (caused by mutations in *NPHS1*), *Salla disease*, and *Northern epilepsy* are characteristic Finnish-heritage conditions. The demographic explanation is two bottlenecks — initial Finnish settlement around 2,000 years ago, and an internal late-medieval bottleneck during which a small Karelian and Savonian subpopulation expanded to colonize the interior. Population geneticists studying Finland have one of the cleanest pedigrees-meet-genome combinations available, and the patterns are textbook.

The teaching move is the same in all three cases. **Drift is not a punishment for inbreeding.** It is a statistical fact about small samples. When a small number of individuals are the ancestors of a large later population, the random sample of alleles they happened to carry becomes the allele pool from which everyone descends. Some recessive disease alleles end up at elevated frequency just by chance. The phenomenon is not a moral judgment about any group; it is a *measurement* about a specific demographic history. The Amish are not "more inbred" in some pejorative sense; the founder couple who started them carried specific alleles, and those alleles are now in the gene pool at higher frequency than they are anywhere else on Earth.

### Recent selection in human populations

Drift is not the only force in recent human evolution. Several alleles show unambiguous signatures of strong positive selection within the past 10,000 to 20,000 years.

**Lactase persistence.** Mammals stop expressing lactase after weaning. This is the ancestral mammalian condition and is still the condition of most humans worldwide — adult lactose intolerance is the default, not the exception. In some populations, however, a regulatory variant keeps the *LCT* gene transcribed throughout life, allowing adults to digest milk. The European-derived T allele at position −13910 upstream of *LCT* (technically in an intron of *MCM6* that acts as an *LCT* enhancer) is at high frequency in northern European populations, dropping with latitude and reaching low frequency in Mediterranean and Middle Eastern populations.

The selective signal is among the strongest known for any recent human adaptation. Bersaglieri et al. 2004 found extended haplotype homozygosity around the variant consistent with a hard sweep within the last 5,000–10,000 years (Bersaglieri T, Sabeti PC, Patterson N, et al. 2004. "Genetic signatures of strong recent positive selection at the lactase gene." *Am J Hum Genet* 74:1111–1120). The estimated selection coefficient is in the range 0.05–0.10 — substantially stronger than most documented human selection signals.

Tishkoff et al. 2007 made the story richer. In African pastoralist populations — Maasai, Beja, Tutsi, others — lactase persistence is also present at high frequency, but it is *not* conferred by the European −13910T variant. It is conferred by *three different SNPs* on different surrounding haplotypes: G/C-14010, T/G-13915, and C/G-13907, all in the same enhancer region of *MCM6* (Tishkoff SA, Reed FA, Ranciaro A, et al. 2007. "Convergent adaptation of human lactase persistence in Africa and Europe." *Nat Genet* 39:31–40; [DOI 10.1038/ng1946](https://www.nature.com/articles/ng1946)). At least four independent mutations in the same regulatory element produced the same biochemical outcome (adult lactase expression) under similar selective pressure (cattle pastoralism), in geographically separated populations.

This is one of the cleanest cases of **convergent evolution at a regulatory element** in any organism. The Chapter 13 stickleback story has a vertebrate parallel here. The protein-coding sequence of lactase did not need to change; what changed is the regulatory machinery deciding when *LCT* gets transcribed. The same module is the target of selection in multiple populations because regulatory modularity makes it the available target.

A note on what drove the selection. The naive reading is "people who could digest milk got more nutrition, so they had more babies, so the allele swept." A more careful reading — emerging in 2023 papers — is that the relationship between lactase persistence and milk consumption is more complex than expected. Milk processing (cheese, yogurt, fermented dairy) reduces lactose load enough that lactose-intolerant adults can consume processed dairy without severe symptoms. The selection may have been intensified during famines (when fresh milk became disproportionately important) and during periods of high pathogen exposure (when dairy was a relatively safe water alternative). The big-picture story (recent strong selection on this enhancer in pastoralist populations) is solid; the proximate driver is still being refined (Evershed RP, Davey Smith G, Roffet-Salque M, et al. 2022. "Dairying, diseases and the evolution of lactase persistence in Europe." *Nature* 608:336–345; [DOI 10.1038/s41586-022-05010-7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-022-05010-7)).

The arithmetic for completeness. Suppose the lactase persistence allele began at frequency 0.01 in a small pastoralist population about 7,500 years ago, with selection coefficient *s* = 0.07 (favoring carriers). Per generation, the allele frequency change under selection from Chapter 10 is approximately:

```
Δp ≈ s · p · (1 − p)
```

At early frequencies (p ≈ 0.01), Δp ≈ 0.07 × 0.01 × 0.99 ≈ 0.0007 per generation — slow but positive. Compound this over 300 generations (7,500 years / 25 years/gen). The allele rises into the regime where the (1−p) term starts to matter, then approaches fixation asymptotically. The expected trajectory takes the allele from ~0.01 to ~0.7 in roughly 250–350 generations under these parameters. The European haplotype reached its current frequency (~0.7–0.8 in northern Europe) in roughly that time window. The arithmetic is consistent with what genomic dating gives.

**Skin pigmentation.** Constitutive skin pigmentation reflects the balance between two opposing UV-related pressures. UV degrades folate, which is essential for DNA synthesis and especially important during early embryonic development; therefore higher UV environments favor darker skin (more melanin protects folate). UV is also required for vitamin D synthesis in the skin; therefore lower UV environments favor lighter skin (less melanin permits more vitamin D production). This is the classical Loomis 1967 / Jablonski-Chaplin 2000 framing.

The genetics is polygenic. More than 100 loci contribute to the variation, and a handful have large effects. **SLC24A5** is the cleanest example: a single substitution (rs1426654, encoding the A111T variant) is near-fixed in European populations and near-zero in African populations, and it accounts for 25–38% of the European-African skin reflectance difference at that locus. The European sweep dates to roughly 11,000–19,000 years ago — well after the initial peopling of Europe ~45,000 years ago — consistent with selection arising as agricultural populations dispersed into northern latitudes where vitamin D pressure intensified. Crawford et al. 2017 identified **MFSD12** as a major-effect locus on dark skin in African populations, with patterns suggesting strong selection for darker pigmentation in some African lineages (Crawford NG, Kelly DE, Hansen MEB, et al. 2017. "Loci associated with skin pigmentation identified in African populations." *Science* 358:eaan8433; [DOI 10.1126/science.aan8433](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aan8433); PMID 29025994).

The teaching point: skin color in humans **does not map cleanly onto continental ancestry**. South Asian populations have light skin via partly different genetic architecture than Europeans. Dark-skinned populations exist on multiple continents. The trait is convergent — the same phenotype, evolved independently, via different genetic routes — rather than monophyletic. Treating skin color as a categorical race marker is, at minimum, bad biology.

**High-altitude adaptation.** Three human populations have independently evolved adaptations to chronic high-altitude hypoxia — Tibetans on the Qinghai-Tibetan plateau, Andean populations (Aymara and Quechua) in the Andes, and Ethiopian highlanders. Each population shows a distinct physiological strategy. Andean populations have elevated hemoglobin concentration (more red blood cells per unit blood); Tibetans have *lower* hemoglobin than lowlanders but more efficient peripheral oxygen utilization; Ethiopians have a third strategy involving different loci. Same selective pressure (atmospheric oxygen partial pressure ~60% of sea level at 4,000 m), three convergent solutions.

The Tibetan story is the cleanest. Yi et al. 2010 and Beall et al. 2010 identified **EPAS1** (encoding HIF-2α, a hypoxia-induced transcription factor) as the major locus under selection. Huerta-Sánchez et al. 2014 then showed something remarkable: the Tibetan EPAS1 haplotype was *introgressed from Denisovans* (Huerta-Sánchez E, Jin X, Asan, et al. 2014. "Altitude adaptation in Tibetans caused by introgression of Denisovan-like DNA." *Nature* 512:194–197; [DOI 10.1038/nature13408](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature13408); PMID 25043035). **Introgression**, here, means the movement of alleles from one species or population into another by hybridization, followed by selection retaining the introduced allele. The ancestors of modern Tibetans interbred with Denisovans somewhere in central Asia tens of thousands of years ago. The Denisovan EPAS1 variant was advantageous at altitude. Selection retained it. Today, ~80% of Tibetan EPAS1 haplotypes carry this archaic variant; it is rare in most other human populations. *We solve a problem (high-altitude hypoxia) using a tool (a specific transcription factor variant) we got from an extinct cousin species who already had it.*

This is the kind of result that ten years ago would have seemed implausible. It is now standard population genetics.

**Malaria resistance.** Already worked above with sickle cell. The point worth adding here is that malaria has driven multiple independent adaptations in human populations beyond HbS. **G6PD deficiency** (X-linked, multiple variant alleles) confers partial protection in hemizygous males. **α-thalassemia** and **β-thalassemia** are widespread in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian, and Southeast Asian populations and confer heterozygote-state protection against malaria at the cost of homozygous-state anemia. **Hemoglobin C** is another β-globin variant common in West Africa with malaria-protective effects. **Duffy antigen negativity** (a regulatory mutation that eliminates DARC expression on red cells) is at near-fixation in sub-Saharan African populations and protects against *Plasmodium vivax* infection (vivax requires DARC for red-cell invasion). The pattern is the same in all five: malaria as selecting agent, different mutations producing protection in different populations, often with significant homozygote costs maintaining the polymorphism via balancing selection. Convergent evolution to the same pathogen, several molecular routes.

### GWAS and the complex-trait era

Most human phenotypes are not single-gene. Height, body mass index, blood pressure, cholesterol levels, age at menarche, educational attainment, propensity to type 2 diabetes — every common complex trait depends on hundreds or thousands of variants, each with small effect. **Genome-wide association studies (GWAS)** have mapped this landscape at remarkable resolution.

Height is the cleanest case. The Yengo et al. 2022 *Nature* paper meta-analyzed GWAS data from 5.4 million participants and identified 12,111 genome-wide-significant variants clustered into 7,209 non-overlapping genomic segments covering ~21% of the genome (Yengo L, Vedantam S, Marouli E, et al. 2022. "A saturated map of common genetic variants associated with human height." *Nature* 610:704–712; [DOI 10.1038/s41586-022-05275-y](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-022-05275-y); PMID 36224396). The variants together explain about 40% of the heritable variance in height in European-ancestry samples, and 10–20% in non-European samples. The authors describe their map as "saturated" — increasing the sample size further in European populations is not expected to identify many new variants of detectable effect, because nearly all such variants have already been found.

Twin studies estimate height heritability at ~0.8. GWAS explains ~0.4. The gap — **missing heritability** — has been one of the most discussed problems in human genetics. Candidate explanations include rare variants too rare for current GWAS power, gene-by-gene and gene-by-environment interactions that inflate twin-study estimates, assortative mating (people partner with people of similar height more often than chance), and population stratification confounding twin-study estimates of heritability. The most likely answer is "all of these contribute in some proportion."

The polygenic-vs-omnigenic debate is the current methodological foreground. Boyle, Li, and Pritchard 2017 *Cell* proposed an **omnigenic** model in which essentially every gene expressed in the relevant tissue contributes to a complex trait, with a small number of **core genes** of larger effect and a large background of **peripheral genes** each contributing tiny amounts through interconnected regulatory networks (Boyle EA, Li YI, Pritchard JK. 2017. "An expanded view of complex traits: from polygenic to omnigenic." *Cell* 169:1177–1186; [DOI 10.1016/j.cell.2017.05.038](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(17)30629-3)). The Yengo 2022 data are more consistent with a highly polygenic but not omnigenic architecture for height, but the debate is technical and the answers may differ across traits.

**Polygenic risk scores (PRS)** sum effect sizes across GWAS-identified variants weighted by an individual's allele counts. PRS is now in clinical use for some conditions, including coronary artery disease risk stratification and breast cancer risk assessment. The major caveat for any clinical application is **portability across ancestries**: a PRS trained on European-ancestry GWAS data loses 30–80% of its predictive power in African-ancestry individuals (Martin AR, Kanai M, Kamatani Y, et al. 2019. "Clinical use of current polygenic risk scores may exacerbate health disparities." *Nat Genet* 51:584–591; [DOI 10.1038/s41588-019-0379-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-019-0379-x)). Recent 2023 work has reframed the portability problem as a *continuous* decline in PRS accuracy with genetic distance from the training population, rather than as a discrete cross-ancestry boundary (Ding Y, Hou K, Xu Z, et al. 2023. "Polygenic scoring accuracy varies across the genetic ancestry continuum." *Nature* 618:774–781; [DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-06079-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06079-4)).

The portability problem matters for two reasons. Scientifically, most GWAS have been done in European-ancestry samples — a sampling bias of the field, not a property of the underlying biology — and the result is that PRS performance reflects who the training data came from. Clinically, PRS that performs unequally across ancestries will create new health disparities if deployed without correction. The All of Us research program and the H3Africa initiative are explicitly designed to address this sampling gap.

### Race, ancestry, and population structure — the careful framing

This is the part of the chapter where students arrive with strong priors from outside the course. The teaching has to do two things at once, and both have to be done.

**Population structure is real and detectable.** Principal components analysis of human whole-genome data produces clusters that correlate with continental geographic origin. The 1000 Genomes Project, the Human Genome Diversity Panel, the Bergström et al. 2020 *Science* analysis of 929 diverse genomes (Bergström A, McCarthy SA, Hui R, et al. 2020. "Insights into human genetic variation and population history from 929 diverse genomes." *Science* 367:eaay5012; [DOI 10.1126/science.aay5012](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aay5012)) — all show detectable continental structure. Allele frequencies differ on average across populations. For some specific medically important variants, the differences are clinically substantial. Sickle-cell carrier frequencies differ by an order of magnitude between West African and Northern European populations. The APOL1 G1 and G2 variants associated with chronic kidney disease are present at >20% frequency in West African populations and near zero in non-Africans, and they are clinically relevant for predicting kidney disease risk and for kidney transplant outcomes. CYP2C19 *17 (rapid metabolizer) and *2 (slow metabolizer) alleles vary by ancestry and affect clopidogrel dosing for cardiovascular prevention. Warfarin maintenance dose varies by ancestry through both CYP2C9 and VKORC1 allele frequency differences. **Population structure is medically real.**

**Race as a social category does not map cleanly onto biological clusters.** Lewontin 1972 — using 17 blood-group and protein-marker loci, the best data available at the time — found that approximately **85% of human genetic variation is within populations**, about 8% is between populations within a "race," and about 6% is between major continental groups (Lewontin RC. 1972. "The apportionment of human diversity." *Evol Biol* 6:381–398). Modern whole-genome data confirm Lewontin's basic apportionment. Human autosomal FST between major continental groups is in the range 0.10–0.15, depending on how populations are grouped and which markers are used. **FST** — the fixation index — is the standard measure of how much genetic variance is between groups versus within groups; FST = 0 means no between-group differentiation, FST = 1 means complete differentiation with no within-group variation. Human FST of 0.10–0.15 is *low* compared to most species; it means 85–90% of human autosomal variation is *within* populations, only 10–15% is *between* populations. Knowing a person's continental population assignment tells you very little about their specific genotype, because most variation lives within rather than between populations.

These two claims are *not in tension*. Population structure is detectable, and most variation is within populations. Both are true. The first claim does not collapse into the second; the second does not erase the first.

The synthesis that the field has converged on:
- Humans cluster genetically.
- The clusters are *continuous* rather than discrete — gradients of allele frequency vary smoothly across geography rather than jumping at categorical boundaries.
- The clusters correlate *imperfectly* with social race categories — self-reported race in U.S. medical data correlates with continental ancestry estimation but with substantial slippage. The "African American" label, for example, hides genomes that range from 60% West African ancestry to 95% West African ancestry, with substantial European and sometimes Native American admixture.
- Any specific genetic variant's frequency in a population is a *statistical statement about a distribution*, not a categorical statement about an individual. Saying "the APOL1 G1 variant is at 22% frequency in West African populations" tells you a population-level fact, not a verdict on any individual.
- **Race is a social category. Ancestry is a continuous genetic gradient. Population structure is a biological phenomenon.** The three are related — they correlate with each other — but they are *not the same thing*. The clinical move increasingly being made is to replace race as a proxy for ancestry with direct ancestry estimation from genetic data.

A few facts to anchor:
- The notion of three to five discrete continental "races" was a 19th-century European typology imposed on biological variation that does not actually partition that way. Modern genetics shows clines and partial structure, not discrete races.
- "African Americans" as a label hides enormous genetic diversity. Typical genomes contain 75–85% West African ancestry, 15–25% European ancestry, with substantial variance — and African ancestry itself is heterogeneous (different West African populations contributed differently to the trans-Atlantic slave trade).
- The clinical use of self-reported race as a proxy for biological ancestry is a *crude approximation* and is increasingly being replaced by direct ancestry estimation. The 2021 NEJM editorial movement away from race-adjusted glomerular filtration rate estimation is a clean example of the field correcting itself.

**The failure modes** the chapter must avoid are two, and they are symmetric.

- **Biological essentialism** — the assumption that races are natural kinds with distinct genetic essences, that some races are "more advanced" than others, that races map cleanly onto cognitive or behavioral traits. Population genetics does not support any of this. Human FST is too low and the clines are too continuous for race-as-essence to work. The history of "scientific" racial typology is a history of pseudoscience used to justify political projects.
- **Total denial of structure** — the assumption that all human variation is uniformly distributed, that no allele frequency differences exist between populations, that ancestry has no medical relevance. The data refute this; allele frequencies do differ; some of those differences are clinically actionable.

The honest middle is where the data are. **Population structure exists and is medically relevant; race is a social construct that approximately tracks some ancestry signals but is not itself a biological natural kind; the same person can belong to multiple ancestry clusters simultaneously; statistical statements about population-level allele frequencies do not determine individual genotype.** This is the synthesis Reich's 2018 book argues for; it is also the synthesis that the 2018 open letter from Coop and 67 co-signatories pushed back on in specific places while agreeing with the basic biology. Both are useful for the educated lay reader. The field, in its careful version, broadly endorses the middle position.

What the chapter does not pretend: it does not pretend to settle every contested detail. It does name what is settled (FST values, the bottleneck pattern, the specific clinical relevance of named variants) and what is contested (the relative role of selection versus drift in producing specific allele-frequency differences, the proper handling of ancestry in clinical algorithms, the use of polygenic risk scores in cross-ancestry settings).

## Case 3 — The molecular basis of human evolution

### How much of the genome differs

Humans and chimpanzees share approximately **98.8% of protein-coding DNA** in alignable regions — a number that has been quoted for decades and is approximately right (Chimpanzee Sequencing and Analysis Consortium. 2005. "Initial sequence of the chimpanzee genome and comparison with the human genome." *Nature* 437:69–87; [DOI 10.1038/nature04072](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature04072)). Single-nucleotide differences in alignable coding regions are roughly 1.2%. Indels (insertions and deletions) account for another ~3% of the genome. Lineage-specific duplications and large structural variants account for several more percent. The most inclusive measure of sequence divergence between humans and chimps is roughly 4–5%, depending on what is counted. The often-quoted 98.5% or 99% identity is correct for what it measures (single-nucleotide identity in alignable coding regions) and misleading as a summary of total genomic difference.

But the more important point: very few of the human-chimp coding differences are obviously functional. Most amino acid changes between the two species are neutral or nearly so under the dN/dS framework you developed in Chapter 13. The interesting differences — the ones that likely matter for human-specific traits — are largely **regulatory** rather than coding. King and Wilson 1975 *Science* proposed exactly this in their three-page paper "Evolution at two levels in humans and chimpanzees" (King MC, Wilson AC. 1975. *Science* 188:107–116; [DOI 10.1126/science.1090005](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1090005)). They had no genome, three rough measures of protein divergence between humans and chimps, and one logical inference: if protein-coding sequences are this similar but the two species are this different, then *most evolution must be in regulatory DNA*. Fifty years of subsequent data have largely vindicated the inference. The stickleback story from Chapter 13 and the lactase story from this chapter are both demonstrations of regulatory evolution at work. King and Wilson saw the principle before the tools to verify it existed.

### Human Accelerated Regions [verify, several specific claims contested]

Pollard et al. 2006 *Nature* and Pollard et al. 2006 *PLoS Genetics* (back-to-back papers in the same year by the same group) introduced the concept of **Human Accelerated Regions (HARs)** — genomic regions that are highly conserved across vertebrates (slow molecular clock, indicating purifying selection or strong functional constraint) but show accelerated substitution rates on the human lineage since the human-chimp split (Pollard KS, Salama SR, Lambert N, et al. 2006. "An RNA gene expressed during cortical development evolved rapidly in humans." *Nature* 443:167–172; [DOI 10.1038/nature05113](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature05113); PMID 16915236).

The strategy is clean. First, find regions that have been deeply conserved across vertebrates — they have functions that purifying selection has kept stable for hundreds of millions of years. Then, among those, find regions where the substitution rate on the human branch is much higher than the conservation pattern predicts. Such regions are candidates for functional changes specific to the human lineage. The 2006 *PLoS Genetics* paper identified 202 such regions. Subsequent work has expanded the catalog to ~3,000 elements.

**HAR1** is the most accelerated of the original set. A 118-bp region containing part of a non-coding RNA gene called *HAR1F*, expressed specifically in Cajal-Retzius neurons in the developing human neocortex between gestational weeks 7 and 19 — a window critical for cortical layering. HAR1 has 18 substitutions on the human lineage versus only 2 on the chimp lineage; under neutral evolution at the genome-wide rate, the expected number across this region would be about 0.27. The substitution rate is on the order of 7× faster than under neutrality. The function of *HAR1F* is incompletely characterized — it appears to be a non-coding RNA that interacts with reelin signaling and cortical neuronal migration, but the precise molecular mechanism is still being worked out [verify]. Subsequent work (Reilly SK, Yin J, Ayoub AE, et al. 2015. "Evolutionary genomics. Evolutionary changes in promoter and enhancer activity during human corticogenesis." *Science* 347:1155–1159) has shown that many HARs function as **developmental enhancers** in the developing human cortex.

**HACNS1** [contested]. Prabhakar et al. 2008 *Science* identified HACNS1, a 546-bp conserved element with 16 human-specific accelerated substitutions, and used transgenic mouse reporter assays to show that the human version drives expression in the developing limb (specifically the anterior wrist and proximal thumb region) while the chimp and rhesus orthologs do not (Prabhakar S, Visel A, Akiyama JA, et al. 2008. "Human-specific gain of function in a developmental enhancer." *Science* 321:1346–1350; [DOI 10.1126/science.1159974](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1159974); PMID 18772437). The natural reading is that this is a regulatory change underlying the evolution of the human opposable thumb.

The result has been challenged. Sumiyama and Saitou 2011 *Molecular Biology and Evolution* raised concerns that some of the apparent "gain of function" may reflect *loss of a repressor element* rather than gain of activity. **Biased gene conversion** — a meiotic recombination phenomenon that preferentially fixes GC-to-AT transitions in certain genomic regions — can also mimic accelerated substitution patterns without invoking positive selection. Subsequent work (Aldea et al. and others, 2020 onward) has partly supported the original functional readout in humanized mice but with more modest effects than the 2008 paper reported. HACNS1 is still a useful pedagogical example — the best-characterized HAR with a functional reporter readout — but it should be presented with caveats. **The interpretation of HAR substitutions as positively selected is not always straightforward; biased gene conversion can produce similar signatures.** This is the kind of methodological caution Chapter 13 trained.

### FOXP2 — language gene, mostly not [contested]

The textbook story, dating from Enard et al. 2002 *Nature*: FOXP2 is a transcription factor; the human protein has two amino acid substitutions in exon 7 compared to the chimp protein; the KE family in London, with severe speech and language deficits, was found to carry a FOXP2 mutation; the Enard 2002 paper estimated a selective sweep on FOXP2 within the past 200,000 years and proposed this as a key event in modern human language evolution (Enard W, Przeworski M, Fisher SE, et al. 2002. "Molecular evolution of FOXP2, a gene involved in speech and language." *Nature* 418:869–872; [DOI 10.1038/nature01025](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature01025)). The story was widely retold as "FOXP2 is the language gene that evolved 200,000 years ago in modern humans."

Three subsequent findings have rewritten this, and the chapter has to walk all three.

**Neanderthals share the human FOXP2 substitutions.** Krause et al. 2007 *Current Biology* recovered FOXP2 sequence from a Neanderthal specimen and found that *Neanderthals carry both of the derived "human-specific" amino acid substitutions* (Krause J, Lalueza-Fox C, Orlando L, et al. 2007. "The derived FOXP2 variant of modern humans was shared with Neandertals." *Curr Biol* 17:1908–1912; [DOI 10.1016/j.cub.2007.10.008](https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(07)01680-9)). The substitutions therefore predate the human-Neanderthal split — they are at least 500,000–700,000 years old, not 200,000 years old. They are not specific to modern humans.

**The selection signal does not survive larger samples.** Atkinson et al. 2018 *Cell* re-examined the population genetics of FOXP2 in a much larger and more diverse human sample, with updated demographic models, and concluded that the apparent selection signal from Enard 2002 does not survive (Atkinson EG, Audesse AJ, Palacios JA, et al. 2018. "No evidence for recent selection at FOXP2 among diverse human populations." *Cell* 174:1424–1435.e15; [DOI 10.1016/j.cell.2018.06.048](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(18)30740-2)). The original signal was probably a demographic artifact of limited population sampling in 2002. **The "FOXP2 was under recent positive selection in modern humans" claim is not robust.**

**FOXP2 is a transcription factor with many downstream targets.** Disruption produces severe speech and orofacial motor deficits — the KE family phenotype — but not loss of language *per se*. Calling FOXP2 "the language gene" is shorthand at best and misleading at worst. Language is not a single trait controlled by a single gene; it involves a distributed cognitive infrastructure with neural, anatomical, and developmental components.

The chapter's framing: FOXP2 is a useful example of *how the field corrects itself*. The 2002 result was foundational. It got large media attention. Subsequent data have revised the original interpretation in two specific ways (the substitutions predate modern humans; the selection signal weakens with better data). This is the Feynman move — show the methodology, name what was learned, name what the original interpretation got wrong, name what the field now thinks. The student should come away with the dN/dS calculation from Chapter 13 still working as a tool, and with a calibrated sense that "X is the gene for Y" claims in human evolution should be greeted with skepticism, especially when X is a transcription factor and Y is a complex cognitive trait. Both are usually wrong.

### Brain evolution: ASPM, MCPH1, and NOTCH2NL [partially contested]

**ASPM and MCPH1** are the **microcephaly genes**. Mutations in either cause autosomal recessive primary microcephaly — a substantially reduced brain size in newborns, often with intellectual disability, with the rest of the body grossly normal. Both genes encode proteins involved in mitotic spindle orientation during neural progenitor cell division. Both genes show signatures of positive selection on the primate lineage. Both have been proposed as drivers of brain-size evolution in hominins.

The 2005 Mekel-Bobrov et al. and Evans et al. papers in *Science* extended this to a stronger claim — that ASPM and MCPH1 underwent recent positive selection within the past few thousand years in modern human populations (Mekel-Bobrov N, Gilbert SL, Evans PD, et al. 2005. "Ongoing adaptive evolution of ASPM, a brain size determinant in Homo sapiens." *Science* 309:1720–1722; Evans PD, Gilbert SL, Mekel-Bobrov N, et al. 2005. "Microcephalin, a gene regulating brain size, continues to evolve adaptively in humans." *Science* 309:1717–1720). The framing — "humans got smarter in the last few thousand years" — generated substantial attention. **The framing has not held up under scrutiny.** Subsequent work has been mixed on whether the recent-selection signal is robust in either gene; the recent-selection claims are contested, while adaptive evolution at these loci across the broader primate lineage (over millions of years) remains well-supported. The chapter's honest line: ancient primate selection at ASPM and MCPH1 is solid; very recent selection in modern humans at these loci is contested and probably overstated.

**NOTCH2NL** is the clearest recent story, and the cleanest example for the chapter to use. Fiddes et al. 2018 *Cell* and Suzuki et al. 2018 *Cell* (back-to-back companion papers) characterized a set of human-specific paralogs — **NOTCH2NLA, NOTCH2NLB, NOTCH2NLC** — that arose by partial duplication and gene conversion from the parental NOTCH2 gene in the hominin lineage roughly 3–4 million years ago (Fiddes IT, Lodewijk GA, Mooring M, et al. 2018. "Human-specific NOTCH2NL genes affect Notch signaling and cortical neurogenesis." *Cell* 173:1356–1369.e22; [DOI 10.1016/j.cell.2018.03.067](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(18)30383-0); PMID 29856954). NOTCH2NL paralogs are expressed in **radial glia** in the developing human neocortex; they delay neural progenitor cell differentiation, prolong the proliferative phase, and increase total neuron output. Knockdown in human cortical organoids reduces neuron output.

The remarkable feature is that **the same chromosomal instability that enabled the human-specific innovation continues to generate disease today**. The NOTCH2NL locus at 1q21.1 is unstable: recurrent deletions cause microcephaly and increased risk of schizophrenia and seizures; recurrent duplications cause macrocephaly and increased risk of autism. The duplication architecture that made the locus available for evolutionary innovation also makes it fragile in present-day individuals. *The same molecular event runs in both directions — adaptive innovation 3 million years ago, disease syndrome now*. This is the cleanest case in human evolutionary genetics of an evolutionary innovation continuing to produce disease through the chromosomal architecture that made the innovation possible. [verify additional details]

**SRGAP2C** is a second-best example. Charrier et al. 2012 and Dennis et al. 2012 (back-to-back in *Cell*) characterized SRGAP2C, a human-specific paralog of SRGAP2 that arose by partial duplication ~2.4 million years ago. SRGAP2C acts as a dominant-negative against the ancestral SRGAP2A, slowing dendritic spine maturation and increasing spine density in cortical neurons. Mouse experiments expressing SRGAP2C produce "humanized" dendritic morphology. This is a single duplication event producing a measurable change in neuronal architecture.

The pattern across NOTCH2NL, SRGAP2C, and several less-characterized cases: **gene duplication in the hominin lineage produced human-specific paralogs that altered neural development**. Gene duplication, which Chapter 13 introduced as one route to evolutionary novelty, is at the center of the human-specific neurodevelopmental story. The 2R whole-genome duplications you read about in Chapter 13 expanded vertebrate Hox clusters. Smaller-scale partial duplications expanded the hominin neural toolkit. Same mechanism, different scale, different timescale.

### Ancient DNA — Pääbo's Nobel-winning work

In October 2022, the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to **Svante Pääbo** "for his discoveries concerning the genomes of extinct hominins and human evolution." The Nobel committee cited his establishment of paleogenomics as a discipline (Nobel Prize Committee. 2022. "[Press release: Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine 2022](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/2022/press-release/)"). This is the only Nobel in genetics that recognizes a body of work all of which has happened during the careers of people currently teaching biology. Pääbo's first Neanderthal DNA recovery was in 1997 (the mitochondrial genome). The draft Neanderthal nuclear genome was published in 2010. The Denisovan genome was published the same year. **Ancient DNA** — the recovery and sequencing of DNA from biological remains tens to hundreds of thousands of years old — became a productive discipline in roughly two decades.

The key achievements:

**The Neanderthal mitochondrial genome, 1997.** Krings, Pääbo, and colleagues recovered ~378 bp of the mtDNA hypervariable region 1 from a Neanderthal specimen (Krings M, Stone A, Schmitz RW, et al. 1997. "Neandertal DNA sequences and the origin of modern humans." *Cell* 90:19–30). This was proof of principle that ancient DNA recovery was feasible at all. The mtDNA was clearly different from modern human mtDNA, placing Neanderthals on a separate matrilineal lineage.

**The draft Neanderthal genome, 2010.** Green et al. 2010 *Science* published the ~3 Gbp draft assembly from three Neanderthal individuals at Vindija Cave, Croatia, at ~1.3× coverage (Green RE, Krause J, Briggs AW, et al. 2010; cited above). Comparison with the genomes of present-day humans from various populations revealed the central biological finding: **non-African humans carry roughly 1–4% Neanderthal ancestry, distributed across the genome in detectable haplotype blocks.** Africans carry essentially zero Neanderthal ancestry. The interbreeding occurred shortly after the out-of-Africa dispersal, probably in the Middle East, between ~50,000 and 60,000 years ago.

The arithmetic for the introgression-percentage claim: in a non-African human individual, the proportion *f* of the genome inherited from Neanderthal ancestors can be estimated from the relative frequencies of Neanderthal-derived versus African-derived alleles at sites where the two ancestral populations differ. Detailed methods like the **D-statistic** (also called the ABBA-BABA test) compare patterns across four populations to test for excess sharing of derived alleles. For a single Eurasian individual, summing across all detectable Neanderthal haplotype blocks gives roughly 1–4% of the genome — call it 2% on average. *No single individual carries 100% of the Neanderthal genome*; each carries a different ~2% subset. Pooled across many non-African individuals, however, roughly 40% of the Neanderthal genome can be reconstructed from present-day humans (Sankararaman S, Mallick S, Dannemann M, et al. 2014. "The genomic landscape of Neanderthal ancestry in present-day humans." *Nature* 507:354–357; [DOI 10.1038/nature12961](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature12961)).

**The Denisovan genome, 2010.** Reich et al. 2010 *Nature* sequenced a high-coverage genome from a single tiny finger bone fragment from Denisova Cave in Siberia and identified an *entirely new hominin population* previously unknown from fossil morphology (Reich D, Green RE, Kircher M, et al. 2010. "Genetic history of an archaic hominin group from Denisova Cave in Siberia." *Nature* 468:1053–1060; [DOI 10.1038/nature09710](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature09710); PMID 21179161). Denisovans contributed an average of ~5% ancestry to present-day Melanesians and Australian Aboriginal populations, with smaller fractions in East Asians and South Asians, and near-zero contribution to most other populations. The Denisovan discovery is one of the cleanest cases in evolutionary biology where *genetics revealed a population the fossil record did not know existed*.

**High-coverage Neanderthal and Denisovan genomes** — Prüfer 2014, Meyer 2012 — provided diploid-quality data enabling fine-grained population-genetic analysis of admixture timing, magnitude, and adaptive consequences.

The synthesis: modern non-African humans carry detectable archaic-hominin ancestry from Neanderthals and (in Pacific populations) Denisovans. The introgression occurred during a window of overlap and interbreeding shortly after the out-of-Africa dispersal. **Speciation is not always reproductively complete.** The biological species concept from Chapter 11, applied to Neanderthals and modern humans, gives the wrong answer if applied strictly — Neanderthals and modern humans were geographically and morphologically distinct, but they interbred and the offspring were fertile enough to leave detectable ancestry today. They were "species" in the morphological and probably the behavioral sense; they were not fully reproductively isolated. The species concept stretches at the edges, and the Pääbo data are the cleanest case in mammals where it stretches.

Selected adaptive introgression cases:
- **EPAS1 in Tibetans** — Denisovan haplotype confers high-altitude adaptation, already worked above.
- **HLA class I alleles** — some introgressed from Neanderthals/Denisovans, broadening the human immune-recognition repertoire (Abi-Rached L, Jobin MJ, Kulkarni S, et al. 2011. "The shape of modern human immune-defense systems modified by multiregional admixture with archaic humans." *Science* 334:89–94).
- **Keratin loci** — Neanderthal-introgressed variants in skin and hair-follicle genes, possibly involved in cold-climate adaptation.
- **TLR (toll-like receptor) loci** — Neanderthal-introgressed variants tuning innate immune responses to specific pathogen classes (Dannemann M, Andrés AM, Kelso J. 2016; Deschamps M, Laval G, Fagny M, et al. 2016).

The depleted regions are equally informative. Neanderthal ancestry is **depleted on the X chromosome** and **depleted near genes** in present-day humans, consistent with weak but pervasive purifying selection against Neanderthal-derived alleles after introgression. Some Neanderthal variants were retained (the adaptive cases); many more were probably weakly deleterious and gradually selected out over the past 50,000 years.

There is also evidence for *modern human → Neanderthal* gene flow at earlier dates (~200,000 years ago), meaning the interbreeding was bidirectional and earlier than the canonical 50,000-year window (Kuhlwilm M, Gronau I, Hubisz MJ, et al. 2016. "Ancient gene flow from early modern humans into Eastern Neanderthals." *Nature* 530:429–433). The picture is, increasingly, of an interconnected web of hominin populations exchanging genes intermittently over a few hundred thousand years rather than three cleanly separated species.

### Recent developments worth flagging

**The Human Pangenome Reference**, Liao et al. 2023 *Nature*, published a draft pangenome incorporating sequences from 47 individuals of diverse ancestries, adding 119 million base pairs of polymorphic sequence and 1,115 gene duplications over the GRCh38 single-reference (Liao WW, Asri M, Ebler J, et al. 2023. "A draft human pangenome reference." *Nature* 617:312–324; [DOI 10.1038/s41586-023-05896-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-05896-x); PMID 37165242). The pangenome shifts the framing of genomics away from "one human genome" toward "the distribution of human genomes." Variants that are present in one population and absent in another no longer get systematically missed by alignment to a reference that comes from a single individual.

**Sediment DNA.** Slon et al. 2017 *Science* and follow-up work demonstrated that hominin DNA can be recovered from cave sediments without bones. The technique has opened a window onto species and time periods where fossils are absent and now reaches roughly 400,000 years back for some lineages.

**Ancient pathogen DNA.** Recovery of *Yersinia pestis* (plague) genomes from Bronze Age skeletons; *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* from pre-Columbian South American remains; ancient *Treponema pallidum* (syphilis) from medieval Europe; ancient *Hepatitis B* from various contexts. The same machinery that recovers human ancient DNA recovers ancient pathogen DNA, and the patterns of co-evolution between humans and their pathogens over the past 10,000 years are now becoming readable.

The big-picture point: **paleogenomics is becoming a normal subfield of evolutionary biology**, with experimental methods, interpretive frameworks, and surprises at the rate of any productive field. The Pääbo Nobel is, in this sense, an early recognition of a discipline whose major findings are still being made.

## Common misconceptions across the three cases

The integrative version of the misconception list from earlier chapters, pruned to the most-likely confusions students will arrive with by chapter 14.

**"Race is genetic."** Population structure is real; race as currently used socially does not map cleanly onto biological clusters; allele frequencies differ on average across populations but within-population variation almost always exceeds between-population variation; FST values for human populations are *low* compared to most species. The honest middle is the right place to stand. Section 2 above worked this in detail.

**"Antibiotic resistance evolves only in clinical settings."** Wrong. Environmental antibiotic concentrations from agricultural runoff, hospital effluent, and pharmaceutical manufacturing maintain resistance reservoirs (Berendonk TU, Manaia CM, Merlin C, et al. 2015. "Tackling antibiotic resistance: the environmental framework." *Nat Rev Microbiol* 13:310–317). Roughly 70% of antibiotic use globally is in agriculture. The selection pressure is everywhere.

**"Humans have stopped evolving."** Wrong. Multiple genome-wide selection scans (Voight et al. 2006; Sabeti et al. 2007; Field et al. 2016) identify dozens of regions under recent positive selection in human populations. Lactase persistence, skin pigmentation, malaria resistance, high-altitude adaptation, immune loci — all show sweeps within the past 10,000–20,000 years. Selection on humans is ongoing.

**"Neanderthals were primitive."** Wrong on several counts. Neanderthal brains were on average *larger* than modern human brains (differently shaped, more elongated). Neanderthals used complex stone tools (Mousterian industry), made composite tools, used fire and pigments, buried their dead in some cases, produced symbolic objects, and contributed detectable ancestry to all present-day non-African human populations. The framing "primitive vs. advanced" is anachronistic and wrong; the cognitive differences from modern humans, if any, were almost certainly narrower than that framing suggests.

**"Mutation is rare and slow."** True per base per generation (~10^−8 in humans); false in absolute terms. Each newborn human carries an average of 60–70 new mutations not present in either parent. In a global population of 8 billion, every possible single-base mutation in the genome occurs many times every generation.

**"GWAS tells you which gene causes a trait."** Misleading. GWAS hits are statistical associations between common SNPs and traits. The SNP is usually in linkage disequilibrium with a nearby causal variant, which may not be in a coding region or may not even be in the gene that the SNP "points to." Mapping GWAS hits to causal genes requires functional follow-up.

**"Polygenic risk scores are deterministic."** Wrong. PRS produces probability distributions, not certainties. A high PRS for CAD does not guarantee a heart attack; a low PRS does not preclude one. Environment, behavior, rare variants, and chance all matter. PRS portability across ancestries adds another layer of uncertainty.

**"The 'language gene' FOXP2 evolved 200,000 years ago in modern humans."** Both halves are wrong, as worked above. Neanderthals carry the substitutions, and the original Enard 2002 selection signal does not survive in larger samples.

**"Genetic distance equals racial distance."** No. Two individuals from the same continental population share on average ~99.9% of their genome; two individuals from different continents share ~99.85%. The absolute genetic distance among all humans is small in evolutionary terms — much smaller than within most other primate species.

**"Humans and chimpanzees are 98.8% identical, so we are basically the same."** Wrong on the meaning of the number. The 98.8% refers to single-nucleotide identity in alignable protein-coding regions. Including indels and structural variants raises total divergence to 4–5%. And — more importantly — small percentage differences in regulatory DNA produce large morphological and cognitive differences, exactly because of the regulatory-evolution mechanisms Chapter 13 worked through. *Most of what makes us different from chimps is not in the small set of coding differences.* It is in the regulatory elaboration that the small percentages hide.

### Exercise 1 — The selective sweep on a hospital floor

A new strain of *Klebsiella pneumoniae* carrying NDM-1 on a plasmid arrives at a hospital ICU on day 1, in one colonized patient. The ICU has 20 beds. Each patient receives a course of meropenem during their ICU stay. Each bed has an average occupancy of 5 days. (b) Using `T_fix ≈ (2/s) × ln(2 N_e)` with *N_e* = 10^9 (intestinal carriage population per patient) and *s* = 1 (during meropenem exposure), compute the within-patient fixation time of NDM-1 over the susceptible Klebsiella the patient already carries.

(c) Argue from first principles why HGT (specifically, conjugative plasmid transfer to a different Enterobacteriaceae species in the patient's gut) accelerates dissemination beyond what selection alone would do.

(d) Propose two stewardship interventions that would slow the spread, and explain in evolutionary terms why each works.

**Goal:** Apply selective-sweep math to a clinically realistic scenario; integrate selection, HGT, and intervention strategy.

## What this book has been building toward

I am going to walk every prior chapter of this book and name the part of the capstone it made possible. The point is not to list the chapters — you have the list. The point is to show that the three cases above *required* each chapter's machinery, and that pulling any one out would break the explanation.

**Chapter 1 — Meiosis and the basis of inheritance.** Sutton and Boveri 1902 saw, before anyone had identified DNA, that chromosomes behave during meiosis the way Mendel's factors behave in pedigrees. That insight grounded every later result. Meiosis segregates alleles. Recombination shuffles them. The chromosomal architecture is the substrate on which everything else acts. In Case 3, the lengths of Neanderthal ancestry blocks in modern non-Africans tell us the date of admixture — because each generation, recombination breaks Neanderthal blocks shorter, and the surviving block length is a clock. The clock is the meiotic recombination rate. Without Chapter 1, the Pääbo paper is unreadable.

**Chapter 2 — Mendel's laws.** Mendel's clean 3:1 ratios are the statistical regularities that meiosis produces under random mating. Hardy-Weinberg, which we used throughout Case 2 to predict allele frequencies in human populations, is Mendel's framework extended to populations. The framework breaks when populations are non-random (assortative mating, founder effects); it breaks when populations have structure (different allele frequencies in different subpopulations); it breaks when selection is strong. *We needed it to know how it breaks.* The sickle-cell equilibrium frequency is calculated by extending Mendel through Hardy-Weinberg with non-equal fitnesses. Mendel's laws are the null model from which all of Case 2 deviates in named ways.

**Chapter 3 — Extensions of Mendelian inheritance.** Real biology breaks Mendel's clean cases. Penetrance is incomplete; alleles interact through epistasis; many traits are polygenic. Case 2's GWAS section requires this framing — height is polygenic, hundreds of variants, each of small effect, with substantial environmental and gene-by-environment contributions. The complex-trait architecture is the practical inheritance pattern for most medically interesting traits in humans, and you needed Chapter 3 to know that Mendelian thinking is the special case, not the general one.

**Chapter 4 — DNA structure, replication, and repair.** The molecular substrate. Mutations arise during replication and from chemical damage; the repair machinery catches most but not all. The mutation rate per base per generation in humans (~1.2 × 10^−8) and in bacteria (~10^−10) are the inputs to every selection calculation we did. The pyrimidine dimers from UV that skin pigmentation protects against are exactly the lesions Chapter 4's NER pathway repairs. The ancient DNA Pääbo's lab works with is dominated by cytosine-to-uracil deamination — a specific chemical damage that the lab has to model and correct for. Without Chapter 4, you cannot read an ancient genome and you cannot calibrate a mutation rate.

**Chapter 5 — Gene to protein.** The chain that gets selected on. Every gene we have discussed — *bla*TEM, FOXP2, EPAS1, HBB, LCT, NOTCH2NL — is a piece of Chapter 5 machinery whose function depends on the codon-by-codon decoding the chapter walked. The A→T at HBB codon 6 changes a codon, changes a tRNA, changes an amino acid, changes a protein surface, changes a quaternary structure, changes a cellular phenotype. Five steps in Chapter 5 connecting one base to one clinical outcome. The genetic code's universality is what makes mRNA vaccines work and what makes recombinant insulin work and what makes Casgevy work. Every translational therapy in this book lives in Chapter 5's substrate.

**Chapter 6 — Gene expression and regulation.** Why the same genome produces many cell types — and, in evolutionary terms, why most morphological evolution hides in regulatory DNA. Case 2's lactase persistence is a regulatory variant. Case 3's HAR1 is a non-coding RNA expressed in developing cortex through regulatory machinery. The stickleback pelvic spine in Chapter 13 was a regulatory deletion. The Casgevy therapy works by editing a regulatory enhancer (BCL11A in erythroid cells) to switch on fetal hemoglobin. *Most of what evolves in mammalian morphology is not what proteins are; it is when and where proteins are made.* That is Chapter 6 at evolutionary scale.

**Chapter 7 — Biotechnology and genomics.** The engineering side. We can now read genomes (next-generation sequencing at $200 per genome in 2024), write them (synthetic biology), edit them (CRISPR-Cas9, base editors, prime editors). The Pääbo Neanderthal genome was assembled with next-generation sequencing methods. The Murray AMR estimate was built from databases that required genomic surveillance of resistance genes. The Tishkoff identification of independent African lactase persistence variants required population-scale sequencing in pastoralist communities. Without Chapter 7's tools, none of the case studies of this chapter would be possible. The cost-per-genome curve is the rate-limiting step on what we can know about human and pathogen evolution.

**Chapter 8 — Mutation, DNA damage, and cancer genetics.** The bridge chapter, and the most important one for understanding why genetics and evolution are one subject. Mutation in the germline is evolutionary raw material — that is Case 2 and Case 3. Mutation in the soma is cancer — that was the cell book's Chapter 14 and this book's Chapter 8. The same molecular event has two fates depending on whether it happens in a germ cell or a somatic cell, and the same equations describe the dynamics. The clonal expansion math Chapter 8 walked for Barrett's esophagus is the same selective-sweep math we walked for antibiotic resistance in Case 1. Cancer is somatic evolution. *Cancer is genetics-and-evolution in microcosm, within one body, on one short timescale.* If you got Chapter 8, you got the bridge.

**Chapter 9 — Evolution and the origin of species.** The qualitative argument — natural selection's four prerequisites: variation, excess offspring, differential reproduction, time. Every case in this chapter requires those four prerequisites. The Daphne Major drought Chapter 9 walked is the same logic operating in a bacterial culture under meropenem (Case 1), in a pastoralist population on milk (Case 2), and in the hominin lineage acquiring NOTCH2NL paralogs (Case 3). Different timescales, same algorithm. The mode-of-selection vocabulary (directional, stabilizing, disruptive, balanced) we used throughout Case 2.

**Chapter 10 — Population genetics and natural selection.** The quantitative formalization. Hardy-Weinberg as the null. The five forces — mutation, selection, drift, gene flow, non-random mating — as the deviations. This is the chapter the capstone has used most heavily. Every allele frequency calculation in this chapter, every equilibrium prediction, every selective-sweep time estimate, every interpretation of population structure (FST) traces back to Chapter 10. The chapter built the quantitative apparatus; this chapter applied it to three real-world cases.

**Chapter 11 — Speciation.** How populations become species, the species concept and its limits. Case 3's Neanderthal-modern human admixture is the cleanest mammalian case where the biological species concept stretches at the edges. Two morphologically and behaviorally distinct lineages — Neanderthals and modern humans — were not fully reproductively isolated, interbred during a window of contact, and produced offspring whose descendants we are. The species concept is approximate; the genomic data are exact. The four named limits of the BSC that Chapter 11 walked (asexual, fossil, hybridizing, ring species) cover this case under "hybridizing." Speciation is a process, not always a completed event.

**Chapter 12 — Phylogenetics and the history of life.** How we reconstruct the history. Without phylogenetics, we cannot say when Neanderthals and modern humans diverged, when the human-chimp split happened, when NOTCH2NL duplicated. The molecular clock from Chapter 12 dates the events. The tree-building methods place the lineages. The Big Five mass extinctions and the deep timeline of life provide the context against which "6 million years ago for human-chimp" is meaningful. Ancient DNA pushes the resolvable tree back to specific specimens with specific dates — Pääbo's Vindija Neanderthals, the Denisova finger bone — and the dates *are* tree-building, applied to genomic data with sediment dating cross-checks.

**Chapter 13 — Molecular evolution and evo-devo.** The mechanism by which sequences change along the branches. Kimura's neutral theory tells us most substitutions are neutral; dN/dS lets us identify the ones that aren't. Selective sweeps carve diversity valleys around recently selected variants. Transposable elements get domesticated into placentas and immune systems. Gene duplication produces novel function. Hox-and-toolkit conservation plus enhancer modularity makes regulatory evolution the dominant mode of morphological change. King and Wilson 1975 vindicated. Every concept in this chapter has reappeared in the capstone. HAR1 is a regulatory element under accelerated evolution on the human branch. FOXP2 dN/dS is the methodological tool that Atkinson 2018 used to walk back the Enard 2002 claim. NOTCH2NL is a gene duplication in the hominin lineage. EPAS1 is a selective sweep — actually an introgressive sweep, a special case Chapter 13 did not name. The stickleback regulatory module that Chapter 13 opened with is mechanistically identical to the LCT enhancer module that Case 2 walked.

**This chapter (Ch 14) — capstone.** Three case studies that require every concept from the book. Antibiotic resistance compresses selection, gene flow, fitness costs, and epistasis into days. Human population genetics extends Hardy-Weinberg into a story about who we are and how we got here, with the race-and-structure framing handled carefully. Molecular human evolution applies regulatory evolution, gene duplication, and ancient DNA to one species — ours. The cases do not tie up neatly. They are three different timescales of the same machinery in action.

### What the book promises a student who finishes it

The promise is not "you know more facts." Facts are cheap. The internet has more facts than you can store. The promise is **you can do mechanistic reasoning across genetics and evolution simultaneously**. Specifically:

You can read a clinical genomic test report — a tumor mutation panel, a pharmacogenomic profile, a carrier screen — and explain what each variant means in terms of the gene-to-protein chain (Chapter 5), the regulatory architecture (Chapter 6), the population frequencies (Chapter 10), and the clinical implications (Chapters 7, 8, 14).

You can read a population-genetic FST analysis and interpret what it does and does not say about the populations being compared. You can hold the two-claims-at-once framing about race and structure without collapsing one into the other.

You can read an antibiogram on a sick patient and reason about *why this strain became resistant, when it probably emerged, and how it might have spread*. You can apply the selective-sweep math to estimate timescales. You can name the difference between fitness-cost-mediated reversion and compensatory-mutation-mediated persistence.

You can read a phylogenetic tree built from sequence data, understand what the branch lengths mean, identify where introgression complicates the tree-as-tree assumption, and connect a specific node on the tree to a specific evolutionary event (a duplication, a sweep, a speciation, a mass extinction).

You can read a paper claiming "positive selection on gene X in humans" with calibrated skepticism — asking the right methodological questions about demographic confounders, biased gene conversion, sample size, replication, and the difference between whole-gene and codon-specific selection signals.

That is what the book is for. The three cases of this chapter are the demonstration. The Final Project is your version of the demonstration. **A student who can do all of this is doing genetics and evolution simultaneously, as the one subject they actually are.**

The closing thought: every chapter of this book has been the same algorithm at a different scale. Mutation. Drift. Selection. Gene flow. The chapters are not separate subjects with different equations; they are different *applications* of one mechanism stack. The bacterial culture, the human pedigree, the hominin lineage are all running the same algorithm with different parameters. *Genetics is evolution one generation at a time. Evolution is genetics many generations at once.* That is the integrative claim. Hold it.

**What would change my mind.** The strong synthesis of this chapter — that mutation, drift, selection, and gene flow operating at different timescales on the same molecular substrate accounts for the three cases (antibiotic resistance, human population structure, molecular human evolution) — would be substantially revised if a major class of evolutionary phenomena emerged that did not reduce to those four forces. Two specific scenarios would force the revision. First, if **non-genetic inheritance** — DNA methylation, histone modification, RNA-mediated, behavioral, or cultural — were shown to account for a major fraction of heritable variance for fitness-relevant traits across multiple species over evolutionary timescales, the Mendelian-plus-population-genetics framework would need to absorb a transgenerational-epigenetic addendum that current frameworks treat as a special case rather than a general one. Some plant systems already require this; whether mammalian evolution does at significant scale remains contested. Second, if **horizontal gene transfer in eukaryotes** turned out to be substantially more common than current estimates suggest — if multicellular eukaryotes routinely exchanged DNA across species boundaries via mechanisms we have not yet characterized — the four-forces framework would need to absorb a fifth (or recognize that bacterial-style HGT is the general case and Mendelian inheritance the special case). The 2023 pangenome and the ongoing reanalysis of "junk DNA" hint at unexpected mobility; whether this rises to a major revision of the framework or remains marginal is an open empirical question. I currently do not believe either of these revisions is needed at the level the chapter operates. But the chapter's confidence is calibrated and revisable on evidence.

**Still puzzling.** Three things at the end of this book that I do not have a clean account for. First, why the rate of recent positive selection in human populations seems to be unusually high — humans appear to be under stronger selection in the last 10,000 years than any of our great-ape relatives have been over similar windows, and I do not have a fully satisfying account of why. The plausible candidates (rapid environmental change, agriculture, novel pathogens, climate variability) are all true and probably all relevant, but the cumulative magnitude is harder to pin down. Second, the relationship between *demographic history* and *adaptive history* in shaping current human variation — disentangling drift from selection is methodologically hard, and many published "selection signals" from the early 2000s have been revised toward "demographic artifact" with better demographic models. The right ratio of drift to selection in producing current allele-frequency variation across populations is still being worked out, and the answer may differ across categories of variants. Third, the deep question of how morphological and behavioral innovation in the hominin lineage was produced by what looks like a modest set of molecular changes (HARs, a handful of gene duplications, regulatory rewiring) — the proximate genetic story is becoming clearer (Chapter 13, this chapter), but the ultimate question of *why this specific configuration of changes produced this specific lineage* with this specific cognitive capacity remains, at the field's frontier, an open question. I expect the next decade of ancient DNA, single-cell genomics, and comparative neuroscience to clarify it. I do not expect to live to see it fully resolved.
