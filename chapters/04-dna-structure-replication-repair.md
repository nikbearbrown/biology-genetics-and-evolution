# Chapter 04 — DNA Structure, Replication, and Repair

**Suggested titles:**
- *The Molecule That Copies Itself, and the Five Ways It Fixes Its Mistakes*
- *Photo 51, Two Bands at the Bottom of a Centrifuge Tube, and the Cost of a Broken Repair Gene*
- *Why the Genome Lasts a Lifetime — Three Layers of Accuracy and the Cancers That Happen When One Layer Fails*

---

**TL;DR.** A human cell copies three billion base pairs every time it divides and gets it almost right. The "almost" matters in both directions. If copying were perfect, evolution would stop; if copying were sloppy by a factor of a thousand, no human would survive infancy. The actual error rate — about one mistake per billion base pairs per replication — is produced by three nested mechanisms acting on the same template: base-pair geometry inside DNA polymerase (~10⁻⁵), 3'→5' exonuclease proofreading (~10⁻⁷), and post-replication mismatch repair (~10⁻⁹ to 10⁻¹⁰). Damage that arrives later — depurination, deamination, oxidation, UV-induced thymine dimers, double-strand breaks — is cleaned up by four more specialized repair pathways: base excision repair (BER), nucleotide excision repair (NER), homologous recombination (HR), and non-homologous end joining (NHEJ). Each pathway is a story Mendel could not have told because he had no idea what his "factors" were chemically. This chapter walks the historical arc — Avery in 1944, Watson-Crick and Franklin in 1953, Meselson-Stahl in 1958, the 2015 chemistry Nobel for the three repair pathways — and traces, step by step, one successfully corrected error from misincorporation to ligation. The clinical anchor is what happens when a repair pathway is missing: xeroderma pigmentosum, Lynch syndrome, hereditary BRCA-driven breast and ovarian cancer. The conceptual anchor is that "mutation rate" is not a property of the polymerase alone — it is the product of an entire defended pipeline, and every defended pipeline can fail.

## A small boy who cannot go outside

Chicago, 2014. A pediatric dermatologist sees a four-year-old boy whose mother has wrapped him in long sleeves, gloves, a wide-brimmed hat, and a UV-blocking face visor before driving the three blocks from her parking spot to the clinic entrance. He has already had two basal cell carcinomas removed from his face. He has hyperpigmented freckling across every patch of skin that has ever been exposed to daylight, including the small triangle of forehead that catches sun when his visor lifts. The diagnosis is **xeroderma pigmentosum** — XP, for short. The estimated lifetime risk of skin cancer in an XP patient is on the order of a thousand to two thousand times the rate in the general population, with the median age of first non-melanoma skin cancer reported around eight years ([Bradford et al. 2011 *J Med Genet* 48:168–176](https://jmg.bmj.com/content/48/3/168) [verify]; for a clinical overview see [Lehmann et al. 2011 *Orphanet J Rare Dis* 6:70](https://ojrd.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1750-1172-6-70) [verify]). The boy's parents are running an arms race against the sun. Every window in their house is UV-filmed. He goes to a special school that schedules outdoor activities at night. The family is from Andhra Pradesh and only one branch of the extended family carries the mutation; the mother and father are second cousins, both heterozygous carriers of a defective copy of an *XP* gene — most commonly *XPA* or *XPC* in clinical series ([Lehmann et al. 2011](https://ojrd.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1750-1172-6-70) [verify]). The boy is homozygous. His cells cannot perform **nucleotide excision repair** — the pathway that ordinarily clips out bulky DNA lesions, including the pyrimidine dimers that ultraviolet photons create across his skin every time he steps outside.

Here is the part to sit with. Every person reading this sentence has been irradiated by ultraviolet light at some point today. Every UV photon that strikes a strand of DNA can cause a pair of adjacent thymines to fuse into a covalent **thymine dimer** — a small lump in the helix that no polymerase will read accurately. Nondefective people repair these dimers within hours. A boy with XP cannot. The dimers persist. The next time the cell divides, the polymerase stalls at the lesion, errors are introduced opposite the damage, mutations accumulate in tumor-suppressor genes, and skin cancers appear. Same UV exposure. Same DNA. Different repair status. Vastly different outcome.

This is the chapter's point in one paragraph. A genome's stability is not a property of DNA the molecule. It is a property of DNA *plus* the machinery that defends it. Take away one defender — one enzyme out of perhaps two hundred — and the same sun that gently freckles the rest of us becomes a carcinogen on a clock. The double helix Watson and Crick described in 1953 is half the story. The other half is what the cell does to repair the helix every minute of every day. We will spend this chapter building both halves, in the order biology had to figure them out.

## Twenty-four years to convince a field — Griffith, Avery, Hershey, and the wrong horse

Before 1944, most biochemists believed proteins carried genetic information. The reasoning was almost reasonable. Proteins are built from twenty amino acids; DNA is built from four nucleotides. Information density seems to favor the more diverse alphabet. Proteins fold into thousands of distinct shapes and catalyze nearly every reaction in the cell; DNA looked like a structural scaffold or a phosphate stockpile. The vote was for protein.

The vote was wrong. The chain of experiments that overturned it is worth walking through carefully because it is a small case study in how science actually changes its mind — not in one decisive paper but in three of them, spread across twenty-four years, each one nailing down an alternative the previous one had left open.

The first experiment was **Frederick Griffith's** in 1928, working at the British Ministry of Health on pneumonia. *Streptococcus pneumoniae* comes in two forms: a smooth-coated virulent strain (S) and a rough-coated avirulent strain (R). Inject live S into a mouse, the mouse dies. Inject live R, the mouse lives. Inject heat-killed S, the mouse lives — heat-killed bacteria are dead. Now mix heat-killed S with live R and inject the mixture. The mouse dies. From the dead mouse, Griffith recovered *live S bacteria*. Something had moved from the dead smooth cells into the living rough cells and *transformed* their phenotype. Griffith called it the "transforming principle" and did not know what it was ([Griffith 1928 *J Hyg* 27:113–159](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2167760/) [verify]).

The second experiment was **Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty's** in 1944, at the Rockefeller Institute in New York. They had spent more than a decade purifying the transforming principle and asking, chemically, what it was. Their strategy was elimination by enzyme. Take a cell extract that transforms R into S. Treat it with proteases — enzymes that destroy protein. Transformation still works. Treat it with ribonuclease — enzymes that destroy RNA. Transformation still works. Treat it with deoxyribonuclease — DNase, an enzyme that destroys DNA. Transformation stops. The transforming principle was DNA ([Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty 1944 *J Exp Med* 79:137–158](https://rupress.org/jem/article/79/2/137/2706/STUDIES-ON-THE-CHEMICAL-NATURE-OF-THE-SUBSTANCE)). The paper landed quietly. Many readers were not yet ready to accept that a molecule as monotonous as DNA could be the carrier of inherited variation. The field needed a second blow.

The third experiment was **Alfred Hershey and Martha Chase's** in 1952, at Cold Spring Harbor. They worked with **bacteriophage** — viruses that infect bacteria — specifically T2 phage, which is a tiny protein capsule containing a DNA payload. The phage docks on an *E. coli* cell, injects something into the cell, and twenty minutes later the cell bursts open releasing hundreds of new phage particles. The question was: what does the phage inject? If proteins carry genetic information, the phage must be injecting protein. If DNA carries it, the phage must be injecting DNA. Hershey and Chase labeled the protein coats of one batch of phage with ³⁵S (sulfur is in protein; not in DNA) and labeled the DNA cores of another batch with ³²P (phosphorus is in DNA's sugar-phosphate backbone; not in any of the standard amino acids). They let each batch infect bacteria, sheared the empty phage coats off the bacterial surface with a kitchen blender, centrifuged the bacteria into a pellet, and measured where the radioactivity went. ³²P went into the bacteria. ³⁵S stayed in the supernatant. The phage injected DNA, not protein ([Hershey and Chase 1952 *J Gen Physiol* 36:39–56](https://rupress.org/jgp/article/36/1/39/9831/INDEPENDENT-FUNCTIONS-OF-VIRAL-PROTEIN-AND-NUCLEIC) [verify]). That was the experiment that finished the argument.

Why did the field need three experiments? Because each ruled out something the previous one had not. Griffith showed that *something* could move heritable information between cells, but the something could have been protein, RNA, DNA, or a small molecule. Avery showed that the something was destroyed by DNase but not by protease — but skeptics could argue that the DNase preparation was contaminated with trace protease activity, or that DNA was a *carrier* of the real (protein) signal, not the signal itself. Hershey and Chase showed that protein and DNA could be separated *during the act of infection*, and only DNA crossed the membrane. The standard of evidence required to overturn a prevailing hypothesis in science is not "a single suggestive paper." It is "an alternative explanation we can name and a follow-up experiment that rules it out." That is what twenty-four years of work produced.

The lesson for this chapter is methodological as much as historical. Every claim I will make in the next twenty pages — about base pairing, about replication, about repair — rests on experiments that ruled out the alternatives. When I tell you that replication is semiconservative, I will tell you how Meselson and Stahl ruled out the other two models. When I tell you that mismatch repair distinguishes parent from daughter strand by methylation in *E. coli*, I will tell you what experiment showed that. The molecular biology of the second half of the twentieth century is, more than almost any other field, a worked example of Popper's demarcation: which models predict what, and what evidence would distinguish them.

## The double helix — geometry, complementarity, antiparallelism

In 1950, **Erwin Chargaff** at Columbia published a quantitative oddity. He had measured the base composition of DNA from many organisms — calf thymus, yeast, *E. coli*, human spleen — and found that, although the overall A+T versus G+C content varied substantially between species, two equalities held in every sample: **the percentage of adenine equals the percentage of thymine, and the percentage of guanine equals the percentage of cytosine** ([Chargaff 1950 *Experientia* 6:201–209](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02173653) [verify]). Why? Chargaff did not know. The equality was a clue without a model.

In April 1953, **James Watson** and **Francis Crick**, working at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, published a one-page paper in *Nature* proposing that DNA is a right-handed double helix in which two sugar-phosphate strands run antiparallel and the bases pair across the interior: adenine with thymine, guanine with cytosine ([Watson and Crick 1953 *Nature* 171:737–738](https://www.nature.com/articles/171737a0)). Their model had three sources. The first was Chargaff's rules — the equalities had to mean something, and complementary pairing meant them. The second was the **X-ray diffraction pattern of B-form DNA** — most famously **Photograph 51**, taken in late 1952 by **Rosalind Franklin** and her graduate student **Raymond Gosling** at King's College London, which showed the unmistakable cross-shaped diffraction signature of a helix and let Franklin measure the helical dimensions from the spacing of the spots ([Franklin and Gosling 1953 *Nature* 171:740–741](https://www.nature.com/articles/171740a0); the published companion paper to Watson and Crick's). The third was a careful working out of which base-pairing geometries actually fit — A-T pair flat, two hydrogen bonds, ~10.85 Å between sugar attachment points; G-C pair flat, three hydrogen bonds, the same ~10.85 Å between sugar attachment points. Any purine-pyrimidine pair has the right width; purine-purine would be too wide and pyrimidine-pyrimidine too narrow. The constancy of width is what makes the helix the same diameter regardless of sequence.

Let me unpack the structure carefully, because the geometry will return at every repair step.

**A nucleotide** has three parts: a phosphate group, a five-carbon sugar (2-deoxyribose), and a nitrogenous base. The bases come in two chemical families. **Purines** are larger, two-ring molecules — adenine (A) and guanine (G). **Pyrimidines** are smaller, one-ring molecules — thymine (T) and cytosine (C). (RNA replaces thymine with uracil, U, but DNA uses T. The reason matters later, when we get to base excision repair.) Each nucleotide is connected to the next by a **phosphodiester bond** between the 3'-OH of one sugar and the 5'-phosphate of the next, forming a directional chain. The chain has a 5' end (free phosphate) and a 3' end (free hydroxyl). This is why we read sequences as 5'-ATGCG-3' and not the other way around; direction is built into the chemistry.

A single strand of DNA is therefore an asymmetric polymer with two distinct ends. Two single strands wind around each other to form a **double helix**, but they do so in a specific orientation: the two strands run **antiparallel**, meaning one strand's 5'→3' direction points up the helix and the other strand's 5'→3' direction points down. This is not optional. The hydrogen-bonding geometry of A-T and G-C base pairs only works when the strands are antiparallel; parallel strands cannot stack the bases at the right angles. ("Antiparallel" is a word that does real work. If you ever find yourself reading a passage on DNA replication that uses it casually, slow down — the antiparallelism is what forces every awkward feature of the lagging strand.)

The base-pairing rules are specific and they are physically enforced by hydrogen-bond geometry. **Adenine pairs with thymine via two hydrogen bonds**: A donates one hydrogen, T donates one hydrogen, they meet in the middle. **Guanine pairs with cytosine via three hydrogen bonds**: G donates two hydrogens and accepts one, C donates one and accepts two. A purine never pairs with a purine (too wide for the helix to maintain its diameter); a pyrimidine never pairs with a pyrimidine (too narrow); and the specific hydrogen-bond donor-acceptor patterns mean A cannot pair with C (wrong geometry) and G cannot pair with T (wrong geometry). The four bases, two pairing rules: A=T and G≡C, where the lines count hydrogen bonds. (I will draw the bonds in the simulator. On paper, "=T" and "≡C" is a quick mnemonic for the bond counts.)

Three structural facts about the helix matter for everything that follows.

**Diameter ~2 nm.** Because every base pair is one purine plus one pyrimidine, every base pair has the same width. The helix is uniform in diameter regardless of which bases occupy which positions. This is why DNA can store *arbitrary* information — the physical shape does not depend on the sequence, so any sequence works.

**~10 base pairs per turn, ~3.4 nm per turn, ~0.34 nm rise per base pair.** The bases stack on top of each other in the interior of the helix, like coins in a stack. The stacking interaction (hydrophobic, plus aromatic-ring stacking) contributes most of the helix's thermodynamic stability — more, in fact, than the hydrogen bonds. The hydrogen bonds give specificity (which base pairs with which); the stacking gives stability (why the helix does not fall apart at body temperature).

**Major and minor grooves.** Because the two backbones are not exactly opposite each other on the helix, the spaces between them along the helical axis come in two sizes: a wider **major groove** (~2.2 nm) and a narrower **minor groove** (~1.2 nm). DNA-binding proteins — transcription factors, polymerases, repair enzymes — read the sequence by reaching their amino acid side chains into the major groove, where the chemical signatures of A-T versus G-C versus T-A versus C-G are distinguishable without melting the helix open. The grooves are what make sequence-specific binding possible.

There is one more thing to say about the discovery itself. **Rosalind Franklin's** Photograph 51 was the crystallographic data that confirmed B-form DNA was helical. Watson saw the photograph through her colleague Maurice Wilkins in early 1953, without Franklin's permission. He has acknowledged this in interviews and in his memoir; Franklin died of ovarian cancer in 1958 at age 37, four years before the 1962 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins (the Nobel is not awarded posthumously, so Franklin would in any case not have shared the prize, but the question of attribution within the field has been re-examined repeatedly since). The historical record is more complicated than the textbook version usually suggests, and the credit Franklin did not receive in her lifetime is part of why I name her here. For a careful reconstruction see Brenda Maddox's *Rosalind Franklin: The Dark Lady of DNA* (HarperCollins, 2002).

## Why the structure is also the copying mechanism — and how Meselson and Stahl proved it

Watson and Crick's 1953 paper ended with one of the most famously understated sentences in twentieth-century biology: "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material." Here is what they meant.

If the two strands of the double helix are complementary — every A paired with a T, every G paired with a C — then *each strand contains all the information needed to specify the other.* Separate the two strands. Provide free nucleotides in solution. Add an enzyme that can build a new strand by reading a template. Each parental strand will direct the synthesis of a new complementary partner. You end up with two double helices, each consisting of one parental strand and one newly synthesized strand. This model is called **semiconservative replication** — each original strand is *conserved* into a daughter duplex, paired with a fresh complement.

The Watson-Crick paper proposed the model. It did not prove it. There were two other possibilities. **Conservative replication** would keep both parental strands together in one daughter duplex and synthesize an entirely new duplex alongside it — old-old plus new-new, not old-new plus old-new. **Dispersive replication** would chop both strands into segments and scramble parental and new material throughout both daughter duplexes — every strand a mosaic of old and new. The three models make different predictions about how parental material would be distributed across generations, and someone needed to look.

In 1958, **Matthew Meselson** and **Franklin Stahl** at Caltech designed what has been called "the most beautiful experiment in biology" — the phrase is associated with **Frederic Lawrence Holmes** in his 2001 book of that title ([Holmes 2001 *Meselson, Stahl, and the Replication of DNA: A History of "The Most Beautiful Experiment in Biology"* Yale University Press](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300085402/meselson-stahl-and-replication-dna/) [verify]). The design is so clean it is worth reconstructing step by step.

Start with **two stable isotopes of nitrogen**. ¹⁴N is the common form, the one you breathe; ¹⁵N is the heavier form, present in trace amounts in nature, indistinguishable chemically but slightly denser. Nitrogen is in every base, so DNA built from ¹⁵N-containing precursors is about 1% denser than DNA built from ¹⁴N. **Density is detectable** by spinning DNA in a tube of concentrated cesium chloride at very high speed for many hours — the cesium ions form a density gradient (denser at the bottom, lighter at the top), and DNA molecules settle out at the depth corresponding to their own density. Run for long enough, and you can resolve density differences of less than 1%.

The protocol. Grow *E. coli* for many generations in medium containing ¹⁵N as the only nitrogen source — all the DNA in the cells becomes ¹⁵N-labeled, fully "heavy." Confirm: spin a sample, find a single band at "heavy" density. Now transfer the cells to medium containing ¹⁴N — no more heavy nitrogen, only light. Let them divide. Take samples at one generation, two generations, three generations. Spin each sample. Look at where the band lands.

Here is what each model predicts.

**Conservative replication.** After one generation, half the DNA should still be fully ¹⁵N (the original parental duplex, intact) and half should be fully ¹⁴N (the entirely new duplex). Two bands: heavy and light. After two generations, the ratio shifts but the bands stay at the same two densities — 25% heavy, 75% light.

**Semiconservative replication.** After one generation, every duplex is a hybrid — one parental ¹⁵N strand, one newly synthesized ¹⁴N strand. One band, at intermediate density. After two generations, the hybrid duplexes each separate again — half make new hybrid duplexes (one ¹⁵N + one ¹⁴N), half make fully light duplexes (¹⁴N + ¹⁴N). Two bands: intermediate and light, in equal amounts.

**Dispersive replication.** After one generation, every strand contains a mix of old and new material — one band at intermediate density, like semiconservative. But after two generations, the strands keep getting diluted with new material, so the band gradually shifts lighter without ever splitting into two distinct bands.

What Meselson and Stahl actually saw, after one generation: **one band, exactly at intermediate density.** Conservative ruled out. After two generations: **two bands, one at intermediate density, one at fully light density**, in approximately equal amounts. Dispersive ruled out. Semiconservative correct ([Meselson and Stahl 1958 *PNAS* 44:671–682](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.44.7.671)).

The experiment is beautiful for three reasons. First, the prediction is *quantitative and specific*: a single band at exactly intermediate density at generation one, a 1:1 mixture of intermediate and light at generation two. Vague predictions cannot be sharply falsified; sharp predictions can. Second, the *same experimental design discriminates among all three competing models*. You do not have to design separate experiments to rule out conservative and dispersive; one density-gradient series does both. Third, the *signal-to-noise is high*. The density difference between ¹⁵N-DNA and ¹⁴N-DNA is small, but it is large enough relative to the cesium-chloride gradient resolution that the bands are crisply separated. The experiment is the cleanest possible discrimination between three models given the tools available in 1958. Meselson, asked later, said the design came together over a long dinner. Sometimes biology is exactly like physics.

Semiconservative replication, settled in 1958, then raised a new question: *how* does it actually work in the cell? Watson and Crick's "immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism" was a logical claim, not a mechanistic one. To copy a 3-billion-base-pair human genome in roughly six hours during S phase requires a great deal of machinery moving in concert. That machinery is what we build next.

## The replication fork — a small machine running at a thousand bases a second

A replication fork is a moving Y-shaped junction in DNA. Two parental strands enter from the right (say); two pairs of daughter strands emerge to the left. At the cusp of the Y, the parental duplex is being unwound; behind the fork, two new duplexes are being assembled. The geometry is forced: DNA polymerases can only synthesize in the 5'→3' direction, while the two parental templates run antiparallel, so synthesis has to happen *in different ways* on the two strands. We will name the players and then trace one fork in order.

**Helicase** is the enzyme that pries apart the two parental strands. It hydrolyzes ATP to power its motion along the DNA, breaking the hydrogen bonds between base pairs as it goes. In *E. coli*, the replicative helicase is **DnaB**, loaded onto the lagging-strand template; it tracks 5'→3' along the parental strand it sits on and pries the duplex open ahead of itself. Helicase is fast — in *E. coli* it can unwind around 1,000 base pairs per second under standard conditions ([Kornberg and Baker 1992 *DNA Replication* 2nd ed., Freeman](https://www.macmillanlearning.com/college/us/product/DNA-Replication/p/0716720035) [verify for specific rate]).

**Single-strand binding proteins** (SSBs in bacteria; RPA in eukaryotes) coat the separated single strands behind the helicase, preventing them from re-annealing and from forming hairpins (intra-strand structures that would block synthesis). They are passive in the sense that they do not hydrolyze ATP, but they are essential — without them, single-stranded DNA would fold up on itself.

**Topoisomerase** solves a problem that did not exist in Watson and Crick's static helix. When you unwind a closed double helix, the helix ahead of the fork has to either rotate or accumulate twist. In a long chromosome, free rotation is not available — the DNA cannot spin fast enough. The accumulated twist (positive supercoiling) would eventually stop the fork. Topoisomerases solve this by *transiently cutting* one or both strands, letting the DNA rotate, and re-sealing the cut. Type I topoisomerases cut one strand; type II topoisomerases cut both. In bacteria, **DNA gyrase** (a type II topoisomerase) introduces negative supercoils ahead of the fork, counteracting the positive twist the helicase generates ([Wang 1996 *Annu Rev Biochem* 65:635–692](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev.bi.65.070196.003223) [verify]). This is also the target of the fluoroquinolone antibiotics (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin), which trap bacterial topoisomerases in their cut state and kill the bacterium — a drug class that exists because bacterial and human topoisomerases are different enough to be selectively poisoned.

**Primase** is the enzyme that synthesizes short **RNA primers** — about 10 nucleotides long in bacteria, slightly longer in eukaryotes. Here is why primers exist at all. DNA polymerase, the enzyme that does the actual synthesis, has a peculiar limitation: it can *extend* an existing chain by adding nucleotides to a free 3'-OH, but it cannot *initiate* a new chain from a single nucleotide on a template. It needs a pre-existing 3'-OH to work from. Primase, which is a specialized RNA polymerase, has no such constraint — it can lay down a short RNA chain on a single-stranded DNA template starting from scratch. So the cell's solution is to use RNA primers as starter sequences, then have DNA polymerase extend them, and clean up the RNA later. (Why RNA and not DNA? Because the cell needs to be able to distinguish primer from finished product. RNA primers are recognizable and removable; if primers were DNA, the cell would have no way to tell starter from final sequence.)

**DNA polymerase** is the actual workhorse. In *E. coli*, the main replicative polymerase is **DNA polymerase III** — a large multi-subunit complex with a synthesis rate of around 1,000 nucleotides per second per fork and an intrinsic error rate (before any proofreading) of about 1 mistake per 10⁵ bases ([Kunkel 2004 *J Biol Chem* 279:16895–16898](https://www.jbc.org/article/S0021-9258(20)61605-1/fulltext) [verify]). In eukaryotes, **DNA polymerase ε** synthesizes the leading strand and **DNA polymerase δ** synthesizes most of the lagging strand, with **DNA polymerase α** (a primase-polymerase complex) doing the very short initial extensions ([Burgers and Kunkel 2017 *Annu Rev Biochem* 86:417–438](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev-biochem-061516-044709) [verify]). Mammalian polymerases are slower than bacterial ones — roughly 50 nucleotides per second per fork — but mammalian cells make up for it by firing tens of thousands of replication origins simultaneously, so an entire human genome can still be copied in around six hours. Bacterial chromosomes have a single origin and rely on speed; eukaryotic chromosomes have many origins and rely on parallelism.

**RNase H** and **FEN1** (in eukaryotes; in *E. coli*, **DNA polymerase I** does an equivalent job) remove the RNA primers once they have served their purpose. The gap that the removed primer leaves behind is filled in by DNA polymerase, using the remaining 3'-OH on the upstream DNA as its starting point.

**DNA ligase** seals the final nick — a phosphodiester bond between two adjacent nucleotides — closing the strand into a continuous polymer. Without ligase, the new strand would be a chain of fragments held together only by base pairing to the parental strand; pull the duplex apart and the daughter strand would fall into pieces. Ligase is what makes the new strand actually one continuous molecule.

Now run the fork. Helicase opens the duplex. SSBs coat the separated strands. Topoisomerase relieves the torsion ahead. Primase lays down an RNA primer on each template. DNA polymerase III (or ε / δ in eukaryotes) extends from the primer.

On one of the two strands, the polymerase is moving in the same direction as the fork is opening — synthesis proceeds continuously, one primer at the start, then continuous extension behind the helicase for the entire length of the chromosome. This is the **leading strand**. Simple.

On the other strand, the polymerase has to synthesize in the direction *opposite* to the fork's motion, because of the antiparallel template orientation. So the polymerase cannot keep up with the fork — it has to keep restarting. The pattern is: primase lays down a primer near the fork; polymerase extends the primer *backward* (away from the fork) until it bumps into the previously synthesized fragment; the fragment is sealed in; the fork has meanwhile opened more template; primase lays down another primer; the cycle repeats. The result is a series of short fragments — **Okazaki fragments**, named for **Reiji Okazaki** and his colleagues, who first showed in 1968 that newly synthesized DNA on the lagging strand exists as short pieces before being joined into a continuous strand ([Okazaki et al. 1968 *PNAS* 59:598–605](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.59.2.598)). In *E. coli*, Okazaki fragments are roughly 1,000–2,000 nucleotides long; in eukaryotes, around 100–200. This is the **lagging strand**.

Why does the cell tolerate the awkwardness of discontinuous synthesis? Because the alternative is worse. The cell could, in principle, have evolved two polymerases — one 5'→3' and one 3'→5' — and run them in opposite directions on the two templates, both continuous, no Okazaki fragments. It did not. The most-cited reason is that a 3'→5' polymerase has a fidelity problem: in 5'→3' synthesis, the high-energy bond that drives nucleotide addition is on the *incoming* nucleotide (the triphosphate that gets cleaved to power the bond formation). If the polymerase makes a mistake, it can simply remove the misincorporated nucleotide, leaving the previously correct 3' end intact, and try again. In hypothetical 3'→5' synthesis, the high-energy bond is on the *growing chain*, and removing a misincorporated nucleotide would leave a dead-end 5' end that no nucleotide could be added to. The geometry of energetics rules out 3'→5' polymerization, period. So the cell pays the cost of Okazaki fragments to get high-fidelity 5'→3' synthesis on both strands. The awkwardness is the price of accuracy.

Two consequences worth naming. First, the **lagging strand is intrinsically slower** in terms of bases newly added per second per replisome — the polymerase has to keep stopping, releasing, restarting. The replication machinery solves this in part by *coupling* the two polymerases into a single replisome, with the lagging-strand template forming a loop so that both polymerases can move in the same direction relative to the moving fork. Imagine a long ribbon (the lagging-strand template) that gets pulled through a loop in the replisome, repeatedly bowing out and being released. The bookkeeping is intricate; the result is that both strands keep up with the fork. Second, the lagging strand has many more *nicks* in it during replication — every junction between Okazaki fragments is a transient single-strand break before ligase seals it. This makes the lagging strand structurally more vulnerable, and we will see in a moment that one of the strand-discrimination mechanisms used by mismatch repair exploits exactly these nicks.

A final number, to anchor the scale. Human cells have approximately 3 × 10⁹ base pairs per haploid genome. Replication in mammalian cells proceeds at around 50 base pairs per second per fork. A single fork would therefore take ~ 3 × 10⁹ / 50 ≈ 6 × 10⁷ seconds — about two years — to copy the genome. Cells do it in six hours. The arithmetic only works because mammalian chromosomes fire roughly 30,000–50,000 replication origins, each generating two forks, all running in parallel ([Berezney, Dubey, and Huberman 2000 *Chromosoma* 108:471–484](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s004120050399) [verify]). Bacteria have one origin; eukaryotes have tens of thousands. Both strategies copy a genome in a few hours. They get there by different routes.

## How accurate is replication, really — and what makes it so accurate

Let me give you four numbers up front, in the order the cell uses them.

**Naive base pairing alone, without an enzyme:** wrong nucleotides bind across from templates roughly once every 10² to 10³ pairings. The energetic difference between a correct A-T pair (with optimal hydrogen-bond geometry) and an incorrect A-C pair (geometry slightly off, only one hydrogen bond fits) is small enough that thermal noise often defeats it.

**DNA polymerase nucleotide selection — base pairing plus active-site geometry:** the polymerase's active site is shaped so that only a correctly paired incoming nucleotide fits cleanly. An incorrect nucleotide creates a slight distortion that slows the rate of phosphodiester bond formation by orders of magnitude. This nucleotide-selection step brings the error rate down to about **1 per 10⁵ bases** ([Kunkel 2004](https://www.jbc.org/article/S0021-9258(20)61605-1/fulltext) [verify]).

**Plus 3'→5' proofreading exonuclease:** the same polymerase has a second active site — an **exonuclease** that cuts nucleotides off the 3' end of the growing strand one at a time. When the polymerase adds a wrong nucleotide, the misshapen 3' end fits poorly in the polymerization active site and is shunted to the exonuclease site, which cleaves it off. The polymerase then tries again. Proofreading reduces the error rate by approximately another factor of 100, to about **1 per 10⁷ bases**.

**Plus post-replication mismatch repair:** the small fraction of errors that escape the polymerase and the exonuclease are picked up by a separate scanning machinery (described in detail in the next section) that detects mismatches in newly synthesized DNA and excises them. Mismatch repair reduces the rate by another roughly 100-fold, to about **1 per 10⁹ to 10¹⁰ bases** — the final, lifetime accuracy.

Note the structure of the argument. Each layer reduces the error rate by ~100-fold. Three layers compound to ~10⁶-fold reduction from naive base pairing. The accuracy is not magic; it is the product of three independent error-correction steps, each acting on the residue of errors the previous step missed.

A second way to read the number. The human genome is about 3 × 10⁹ base pairs. A mutation rate of 10⁻⁹ per base per replication implies about **3 mutations per cell division**. A typical somatic cell in your body has divided perhaps 10–50 times since you were a zygote (more in the skin and gut epithelia; fewer in long-lived neurons). The total mutational burden in any given cell at age 50 is on the order of hundreds to a few thousand mutations per cell, concentrated in tissues with high turnover. Most of those mutations fall in non-coding sequence and are harmless. A few will hit tumor-suppressor or oncogene loci. Chapter 8 will pick up this thread — cancer is what happens when the dice roll badly in a cell that has time and opportunity to keep rolling. For now, the point is that the mutation rate is not zero. It is engineered to be small but nonzero, and the engineering is the three-layer system I just described.

## Worked example — one corrected error, from misincorporation to ligation

Now we slow down and trace a single error all the way through the correction pipeline. This is the chapter's mechanistic centerpiece. I want every step explicit, every enzyme named, every motion accounted for. The example is in *E. coli* because the prokaryotic version is cleaner than the eukaryotic version — fewer proteins, the same logic.

**Setup.** A replication fork is moving along an *E. coli* chromosome at ~1,000 bp/sec. DNA polymerase III is extending the leading strand from an RNA primer. The template strand contains, at some position, the sequence ...3'-T-3'-A-...; the correct complementary base to incorporate is A (across from T) followed by T (across from A).

**Step 1 — Nucleotide selection.** The polymerase's active site accepts the four incoming deoxynucleoside triphosphates (dATP, dGTP, dCTP, dTTP) one at a time from the cellular pool. The active site is shaped to favor a planar, geometrically correct Watson-Crick pair. A correctly-pairing dATP across from the template T fits cleanly: the polymerase rapidly catalyzes the phosphodiester bond, releases pyrophosphate, and moves on. The error rate at this step alone is ~10⁻⁵ — about one in a hundred thousand incorporations is wrong.

**Step 2 — Wrong nucleotide incorporated.** Suppose, at the next position (template A), the polymerase incorporates a dGTP instead of the correct dTTP. The misincorporated G now sits across from A in the daughter strand — an A•G mispair. The pair has the wrong hydrogen-bonding pattern and the wrong width; the daughter strand's 3' end now has a slight distortion.

**Step 3 — Proofreading by 3'→5' exonuclease.** The misshapen 3' end fits poorly in the polymerization active site — the next dNTP cannot dock cleanly because the geometry is off. The strand's 3' end is shunted instead to a second active site on the polymerase, the **3'→5' exonuclease**. This site cleaves the most recently added nucleotide off the chain. The misincorporated G is excised. The polymerase now has a clean 3' end again (the previous correctly-paired A) and tries again, this time incorporating the correct dTTP. The error has been corrected at the moment of synthesis. The error rate falls to ~10⁻⁷.

**Step 4 — Error escapes proofreading.** Now suppose, at some other position, an error slips past proofreading. (This happens roughly one time in a hundred mispair events; the exonuclease is good, not perfect.) The replication fork continues moving. The mispair is now several bases behind the polymerase, embedded in newly synthesized double-stranded DNA. Proofreading no longer has access — its window is at the very 3' end of the strand.

**Step 5 — MutS recognition.** A protein called **MutS** scans the newly replicated DNA, looking for mispairs. MutS is a dimer; it grips the DNA and slides along it, "feeling" the helix for the small distortions that mispairs cause. When it finds a mispair, it clamps down, recruits a second protein **MutL** (which acts as a scaffold), and the MutS-MutL complex now sits at the lesion.

**Step 6 — Strand discrimination — the parent/daughter problem.** Here is the deep problem mismatch repair has to solve. The mispair has one correct base (on the parental template) and one wrong base (incorporated by the polymerase on the daughter strand). MMR must remove the wrong base, not the correct one — which means it must distinguish which strand is the daughter (just-synthesized, fallible) and which is the parent (correct by definition). How does the cell tell?

In *E. coli*, the answer is **DNA methylation**. The bacterium maintains a maintenance methyltransferase called **Dam** that adds a methyl group to the adenine in every GATC sequence in the genome. After replication, the parental strand is fully methylated (Dam has had time since the previous replication round), while the daughter strand is *unmethylated* — it has just been synthesized and Dam has not yet had time to find and methylate it. The DNA at and near a mismatch is therefore transiently **hemimethylated**: methylated on the parent strand, unmethylated on the daughter strand. A protein called **MutH** binds a nearby hemimethylated GATC site and cuts the *unmethylated* strand. This nicks the daughter strand — and only the daughter strand — at a position near the mismatch. The MMR machinery has now identified which strand is the daughter and where to enter it.

In eukaryotes, the methylation trick is not used (eukaryotic DNA methylation has different functions and timing). Instead, eukaryotic MMR uses **nicks left in the daughter strand by ongoing replication** — specifically, the nicks between Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand, and corresponding nicks left by ribonucleotide excision on the leading strand — as the strand-discrimination signal. The mismatch repair machinery enters at the nearest nick and proceeds toward the mismatch ([Kunkel and Erie 2015 *Annu Rev Genet* 49:291–313](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev-genet-112414-054722) [verify]). The biology is different; the logic is the same. Find an asymmetry, use the asymmetry to identify the daughter strand, cut the daughter.

**Step 7 — Excision.** Once the daughter strand is nicked, an **exonuclease** (one of several, depending on the direction of the nick relative to the mismatch) chews back from the nick through the mismatch and continues for a short distance past it. The result is a single-stranded gap on the daughter strand, several hundred to a couple of thousand nucleotides long, with the parental strand still intact as a template.

**Step 8 — Resynthesis and ligation.** DNA polymerase III (in *E. coli*; DNA polymerase δ in eukaryotes) fills in the gap using the parental strand as template — this time with the *correct* base across from A, namely T. DNA ligase seals the final nick. The mismatch is gone. The error has been corrected. Final error rate: ~10⁻⁹ per base per replication.

**The lesson.** A 10⁻⁹ error rate is achieved by three layered systems: polymerase fidelity (~10⁻⁵ → corrects mispair geometry at the active site), proofreading exonuclease (~10⁻⁷ → catches errors at the moment of synthesis), mismatch repair (~10⁻⁹ → catches the residue post-replication). Each layer catches ~100-fold more errors than the previous would have caught. Compounded, they give ~10⁵-fold reduction over naive base pairing. Knock out any one of these layers and the error rate spikes by ~100-fold.

**The clinical sequel.** **Lynch syndrome** — also called hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC) — is what happens when an individual inherits a defective copy of one of the human MMR genes, most commonly **MLH1** (the human homolog of *E. coli* MutL) or **MSH2** (the homolog of MutS). Heterozygotes have one good copy and are largely protected. But in any rapidly dividing tissue — the colon epithelium replaces its entire surface every few days — the second copy can be lost by random somatic mutation, and the resulting MMR-deficient cell now accumulates mutations ~100-fold faster than its neighbors. The fastest-mutating sequences in the genome are **microsatellites** — short tandem repeats like (CA)ₙ where slippage during replication generates length changes that MMR ordinarily corrects. Without MMR, microsatellite repeats fluctuate in length wildly, producing the diagnostic phenotype called **microsatellite instability (MSI-high)**. About 3% of all colorectal cancers in the general population, and a larger fraction of early-onset cases, arise from Lynch-syndrome inheritance ([Lynch et al. 2015 *Nat Rev Cancer* 15:181–194](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrc3878) [verify]; founding clinical description [Lynch et al. 1966 *Arch Intern Med* 117:206–212](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/article-abstract/566930) [verify]; molecular basis with MMR-deficient mutator phenotype [Lindor et al. 2006 *J Clin Oncol* 24:1043–1048](https://ascopubs.org/doi/10.1200/JCO.2005.04.4214) [verify]). The 2015 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized **Paul Modrich** for working out the MMR pathway, alongside **Tomas Lindahl** (BER) and **Aziz Sancar** (NER) ([Nobel Prize in Chemistry 2015](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2015/summary/)).

The mechanism that corrects one wrong G across from one A is the same mechanism that protects you from a colon cancer at age 35. The molecular biology and the clinical genetics are not separate stories. They are the same story told at different scales.

## Damage that isn't a mispair — three more repair pathways

So far we have been tracking errors made during replication. But DNA is also chemically attacked at every moment, regardless of whether it is replicating. The atmosphere is oxidative. Water spontaneously hydrolyzes the bonds that hold bases onto the sugar backbone. Cellular metabolism generates reactive oxygen species. Ultraviolet light from the sun is absorbed by DNA's bases and drives chemistry the cell did not authorize. Estimates put the rate of spontaneous DNA damage events at tens of thousands per cell per day ([Lindahl 1993 *Nature* 362:709–715](https://www.nature.com/articles/362709a0); Nobel lecture [Lindahl 2015](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2015/lindahl/lecture/)). Three more repair pathways clean up most of this damage. Each handles a different class of lesion. Each is, in its own way, a beautiful piece of biochemistry. We will take them in order of lesion size.

### Base excision repair — single damaged bases

The smallest lesions are individual chemically modified bases. The most common: **depurination** (a purine — A or G — spontaneously falling off its sugar, leaving an abasic site, perhaps 10,000 events per cell per day); **deamination** of cytosine to uracil (loss of an amino group, converting a C-G pair into a U-G pair, which on replication would become a T-A pair — a permanent mutation if not corrected); and **oxidation** of guanine to 8-oxoguanine (a particularly mutagenic lesion because 8-oxoG can mispair with adenine, generating G-T transversions on the next round of replication).

**Base excision repair** handles these one-base lesions. The machinery is simple and elegant. A family of enzymes called **DNA glycosylases** — each one specific for a particular kind of damaged base — slides along the DNA, flips suspect bases out of the helix one at a time into a small pocket in the enzyme, and tests whether the base is the one it is looking for. If yes, the glycosylase cleaves the glycosidic bond between the base and the sugar, leaving an abasic site (a sugar with no base attached). The most-studied example is **uracil-DNA glycosylase** (UDG), which scans for any uracil — a base that should not be in DNA — and removes it. (This is why DNA uses thymine instead of uracil: cytosine deaminates to uracil spontaneously, and the cell needs to be able to recognize uracil as "damage" rather than "normal." If DNA used U, every spontaneous deamination of C would be invisible.)

After the glycosylase removes the base, an enzyme called **AP endonuclease** (AP = apurinic/apyrimidinic, the abasic site) cuts the sugar-phosphate backbone next to the missing base. A short stretch of DNA is then removed by an exonuclease activity (or by FEN1 in long-patch BER), DNA polymerase fills in the correct nucleotide using the opposite strand as template, and ligase seals the nick. Five enzymes, one lesion at a time, billions of lesions per genome per lifetime.

**What goes wrong when BER fails.** Mutations in the DNA glycosylase **MUTYH** — which corrects 8-oxoG·A mispairs — cause **MUTYH-associated polyposis (MAP)**, an autosomal-recessive predisposition to colorectal cancer with multiple adenomatous polyps ([Al-Tassan et al. 2002 *Nat Genet* 30:227–232](https://www.nature.com/articles/ng828) [verify]). Because BER deals with such a common lesion class, complete loss of BER is generally embryonic lethal in mammals; the clinical syndromes come from partial losses of specific glycosylase activities.

### Nucleotide excision repair — bulky lesions

A larger class of damage is "bulky lesions" — chemical adducts large enough to distort the helix locally. The canonical example is the **thymine dimer**, where ultraviolet light (UVB, specifically around 260 nm) causes two adjacent thymines on the same strand to form a covalent ring fusion (a cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer, CPD). The dimer is a kink in the helix that no replicative polymerase will copy accurately. Other bulky lesions include adducts formed by environmental chemicals — benzo[a]pyrene from cigarette smoke or charred meat; aflatoxin from moldy peanuts; some chemotherapy drugs like cisplatin, which intentionally damages cancer cells' DNA.

**Nucleotide excision repair** is the pathway that handles bulky lesions. It works on a longer length scale than BER. A complex of proteins led by **XPC** in mammals (or its bacterial equivalent **UvrA-UvrB**) scans the genome for distortions. When it finds one, it recruits a larger machine that opens up the helix around the lesion, makes two cuts — one on each side of the damaged region — and excises a single-strand patch of roughly 25–30 nucleotides containing the damage. DNA polymerase then fills the gap using the undamaged opposite strand as template, and ligase seals. The lesion is gone ([Sancar Nobel lecture 2015](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2015/sancar/lecture/)).

The key players in mammalian NER are named after the disease they cause when they fail: **XPA, XPB, XPC, XPD, XPE, XPF, XPG**, where "XP" is for **xeroderma pigmentosum** ([Cleaver 1968 *Nature* 218:652–656](https://www.nature.com/articles/218652a0) was the founding observation that XP cells fail to repair UV damage). Loss-of-function mutations in any one of these genes blocks NER and produces the XP phenotype: extreme UV sensitivity, freckling on sun-exposed skin in early childhood, and a thousand-to-two-thousand-fold elevated lifetime risk of non-melanoma skin cancer ([Bradford et al. 2011](https://jmg.bmj.com/content/48/3/168) [verify], for the elevated-risk estimate; the precise multiplier varies across cohorts). The boy in the chapter's opening case has one of these mutations. Every UV photon that strikes his skin damages DNA that his cells cannot repair. The only intervention available is to keep the photons off his skin.

A bonus complication: NER has two flavors. **Global genome NER (GG-NER)** handles damage anywhere in the genome and is initiated by XPC. **Transcription-coupled NER (TC-NER)** handles damage in the template strand of actively transcribed genes — when RNA polymerase stalls at a lesion, it recruits the repair machinery directly to the site. TC-NER is faster and more efficient than GG-NER for the small fraction of the genome that is being transcribed at any given time. The clinical syndrome **Cockayne syndrome** is what happens when TC-NER specifically fails — patients have developmental and neurological symptoms in addition to UV sensitivity, because the failure to repair damage in active genes is particularly catastrophic for differentiated cells.

### Double-strand break repair — when the chromosome itself is broken

The most dangerous lesion of all is a **double-strand break (DSB)** — both strands of the helix severed at the same position. A DSB can be caused by ionizing radiation (X-rays, gamma rays, some cosmic rays), by certain chemicals (bleomycin), by mechanical stress on replicating chromosomes, or — surprisingly often — by the cell deliberately cutting its own DNA (V(D)J recombination in lymphocytes; meiotic crossover; CRISPR-Cas9). A DSB is dangerous because the two ends of the broken chromosome can drift apart, fuse to other broken ends in the cell to form chromosomal translocations, or be lost entirely. A single unrepaired DSB can kill a cell. Multiple misrepaired DSBs are how chromosomal rearrangements in cancer arise.

There are two main DSB-repair pathways in mammals, and they have very different fidelity profiles.

**Homologous recombination (HR)** is the high-fidelity pathway. It is available only in S and G2 phases of the cell cycle, after DNA replication, because it requires a **sister chromatid** — an identical copy of the broken chromosome — as a template. The pathway is roughly as follows. The broken ends are first **resected** by nucleases — one strand is chewed back, leaving a long 3' single-stranded overhang on each side of the break. The single-stranded overhang is coated with the protein **RAD51**, which forms a nucleoprotein filament. The RAD51 filament then searches the genome for a matching sequence; when it finds one (the sister chromatid), it invades the homologous duplex, displaces one strand, and pairs with the complementary sequence. DNA synthesis extends the invading strand using the sister chromatid as template, copying over the broken region. The structure is resolved by nucleases that cut the crossover intermediates and rejoin everything. The break is repaired with no loss of information, because the sister chromatid was an exact copy of what was lost. HR is essentially error-free.

The key proteins in HR include **BRCA1** and **BRCA2** (the genes whose germline mutations were mentioned in Chapter 03's two-sisters opening), **RAD51**, **PALB2**, **MRN complex (MRE11-RAD50-NBS1)**, **CtIP**, and others. Germline loss-of-function mutations in **BRCA1** or **BRCA2** disable HR. Heterozygotes have one good copy and are normally fine; but in rapidly dividing tissues like breast and ovarian epithelium, the second copy can be lost somatically, generating an HR-deficient cell that now accumulates chromosomal damage rapidly and is at high risk of becoming a tumor. The estimated lifetime breast-cancer risk in BRCA1 carriers is around 60–72% by age 80 ([Kuchenbaecker et al. 2017 *JAMA* 317:2402–2416](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632503) [verify]), with ovarian risk around 40–44% in the same cohort. The reason the cancers cluster in breast and ovarian tissue specifically, rather than appearing everywhere, is still incompletely understood — the tissue-specificity is a real puzzle in BRCA biology.

**Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)** is the error-prone pathway. It is available throughout the cell cycle, including G1 when no sister chromatid exists. The pathway is simpler than HR: the two broken ends are bound by the **Ku70/Ku80** heterodimer, recruited along with **DNA-PKcs** and **LIG4** (DNA ligase IV), and the ends are ligated back together — directly, without any homologous template. The fidelity problem is that the broken ends often have small chemical modifications (because real breaks rarely produce clean blunt ends) and need to be trimmed or filled before they can be ligated. Trimming and filling introduces small insertions or deletions at the junction. The chromosome is repaired in the sense that there are no longer two ends drifting; it is *not* repaired in the sense that the original sequence is preserved. NHEJ is "the default DSB repair in mammals" in the sense that most G1 cells use NHEJ for most breaks, and even S-phase cells may use NHEJ if the break geometry is inconvenient for HR. NHEJ is also the pathway that CRISPR-Cas9 editing exploits: when Cas9 makes a targeted double-strand break, the cell repairs it by NHEJ, often introducing small insertions or deletions that knock out the targeted gene.

The choice between HR and NHEJ is regulated by **cell-cycle position** (HR requires S/G2), **end-resection commitment** (once resection begins, HR is favored), and **chromatin context**. BRCA1 plays a role in promoting end resection and steering breaks toward HR; 53BP1 plays a role in blocking resection and steering toward NHEJ. In BRCA1-deficient cells, the balance tips toward NHEJ even when HR would have been appropriate, which is part of why BRCA1 loss is so destabilizing.

**Synthetic lethality — the PARP inhibitor story.** Here is where the chapter cashes in a cross-reference to the cell-biology book's Chapter 11. **Synthetic lethality** is the situation where mutating gene A alone does not kill a cell, mutating gene B alone does not kill a cell, but mutating both kills the cell. The pair (A, B) is "synthetically lethal." For BRCA-deficient tumors, the partner gene turns out to be **PARP1**, an enzyme involved in **base excision repair** (specifically, in detecting single-strand breaks and recruiting BER machinery). Cells with functioning HR can tolerate loss of PARP1 — the small fraction of single-strand breaks that escape BER and become double-strand breaks during replication get repaired by HR. Cells with functioning PARP1 can tolerate loss of BRCA1/2 — they rarely have to use HR, so most damage is handled by other pathways. But cells with *both* PARP1 inhibited *and* BRCA1/2 mutated cannot repair the single-strand breaks that become double-strand breaks, cannot use HR to fix them, default to NHEJ which is error-prone, and either die from the accumulation of damage or undergo catastrophic chromosomal rearrangement.

This is the mechanism of **olaparib** and the other PARP inhibitors approved for BRCA-mutated breast, ovarian, prostate, and pancreatic cancers ([Lord and Ashworth 2017 *Science* 355:1152–1158](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aam7344) [verify]; founding clinical demonstration [Bryant et al. 2005 *Nature* 434:913–917](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03443) [verify] and [Farmer et al. 2005 *Nature* 434:917–921](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03445) [verify]). The drug does not directly kill BRCA-deficient cells. It blocks a different repair pathway (BER, by trapping PARP1 on DNA), and that block forces the cell to use the pathway it cannot use (HR, missing because of BRCA loss). The tumor cells die from a repair pathway dependency the surrounding normal cells do not have. The normal cells have one good copy of BRCA1 or BRCA2 and can do HR; the tumor cells lost the second copy somatically and cannot. Synthetic lethality turns the tumor's defect into the tumor's vulnerability. This is one of the cleanest "targeted therapy" mechanisms in oncology, and it traces directly to the molecular logic of DNA repair we have spent this chapter building.

## Telomeres and the end-replication problem — a quick visit to a deep problem

There is one more replication problem we have to name, because it is going to matter for Chapter 8 (cancer) and for any later discussion of cellular aging. Linear chromosomes have ends. RNA primers on the lagging strand are eventually removed and the gap is filled — except at the very end of the chromosome, where there is no upstream DNA to extend from after the terminal primer is removed. Every round of replication, the lagging strand at each chromosome end shortens by the length of the terminal RNA primer (~10–100 nucleotides in different systems).

This is the **end-replication problem**, first articulated by James Watson and Aleksei Olovnikov independently in the early 1970s. Without some fix, chromosomes would shorten with every cell division until they ran out of essential genes. The fix is **telomeres** — long, repetitive, non-coding sequences at chromosome ends (in vertebrates, the hexamer **TTAGGG** repeated thousands of times). Telomeres are a buffer: they shorten with each division, but the shortening hits non-coding sequence, not genes. When the buffer is exhausted, the cell senses critically short telomeres as DNA damage and enters a permanent growth arrest called **senescence**, or it dies. This buffer mechanism puts a counter on the number of times a normal somatic cell can divide — the **Hayflick limit**, around 50 population doublings in cultured human fibroblasts ([Hayflick and Moorhead 1961 *Exp Cell Res* 25:585–621](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0014482761901926) [verify]).

Some cell types maintain telomeres by expressing the enzyme **telomerase**, which carries its own RNA template and adds telomeric repeats back onto chromosome ends. Germline cells, stem cells, and most cancer cells express telomerase; most somatic cells do not. **Carol Greider, Elizabeth Blackburn, and Jack Szostak** shared the 2009 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for discovering telomerase ([Nobel Prize 2009](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/2009/summary/)). The cancer relevance is direct: most human cancers reactivate telomerase as one of the somatic changes that lets them divide indefinitely, escaping the Hayflick limit. We will pick this up in Chapter 8.

For now: telomeres are how linear chromosomes survive the end-replication problem. The mechanism is a buffer plus a (mostly suppressed) maintenance enzyme. Both pieces matter.

## Common misconceptions

Three errors I see in student work, each easy to make and worth correcting explicitly.

**"DNA replication is perfectly accurate."** It is not. The accuracy is ~10⁻⁹ per base per replication, which is high but not infinite. A human cell making 3 × 10⁹ base pairs of new DNA every division therefore introduces an average of about three new point mutations per cell division. Over a lifetime of trillions of cell divisions, this adds up — most of the mutational burden of cancer (Chapter 8) comes from this slow accumulation. If replication *were* perfectly accurate, there would be no variation for evolution to act on, and you, the reader, would not exist in your current biological form. The accuracy is high enough to keep cells viable across thousands of divisions but low enough to keep populations evolving. Both bounds matter.

**"DNA repair fixes all damage."** It does not. Each repair pathway has limits — some lesions are not recognized by any glycosylase, some thymine dimers go undetected before replication, some double-strand breaks are misrepaired by NHEJ in ways that produce chromosomal translocations. The cell makes a probabilistic bet: most damage will be cleaned up, the residue will mostly be benign, and the rare unrepaired damage in a critical location is a small cost to pay for a system that mostly works. Cancer is what happens when the small cost accumulates in a single cell lineage past the point where the cell can be controlled. (The somatic-evolutionary view of cancer is exactly the picture this implies: repair is statistical, not absolute, and selection within the body's tissues turns the residue into disease.)

**"PARP inhibitors directly kill BRCA-deficient cells."** They do not. The drug blocks PARP1, an enzyme involved in BER (and in single-strand-break detection more generally). The block is not directly lethal. It becomes lethal *only* in cells that also cannot do homologous recombination — that is, cells that have lost both copies of BRCA1 or BRCA2. Normal cells in the patient retain one good BRCA allele and can do HR; the tumor cells have lost both alleles somatically and cannot. The drug kills the tumor by forcing it to depend on the very pathway it lacks. This is **synthetic lethality**, not direct toxicity, and the distinction is the whole reason PARP inhibitors are safer than older chemotherapies — they kill repair-deficient cancer cells while sparing repair-competent normal cells.

There is a fourth misconception worth flagging briefly. **"DNA polymerase reads sequences."** It does not, in the sense that it does not "know" which base it is putting in. The polymerase is a machine that accepts a template and a nucleotide pool, geometrically rejects incoming nucleotides whose pair geometry is wrong, and catalyzes the bond when the geometry is right. The "reading" is the chemistry of base-pair fit. There is no symbolic operation happening. This is worth saying because the language of "reading" and "code" — borrowed from information theory — sometimes leads students to imagine an active interpretive step at each base. There is no such step. The interpretation is the geometry, all the way down.

## Exercises

These are graduated: warm-up, application, synthesis, challenge. Work through them in order; each prepares the next.

**Warm-up.**

1. A DNA strand has the sequence 5'-AATGCCGTA-3'. Write the complementary strand with its 5' and 3' ends labeled in the correct antiparallel orientation. Then count: how many hydrogen bonds in total hold this nine-base-pair duplex together? Show your work.

2. *E. coli* DNA polymerase III has an intrinsic error rate of ~1 per 10⁵ bases before proofreading. Proofreading reduces this to ~1 per 10⁷. Mismatch repair brings it to ~1 per 10⁹. The *E. coli* genome is approximately 4.6 × 10⁶ base pairs. How many uncorrected mutations would you expect per replication round at each stage of error correction? At which stage does the expected number drop below 1 per generation?

3. Meselson and Stahl observed a single band at intermediate density after one generation of growth in ¹⁴N medium following ¹⁵N pre-labeling. The dispersive model also predicts a single band at intermediate density at this same time point. What additional time point and observation distinguishes semiconservative from dispersive replication? Explain.

**Application.**

4. *Given a specific damage type, predict which repair pathway should handle it and what disease results if that pathway fails.* For each of the following lesions, name the appropriate repair pathway and the named human disease that results when that pathway is genetically defective:
    - (a) A cytosine on one strand spontaneously deaminates to uracil.
    - (b) A pair of adjacent thymines on the same strand are crosslinked by UV light into a cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer.
    - (c) During replication, DNA polymerase inserts a G across from an A; the mispair escapes the polymerase's proofreading exonuclease.
    - (d) Ionizing radiation produces a double-strand break in a cell in G1 phase (no sister chromatid available).
    - (e) The same double-strand break, but in a cell in S phase (sister chromatid available).

5. *Compute mutation accumulation for a hypothetical Lynch syndrome carrier versus a wild-type individual over 50 years and explain why colorectal cancer is the most common manifestation.* Assume a wild-type colon epithelial cell has a mutation rate of ~10⁻⁹ per base per division, and a colon epithelial cell that has lost both copies of *MLH1* (the second copy by somatic mutation in a Lynch carrier) has a mutation rate of ~10⁻⁷ per base per division — roughly 100-fold higher. Assume the colon epithelium completely replaces itself every 5 days. Over 50 years, estimate the lifetime mutation burden per colon epithelial cell for (a) a wild-type individual and (b) a Lynch-syndrome carrier whose colon cell has lost the second MMR allele. Then explain in one paragraph why colorectal cancer, rather than (say) muscle cancer, is the most common malignancy in Lynch syndrome — connect the answer to the turnover rate of different tissues.

6. *Explain at the molecular level why olaparib (PARP inhibitor) selectively kills BRCA-deficient tumor cells.* In your answer: (a) name the repair pathway that PARP1 participates in; (b) name the repair pathway that BRCA1/2 participates in; (c) explain why blocking PARP1 alone is not lethal in a normal cell; (d) explain why losing BRCA1/2 alone is not immediately lethal in a normal cell; (e) explain why losing both pathways simultaneously is lethal; (f) explain why a BRCA-heterozygous patient's normal cells are spared by olaparib while the tumor cells (which have lost the second BRCA allele) are killed.

**Synthesis.**

7. The chapter argues that the 10⁻⁹ error rate is achieved by three layered systems, each catching ~100-fold more errors than the previous would have caught. Suppose evolution had produced only two layers (polymerase fidelity plus proofreading, no MMR). The error rate would be ~10⁻⁷ per base per replication. Estimate the new average number of mutations per cell division in a 3 × 10⁹-base-pair human genome. Estimate the new lifetime mutational burden after ~10 cell divisions in a colon epithelial cell. Discuss what consequences this would have for organismal viability and for cancer incidence. Now consider the opposite: a hypothetical fourth layer that brings the error rate to 10⁻¹¹. What would the evolutionary cost of this extra layer be? (Hint: think about how natural selection acts on populations and what variation is needed.)

8. The Meselson-Stahl experiment is sometimes called "the most beautiful experiment in biology." Identify three properties of the experiment that justify the description, drawing on the chapter's discussion. Then propose a hypothetical follow-up experiment that *could not* be settled by the same density-gradient method — that is, find a question about DNA replication for which Meselson-Stahl's specific approach would be silent.

**Challenge.**

9. A patient presents with a personal history of three early-onset cancers — colon at 38, endometrial at 43, urinary tract at 48 — and a family history showing similar early-onset cancers in two siblings and one parent. Tumor sequencing reveals microsatellite instability (MSI-high) in all three of her tumors. Germline sequencing reveals one functional and one non-functional copy of *MSH2*. (a) Name the syndrome. (b) Explain the **two-hit hypothesis** ([Knudson 1971 *PNAS* 68:820–823](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.68.4.820) [verify]) as it applies here: what is the germline hit, what is the somatic hit, and why do these two hits together produce tumors but the germline hit alone does not? (c) Explain why MSI-high is the diagnostic biomarker — connect it mechanistically to MMR deficiency and microsatellite slippage. (d) Why does the same germline mutation produce tumors mainly in epithelial tissues with high turnover (colon, endometrium, urinary tract) rather than in tissues with low turnover?

10. Many DNA-damaging chemotherapy drugs — cisplatin, doxorubicin, etoposide, bleomycin — work by causing types of damage that the cell would normally repair. Why are they nevertheless effective as cancer treatments? Pick one of the drugs and trace, in mechanistic terms, the path from drug binding to DNA to tumor-cell death. Discuss why tumor cells are often (though not always) more sensitive than normal cells to these drugs — connect the answer to what we said about repair-pathway loss in cancer cells.

11. Design a thought experiment. You are given a hypothetical organism whose replicative DNA polymerase has *no* proofreading exonuclease activity but whose mismatch repair system is unusually efficient. What error rate would you predict per replication, given that polymerase fidelity alone is ~10⁻⁵ and MMR adds ~100-fold? What additional vulnerabilities might this organism have, compared to one with a normal polymerase plus normal MMR? (Hint: think about which lesions MMR can and cannot recognize.) Propose one experimental observation that would distinguish the two organisms.

## What the chapter built — synthesis

Step back and look at what we have.

Mendel had factors that segregated. He did not know what they were. By 1944 we knew they were DNA. By 1953 we knew DNA was a double helix in which two complementary strands ran antiparallel, with A pairing with T and G pairing with C through specific hydrogen-bond geometries. By 1958 we knew the helix replicates semiconservatively — each daughter duplex carries one parental strand and one new strand, with the new strand synthesized by reading the parent as a template. By 1968 we had Okazaki fragments and the awkwardness of lagging-strand synthesis. By the 2000s we had three layers of accuracy at replication (polymerase geometry, proofreading exonuclease, mismatch repair) and three broad pathways for non-replication damage (base excision repair, nucleotide excision repair, double-strand break repair, the last subdivided into homologous recombination and non-homologous end joining). The mutation rate is ~10⁻⁹ per base per replication, the product of all these layers.

The clinical sequel is the chapter's other half. Lose MMR (germline mutation in MLH1, MSH2, or related genes) and you get Lynch syndrome, with massively elevated risk of colorectal, endometrial, and related cancers. Lose NER (mutation in any of the XP genes) and you get xeroderma pigmentosum, with a thousand-to-two-thousand-fold elevated lifetime risk of skin cancer from ordinary UV exposure. Lose HR (germline mutation in BRCA1 or BRCA2) and you get hereditary breast-and-ovarian cancer syndrome, with lifetime breast cancer risks in the 60–72% range and ovarian risks in the 40–44% range. Each syndrome is a specific repair pathway broken and a specific tissue context where the breakage matters most.

The conceptual move underneath all of this is the one I want you to carry away. **Mutation rate is not a property of DNA. It is a property of DNA plus its defenders.** A genome that copies itself once with ~10⁻⁹ accuracy is a genome surrounded by helicases, polymerases with exonuclease proofreading, mismatch-repair scanners, base-excision glycosylases, nucleotide-excision scaffolds, homologous-recombination invaders, and end-joining ligators — all running constantly, all costing energy, all evolved over more than three billion years to keep one specific information polymer stable enough to be inherited. Lose one defender and the stability drops by a factor of 100. Lose two defenders synergistically (BRCA + PARP) and the cell dies. The defenders are not optional. They are the genome.

This is also where Mendel's framework finally meets molecular biology. The "factors" Mendel inferred from pea phenotypes are stretches of DNA whose sequence specifies (Chapter 5) the proteins that build the organism. The "mutations" that produce the alleles Mendel saw are sequence changes that survived all three layers of replication fidelity and all four repair pathways. The dominance relationships Chapter 02 worked out and the penetrance discussion of Chapter 03 are downstream of *what specific sequence change happened where, and how the cell handled it.* The framework of transmission genetics — alleles, gametes, ratios — is the macroscopic shadow of the molecular machinery we have just walked through. The same logic, two levels of resolution.

## Bridge to Chapter 5

DNA stores information. We have now seen the storage molecule (the double helix), the copying mechanism (semiconservative replication via the fork machinery), and the maintenance system (five repair pathways with overlapping coverage). What we have *not* seen yet is how the information actually gets used — how the sequence of bases in DNA becomes the sequence of amino acids in a protein, which is the molecule that does the real work in the cell.

Chapter 5 is the central dogma in mechanistic detail. **Transcription**: how RNA polymerase reads the DNA template and produces a complementary RNA strand. **RNA processing**: how the eukaryotic pre-mRNA gets a 5' cap, a poly-A tail, and its introns spliced out by the spliceosome. **The genetic code**: the triplet mapping from codons (sequences of three nucleotides) to amino acids, including the start codon AUG, the three stop codons, and the redundancy that lets most amino acids be specified by multiple codons. **Translation**: how the ribosome decodes the mRNA codon by codon, with tRNAs delivering the right amino acid for each codon, and how peptide bonds form to grow the protein chain. The whole flow from a gene's sequence to a folded functional protein is one continuous chain of physical and chemical events. Chapter 4 was the storage and maintenance of the message. Chapter 5 is reading the message out loud.

**What would change my mind.** If a careful re-analysis of human germline mutation rates by next-generation sequencing showed the per-base per-replication mutation rate to be substantially different from ~10⁻⁹ — say, an order of magnitude higher or lower — the three-layer-accuracy story would need quantitative revision. The model holds because each layer's ~100-fold reduction is empirically supported (Kunkel and Erie 2015 for MMR; Kunkel 2004 for polymerase fidelity and proofreading; pedigree-based estimates of germline mutation rate from large trio studies, e.g., [Jónsson et al. 2017 *Nature* 549:519–522](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature24018) [verify]). If a new layer of error correction is discovered (some have been proposed and remain unsettled, e.g., translesion-synthesis polymerases whose role is more nuanced than simple bypass), the layering arithmetic above will need to add a fourth term.

**Still puzzling.** Why BRCA mutations preferentially cause breast and ovarian cancer, rather than producing cancers more uniformly across tissues, is something I do not yet fully understand. HR operates in every dividing cell, so an HR defect should in principle be a generic risk factor. The tissue specificity must reflect something about which other repair pathways are available in different tissues, or about how often double-strand breaks occur as a function of tissue type and hormonal context, but I have not seen a single mechanistic explanation that fully accounts for the observed tissue distribution. This is an active research area and the right answer when a student asks is: it is a known unresolved problem.
